Types of Waves

Transverse Waves

Particles vibrate perpendicular to the direction of wave travel.

〜〜〜〜〜

Examples: Light, water waves, radio waves, microwaves, all EM waves

Longitudinal Waves

Particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave travel. Have compressions and rarefactions.

||| ||| |||

Examples: Sound waves, seismic P-waves

The Wave Equation

v = fλ

Wave speed = frequency × wavelength

📝 Worked Examples

1. A wave has frequency 50Hz and wavelength 4m. Find its speed.

v = fλ = 50 × 4 = 200 m/s

2. Sound travels at 340m/s with frequency 680Hz. Find wavelength.

λ = v/f = 340/680 = 0.5 m

3. Light speed = 3×10⁸ m/s, λ = 6×10⁻⁷ m. Find frequency.

f = v/λ = 3×10⁸/6×10⁻⁷ = 5×10¹⁴ Hz

Speed of light in vacuum = 3 × 10⁸ m/s. Speed of sound in air ≈ 340 m/s at 20°C. Sound travels faster in solids than liquids than gases.

Reflection & Refraction of Waves

Reflection

  • Angle of incidence = angle of reflection
  • Wave bounces off a surface
  • Speed and wavelength unchanged
  • Frequency unchanged
  • Examples: Echo (sound), mirror (light)

Refraction

  • Wave changes speed when entering a new medium
  • Frequency stays the same
  • Wavelength changes
  • Wave bends toward/away from normal
  • Snell's law: n₁sin θ₁ = n₂sin θ₂

Refraction of Water Waves

In shallow water, waves slow down and wavelength decreases. Frequency remains constant. Waves bend toward the normal when slowing down.

Diffraction & Interference

Diffraction

The spreading of waves around obstacles or through gaps. Most noticeable when gap size ≈ wavelength.

  • Sound diffracts easily (long wavelength)
  • Light diffracts less (very short wavelength)
  • Radio waves diffract around hills

Interference

When two waves meet, they superpose (add up).

Constructive
Crests meet crests
→ Larger amplitude
Destructive
Crest meets trough
→ Cancellation
Principle of Superposition: When two waves overlap, the resultant displacement equals the sum of individual displacements.

Sound Waves

Properties of Sound

  • Longitudinal mechanical wave — needs medium to travel
  • Cannot travel through vacuum
  • Speed in air ≈ 340 m/s; faster in solids and liquids
  • Audible range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz
  • Ultrasound: above 20,000 Hz
  • Infrasound: below 20 Hz

Pitch

Related to frequency. High frequency = high pitch. Low frequency = low pitch.

Loudness

Related to amplitude. Large amplitude = loud sound. Small amplitude = quiet sound.

Echo

Reflection of sound. Echo heard if reflected sound arrives ≥ 0.1s after original. Minimum distance = 17m from reflecting surface.

The Doppler Effect & Electromagnetic Spectrum

Doppler Effect

The apparent change in frequency of a wave when the source or observer is moving.

  • Source approaching → higher frequency (higher pitch)
  • Source moving away → lower frequency (lower pitch)
  • Example: Ambulance siren changing pitch as it passes
  • Used in radar speed guns, medical ultrasound

4. Explain the difference between constructive and destructive interference.

Model Answer: Constructive interference occurs when two waves meet in phase (crest meets crest), resulting in a wave with larger amplitude. Destructive interference occurs when waves meet out of phase (crest meets trough), resulting in cancellation or reduced amplitude.
