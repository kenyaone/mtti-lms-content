🍶 Alcohols (Alkanols)

Structure and Naming

Alcohols contain the hydroxyl group –OH. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH.

NameFormulaUse
MethanolCH₃OHSolvent, fuel (toxic)
EthanolC₂H₅OHBeverages, antiseptic
Propan-1-olC₃H₇OHSolvent, perfumes

Preparation of Ethanol

  • Fermentation: Glucose + yeast (zymase) → Ethanol + CO₂ (25–37°C, anaerobic)
  • Hydration of ethene: CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH (300°C, H₃PO₄, high pressure)

Chemical Reactions of Ethanol

  • Combustion: C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O (blue flame)
  • Mild oxidation: Ethanol → Ethanal (with K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄, warm)
  • Strong oxidation: Ethanol → Ethanoic acid (excess oxidant)
  • Dehydration: C₂H₅OH → CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O (conc. H₂SO₄, 180°C)
  • Esterification: Ethanol + acid → ester + water
🧪 Aldehydes and Ketones

Aldehydes (Alkanals)

Contain the –CHO group at the end of the carbon chain.

  • Methanal (HCHO) — preservative (formalin); disinfectant
  • Ethanal (CH₃CHO) — made by oxidation of ethanol

Ketones (Alkanones)

Contain the C=O group within the carbon chain (not at the end).

  • Propanone (CH₃COCH₃) — solvent in nail polish remover
  • Butanone (CH₃COC₂H₅) — solvent in industry

Chemical Tests to Distinguish Aldehydes from Ketones

Test / ReagentAldehydeKetone
Fehling's solution (warm)Brick-red precipitate ✅No change ❌
Tollens' reagent (warm)Silver mirror ✅No change ❌
Acidified KMnO₄DecolourisesNo reaction
Key Principle: Aldehydes are reducing agents — they reduce Fehling's and Tollens' reagent. Ketones are NOT reducing agents.
🍋 Carboxylic Acids (Alkanoic Acids)

Structure

Contain the carboxyl group –COOH. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH.

NameFormulaSource / Use
Methanoic acidHCOOHAnt stings, leather tanning
Ethanoic acidCH₃COOHVinegar (~5%), solvent
Propanoic acidC₂H₅COOHFood preservative
Butanoic acidC₃H₇COOHRancid butter smell

Preparation of Ethanoic Acid

C₂H₅OH → CH₃CHO → CH₃COOH   (stepwise oxidation: K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄)

Chemical Properties

  • Weak acids — partially ionise in water
  • With metals: 2CH₃COOH + Mg → (CH₃COO)₂Mg + H₂ ↑
  • With carbonates: 2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂ ↑
  • With alkalis: CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O (sodium ethanoate)
🧴 Esters and Esterification

The Esterification Reaction

A reversible reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol, catalysed by concentrated H₂SO₄.

Carboxylic acid + Alcohol ⇌ Ester + Water  (conc. H₂SO₄, warm)
Example 1: CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O
Ethanoic acid + Ethanol → Ethyl ethanoate + Water
Example 2: HCOOH + CH₃OH ⇌ HCOOCH₃ + H₂O
Methanoic acid + Methanol → Methyl methanoate + Water

Naming Rule for Esters

Alkyl (from alcohol) + Alkanoate (from acid).

AcidAlcoholEster Name
Ethanoic acidPropan-1-olPropyl ethanoate
Methanoic acidEthanolEthyl methanoate
Propanoic acidMethanolMethyl propanoate

Uses of Esters

  • Artificial flavourings and perfumes (sweet, fruity odours)
  • Solvents in paints, varnishes, nail polish
  • Plasticisers in PVC manufacturing
  • Fats and oils in food are naturally occurring esters (triglycerides)
🔗 Polymers

Addition Polymerisation

Unsaturated monomers (containing C=C) join together with no by-products. The double bond opens to link monomers.

MonomerPolymerUses
Ethene (CH₂=CH₂)Polyethylene / PolytheneBags, bottles, pipes
Chloroethene (CH₂=CHCl)PVCPipes, wire insulation, floors
Styrene (C₆H₅CH=CH₂)PolystyrenePackaging, foam cups
Tetrafluoroethene (CF₂=CF₂)PTFE / TeflonNon-stick cookware

Condensation Polymerisation

Monomers join with elimination of a small molecule (H₂O or HCl).

  • Nylon 6,6: Hexane-1,6-diamine + Hexanedioic acid → Polyamide + H₂O
    Uses: ropes, fabric, toothbrush bristles, parachutes
  • Terylene (Dacron): Diol + Dicarboxylic acid → Polyester + H₂O
    Uses: clothing fibres, plastic bottles (PET)
⚠️ Most synthetic polymers are non-biodegradable — they persist in the environment for hundreds of years. Solutions: recycling, using biodegradable plastics, reducing single-use plastics.
🌿 Fats, Oils and Soaps

Fats and Oils — Triglycerides

Esters of glycerol (propane-1,2,3-triol) with three fatty acid molecules.

TypeBondsState at R.T.Example
Saturated fatC–C onlySolidButter, lard, palm oil
Unsaturated oilContains C=CLiquidSunflower, olive oil
Hydrogenation: Adding H₂ across C=C double bonds (Ni catalyst, ~180°C) converts liquid oils into semi-solid fats. Used in margarine production.

Soap Making — Saponification

Triglyceride + 3NaOH (boiling) → 3 RCOONa (soap) + Glycerol

Sodium salts of fatty acids → hard soap. Potassium salts (using KOH) → soft/liquid soap.

How Soap Cleans — Micelle Formation

  • Soap molecule: hydrophilic head (–COO⁻, water-loving) + hydrophobic tail (hydrocarbon chain, oil-loving)
  • Tails embed into grease; heads point into water → spherical micelle surrounds grease droplet → rinsed away
⚠️ Soap forms insoluble scum with Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ in hard water. Soapless (synthetic) detergents work in hard water as they don't form scum.
