Occurrence of Sulphur

Sulphur occurs naturally as a free element underground in Texas and Louisiana (USA) and Sicily (Italy). Also as sulphide and sulphate ores:

  • Metallic sulphides: Iron pyrites (FeS₂), Zinc blende (ZnS), Copper pyrites (CuFeS₂)
  • Metallic sulphates: Gypsum (CaSO₄)
  • Hydrogen sulphide (H₂S) in natural gas

Frasch Process & Properties of Sulphur

Extraction: The Frasch Process

Three concentric pipes (Frasch pump) drill to underground deposits:

PipeFunction
OutermostPumps superheated water (170°C, 10 atm) down to melt sulphur (MP = 114°C)
InnermostForces hot compressed air down to create froth
MiddleBrings froth of molten sulphur up to surface

Froth settles in tanks: upper layer = molten sulphur (density), lower = water. Solidifies to give 99% pure sulphur.

Why not conventional mining? Deposits lie deep under quicksand; associated poisonous SO₂ gases.

Physical Properties of Sulphur

  • Yellow solid; molecule is a puckered ring of 8 sulphur atoms (S₈)
  • Insoluble in water; soluble in organic solvents (CS₂, xylene)
  • Poor conductor of heat and electricity (covalent structure)

Effect of heat:

  • ~113°C: melts to amber mobile liquid (S₈ rings open to chains)
  • ~160°C: dark, viscous liquid (very long chains: S₁₀₀₀₀₀)
  • ~195°C: maximum viscosity
  • ~444°C (BP): red-brown vapour (S₈, S₆, S₄, S₂)
  • Vapour on cold surface → yellow flowers of sulphur

Chemical Properties of Sulphur

Burning in air:

S(s) + O₂(g) → SO₂(g) [blue flame]

With conc. H₂SO₄:

S(s) + 2H₂SO₄(l) → 3SO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

With conc. HNO₃:

S(s) + 6HNO₃(l) → H₂SO₄(aq) + 6NO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

With metals:

Fe(s) + S(s) → FeS(s) [heat; grey+yellow → black]
H₂(g) + S(s) → H₂S(g)

Note: Sulphur does NOT react with inert gases, nitrogen, or iodine.

Allotropes of Sulphur

Allotropy = existence of an element in more than one form without change of state. Sulphur has two crystalline allotropes:

PropertyRhombic Sulphur (α)Monoclinic Sulphur (β)
StabilityStable below 96°CStable above 96°C
ColourBright yellowPale yellow
Melting point113°C119°C
Density~2.06 g/cm³ (heavier)~1.98 g/cm³ (lighter)
ShapeOctahedral crystalsNeedle-like (prismatic)
OccurrenceOccurs naturallyOnly above 96°C
💡 96°C is the transition temperature — both allotropes are stable at this point. Both have the same molecular formula S₈.

Uses of Sulphur

  • Industrial manufacture of H₂SO₄ (Contact process)
  • Fungicide for skin diseases
  • Vulcanisation (hardening) of rubber
  • Manufacture of Ca(HSO₃)₂ (bleaching agent)
  • Matches and fireworks
  • Dyes, drugs (sulphur ointments), hair oils
  • Crop fungicides (e.g., ridomil, dithane)

Sulphur(IV) Oxide – SO₂

Laboratory Preparation

Dilute HCl (or H₂SO₄) + sodium sulphite:

Na₂SO₃(aq) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + SO₂(g)

Gas dried with conc. H₂SO₄; collected by downward delivery (denser than air).

Note: HNO₃ cannot be used — it oxidises SO₂ to H₂SO₄.

Properties of SO₂

  • Colourless gas; pungent, irritating smell
  • Does not burn and does not support combustion
  • Acidic (low pH); dissolves in water: SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃ (sulphurous acid)

SO₂ as reducing agent: Reduces KMnO₄ (purple → colourless) and K₂Cr₂O₇ (orange → green)

5SO₂(g) + 2KMnO₄(aq) + 2H₂O → K₂SO₄ + 2MnSO₄ + H₂SO₄
K₂Cr₂O₇(aq) + 3SO₂(g) + H₂SO₄(aq) → K₂SO₄ + Cr₂(SO₄)₃ + H₂O

Orange → green is the usual chemical test for SO₂.

SO₂ as oxidising agent: Reacts with H₂S (stronger reducing agent):

2H₂S(g) + SO₂(g) → 2H₂O(l) + 3S(s) [yellow deposits]

SO₂ as bleaching agent: Bleaches by reduction (not permanent — colour can be restored by oxidation or air exposure):

SO₂(g) + H₂O(l) + [Dye+O] → Dye + H₂SO₄(aq) [colourless]

SO₂ with alkali (NaOH):

2NaOH(aq) + SO₂(g) → Na₂SO₃(aq) + H₂O(l) [limited SO₂]
NaOH(aq) + SO₂(g) → NaHSO₃(aq) [excess SO₂]

Tests for SO₂

  • Characteristic pungent smell
  • Bleaches moist flower petals
  • Decolourises purple KMnO₄
  • Turns acidified orange K₂Cr₂O₇ filter paper to green (most reliable test)

The Contact Process – Industrial Manufacture of H₂SO₄

Raw Materials

  • Sulphur or sulphide ores (cheaper from pyrites)
  • Oxygen (from air)
  • Water
  • Concentrated H₂SO₄ (for absorption)

Process Steps

StepProcessEquation
1Burn sulphur/pyrites to get SO₂S + O₂ → SO₂
2Purification + drying (electrostatic precipitation; conc. H₂SO₄)—
3Heat exchanger (cool incoming gases)—
4Catalytic chamber: SO₂ + O₂ → SO₃2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ + Heat [V₂O₅, 450°C]
5Heat exchanger (cool hot SO₃)—
6Absorption: SO₃ + conc. H₂SO₄ → OleumSO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇
7Dilution: Oleum + H₂O → conc. H₂SO₄H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄
⚠️ SO₃ is NOT dissolved directly in water — it produces corrosive acid mist with a hissing sound. It's dissolved in conc. H₂SO₄ to form oleum (pyrosulphuric acid) first.

Conditions and Reasons for Catalytic Chamber

ConditionValueReason
Temperature450°C (compromise)Low T → higher yield (exothermic) but slow rate; high T → fast but low yield. 450°C balances both
PressureSlightly above atmospheric (~2-3 atm)High pressure would liquefy SO₃ and is expensive; slight above atm gives 98% conversion
CatalystVanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅)Speeds up reaction; preferred over platinised asbestos — cheaper, not easily poisoned

Pollution control: Unreacted SO₂ neutralised with Ca(OH)₂:

Ca(OH)₂(aq) + SO₂(g) → CaSO₃(aq) + H₂O(l)

Properties of Sulphuric(VI) Acid

Physical Properties (Conc. H₂SO₄)

  • Colourless, odourless, oily liquid; density 1.84 g/cm³
  • Very soluble in water — gives out heat (exothermic)
  • Hygroscopic — absorbs atmospheric moisture (used as drying agent)
⚠️ Always add acid to water — never water to acid (violent exothermic reaction).

Conc. H₂SO₄ as Dehydrating Agent

Blue CuSO₄·5H₂O → White anhydrous CuSO₄:

CuSO₄·5H₂O(s) → CuSO₄(s) + 5H₂O(l) [conc. H₂SO₄]

Sugar (charring):

C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁(s) → 12C(s) + 11H₂O(l) [conc. H₂SO₄; spongy black mass]

Dehydration of ethanol → ethene:

C₂H₅OH(l) → C₂H₄(g) + H₂O(l) [conc. H₂SO₄, 160–180°C]

Conc. H₂SO₄ as Oxidising Agent (hot)

Reduced to SO₂ and water:

Cu(s) + 2H₂SO₄(l) → CuSO₄(aq) + SO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)
C(s) + 2H₂SO₄(l) → CO₂(g) + 2SO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

Dilute H₂SO₄ – Typical Acid Reactions

With metals above H in reactivity:

Mg(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → MgSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)

With carbonates:

Na₂CO₃(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → Na₂SO₄(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)

With oxides/hydroxides:

CuO(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → CuSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l) [black → blue solution]

Note: Dilute H₂SO₄ does not react with copper (below H in reactivity series).

Uses of H₂SO₄

Manufacture of: fertilisers, detergents, plastics, dyes, paints, lead accumulators, polymers, petroleum refining. Also used as a drying agent.

Hydrogen Sulphide – H₂S

Laboratory Preparation

Dilute HCl + iron(II) sulphide:

FeS(s) + 2HCl(aq) → FeCl₂(aq) + H₂S(g)

Dried with anhydrous CaCl₂. Collected by downward delivery (denser than air) or over warm water.

Test for H₂S: Filter paper soaked in lead(II) ethanoate turns black (PbS).

Properties of H₂S

  • Colourless, very poisonous gas; repulsive "rotten eggs" smell
  • Slightly soluble in water → weak acid (H₂S ⇌ H⁺ + HS⁻)

Combustion:

2H₂S(g) + 3O₂(g) → 2SO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g) [excess O₂]
2H₂S(g) + O₂(g) → 2S(s) + 2H₂O(g) [limited O₂; yellow S deposits]

H₂S as Reducing Agent

H₂S is oxidised to S; the oxidising agent is reduced:

ReactionObservation
K₂Cr₂O₇(acidified) + H₂SOrange → green; yellow S deposits
KMnO₄ + H₂SPurple → colourless; yellow S deposits
FeCl₃ + H₂SBrown → pale green (Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺)
Br₂(aq) + H₂SRed-brown → colourless; yellow S deposits

With conc. HNO₃:

2HNO₃(aq) + H₂S(g) → 2H₂O + 2NO₂(g) + S(s)

Metal sulphides (mostly black):

Pb²⁺(aq) + S²⁻(aq) → PbS(s) [black]
Cu²⁺(aq) + S²⁻(aq) → CuS(s) [black]

Tests for Sulphate/Sulphite Ions & Pollution

Test for Sulphates (SO₄²⁻)

Add Ba²⁺ (from BaCl₂ or Ba(NO₃)₂), then acidify with dil HCl or HNO₃:

→ White precipitate insoluble in dilute acid = SO₄²⁻ confirmed (BaSO₄)

Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s) [white ppt; insoluble in acid]

Note: BaSO₃ and BaCO₃ also form white ppts, but dissolve in dilute acid (so BaSO₄ is the only one that persists).

Test for Sulphites (SO₃²⁻)

Add Ba²⁺ → white precipitate (BaSO₃). Then add dilute HCl:

→ White precipitate dissolves + colourless gas that turns orange K₂Cr₂O₇ paper to green = SO₂ confirmed

BaSO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → BaCl₂(aq) + SO₂(g) + H₂O(l)

Environmental Pollution by Sulphur Compounds

Sulphur(IV) oxide (SO₂):

  • Emitted when sulphur-containing fuels burn; during metal extraction; in H₂SO₄ manufacture
  • SO₂ → SO₃ → H₂SO₄ (acid rain/fog)

Effects of acid rain:

  • Leaching of minerals from soil
  • Erosion of stone buildings
  • Corrosion of metallic structures
  • Respiratory irritation
  • Death of plants (defoliation)
  • Destruction of aquatic life
  • Stunted plant growth (chlorosis)

H₂S: Very poisonous; even small concentrations are dangerous.

Q5. Explain with equations and observations what happens when concentrated sulphuric acid is added to copper turnings and heated.
No reaction with cold dilute H₂SO₄ (copper is below hydrogen in the reactivity series). However, hot concentrated H₂SO₄ acts as an oxidising agent. Brown/colourless fumes of sulphur(IV) oxide (SO₂) are given off and the blue copper(II) sulphate solution forms: Equation: Cu(s) + 2H₂SO₄(l) → CuSO₄(aq) + SO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) Observation: Evolution of SO₂ (pungent smell), blue solution of CuSO₄ forms.
Q6. Describe the steps and equations for the Contact Process, explaining why SO₃ is absorbed in concentrated H₂SO₄ rather than water.
The Contact process: (1) Burn sulphur or pyrites to produce SO₂ (S + O₂ → SO₂). (2) Purify and dry SO₂ (remove dust with electrostatic precipitation; dry with conc. H₂SO₄). (3) Pass through heat exchanger. (4) React SO₂ with O₂ over V₂O₅ catalyst at 450°C to form SO₃: 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃. (5) Cool in heat exchanger. (6) Absorb SO₃ in concentrated H₂SO₄ — NOT water, because dissolving SO₃ directly in water produces corrosive acid mist/spray which is hazardous: SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇ (oleum). (7) Dilute oleum: H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄.
Q7. How would you test a solution to determine whether it contains SO₃²⁻ or SO₄²⁻ ions? State observations for each.
Add barium chloride solution — both SO₃²⁻ and SO₄²⁻ form white precipitates (BaSO₃ and BaSO₄ respectively). Then acidify with dilute HCl. If the precipitate dissolves and a gas is produced that turns acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ paper from orange to green, the ion is SO₃²⁻ (produces SO₂ gas). If the precipitate remains insoluble in dilute acid, the ion is SO₄²⁻ (BaSO₄ is insoluble in acids).
