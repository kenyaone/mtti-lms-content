🌿 Support in Plants
🌿 Support: To carry part of the weight/mass of an organism. Locomotion: Progressive change in the position of an organism.

Importance of Support in Plants

  • Exposes leaf surface to sunlight for photosynthesis
  • Ensures flowers are exposed to pollination agents
  • Exposes fruits and seeds to agents of dispersal
  • Resists breakage due to own weight and other organisms
  • Allows proper transport and translocation of materials

Tissues Providing Mechanical Support in Plants

TissuePropertiesRole
CollenchymaNot lignified; flexibleSupport in young/herbaceous stems
SclerenchymaLignified; rigidMechanical support in stems and roots
Xylem (vessels/tracheids)Lignified; hollowSupport + water transport

How Support is Achieved in Plants

  • Turgidity — cells absorb water, become turgid → firm → holds herbaceous plants upright
  • Collenchyma and sclerenchyma — closely packed cells in stem and roots
  • Lignified xylem — provides rigid support to stems, roots and leaves
  • Inelastic cuticle — waxy layer on epidermis helps maintain shape
  • Tendrils — climbing plants obtain support from other plants/objects
🌱 Young herbaceous plants stay upright through turgidity and presence of collenchyma. In older woody plants, lignified xylem provides the main support.
🦴 Types of Skeletons

Exoskeleton

Rigid framework found on the OUTSIDE/surface of an animal's body.

Examples: Arthropods — crabs, insects

Advantages:

  • Supports/protects delicate inner parts
  • Waterproof (prevents drying out)
  • Surface for muscle attachment

Endoskeleton

Rigid framework covered by body tissues; found INSIDE the animal's body.

Examples: Chordates — cat, fish, humans

Importance:

  • Supports body; gives shape
  • Protects delicate organs (skull→brain, ribs→heart/lungs)
  • Enables locomotion (bones = levers)
  • Red blood cells formed in bone marrow
  • Stores minerals (calcium, phosphorus)

The Human Skeleton — Two Parts

Axial Skeleton

Forms the main axis of the body.

  • Skull
  • Vertebral column
  • Sternum (breastbone)
  • Ribs

Appendicular Skeleton

Limbs and girdles attached to axial skeleton.

  • Pectoral girdle (shoulder) + forelimbs
  • Pelvic girdle (hip) + hindlimbs
  • Bones: humerus, ulna, radius, femur, tibia, fibula, carpals, tarsals, phalanges

Fish Adaptation for Locomotion in Water

  • Streamlined body → reduces water resistance/friction
  • Flexible vertebral column → allows sideways movement
  • Myotomes (muscles) → produce swimming movements
  • Paired fins (pectoral, pelvic) → balance, pitch control
  • Unpaired fins (dorsal, anal, caudal) → steering, propulsion
  • Swim bladder → buoyancy (controls depth)
  • Scales → smooth surface; mucus reduces friction
🔩 The Vertebral Column

Functions of the Vertebral Column

  • Gives flexibility and supports body weight
  • Protects the spinal cord
  • Absorbs shock (via intervertebral discs)
  • Provides surface for muscle attachment

General Features of a Vertebra

FeatureFunction
Centrum (solid body)Supports weight of body
Neural canalPassage and protection for spinal cord
Neural archEncloses neural canal
Neural spineMuscle attachment
Transverse processLateral; muscle attachment
FacetsArticulation with adjacent vertebrae

Bones of the Vertebral Column

Cervical (7)
Neck region. Atlas (1st) — allows nodding. Axis (2nd) — allows rotation. Others support weight of head.
Thoracic (12)
Chest region. Long backward neural spine; articulates with ribs to form rib cage; protects heart/lungs.
Lumbar (5)
Lower back. Large centrum; supports upper body weight; protects abdominal organs.
Sacral (5 fused)
Fused to form sacrum; attaches hip girdles; protects alimentary canal.
Caudal/Coccyx (4 fused)
Tail bone; vestigial structure in humans.
🔑 Intervertebral discs between vertebrae act as cushions — absorb shock, reduce friction, allow flexibility of the vertebral column.

Joints

🔩 Joint: The point where two or more bones meet to allow movement.

Ball & Socket

360° movement in all planes

e.g. shoulder, hip

Hinge Joint

180° — movement in ONE plane only

e.g. elbow, knee

Pivot Joint

Rotation only

e.g. atlas on axis (head rotation)

⚙️ Synovial Joints & Muscles

Features of Synovial (Freely Movable) Joints

  • Bone ends covered with articular cartilage — reduces friction
  • Enclosed by a capsule of ligaments — holds bones together
  • Synovial membrane — secretes synovial fluid
  • Synovial fluid — lubricates joint, absorbs shock, nourishes cartilage

Key Connective Tissues

StructureDefinitionFunction
LigamentConnective tissue joining BONE to BONEHolds bones together at joints; limits excessive movement
TendonConnective tissue joining MUSCLE to BONETransmits muscle force to bone for movement
CartilageSoft supporting tissue at jointsCushions bones; absorbs shock; reduces friction
Intervertebral discCartilage between vertebraeAbsorbs shock; reduces friction; allows flexibility

Three Types of Muscles

Skeletal (Voluntary)

  • Striated/stripped
  • Multinucleated
  • Long, cylindrical fibres
  • Under voluntary control
  • e.g. biceps, triceps

Smooth (Involuntary)

  • Unstriated
  • Uninucleated
  • Short, spindle-shaped fibres
  • Involuntary control
  • e.g. gut, blood vessels, iris

Cardiac (Heart)

  • Striated (lightly)
  • Myogenic (self-stimulating)
  • Never fatigued
  • Involuntary
  • Pumps blood continuously
💪 Muscles & Movement — Biceps & Triceps
💪 Antagonistic Muscles: Muscles that work in PAIRS — when one contracts the other relaxes. They pull in opposite directions to create movement.

Biceps & Triceps — Arm Movement

MuscleAttachmentAction when contractedRole
BicepsScapula → RadiusPulls radius → arm BENDS (flexes)Flexor
TricepsScapula + Humerus → UlnaPulls ulna → arm STRETCHES (extends)Extensor

Bending the arm (flexion)

  • Biceps contracts (shortens)
  • Pulls radius upward
  • Triceps relaxes (extends)
  • Forearm moves up

Straightening the arm (extension)

  • Triceps contracts (shortens)
  • Pulls ulna downward
  • Biceps relaxes
  • Arm straightens
🔑 Bones act as levers, muscles provide the force, and joints are the fulcrum. Tendons attach muscles to bones and transmit the pulling force.

Importance of Locomotion in Animals

  • Search for food and water
  • Search for mates and breeding sites
  • Escape from predators and harmful conditions
  • Find shelter

✏️ Practice Questions

1. State FOUR functions of the vertebral column.

1. Gives flexibility to the body
2. Supports the weight of the body
3. Protects the spinal cord
4. Absorbs shock (via intervertebral discs)
5. Provides surface for muscle attachment

2. Explain the antagonistic action of the biceps and triceps during bending and straightening of the arm.

Bending: Biceps contracts (pulls radius up) → arm bends. Triceps relaxes simultaneously.
Straightening: Triceps contracts (pulls ulna down) → arm straightens. Biceps relaxes simultaneously.
They are antagonistic — they work in opposition, always doing opposite actions.

3. Distinguish between a ligament, tendon and cartilage.

Ligament: Connective tissue joining BONE to BONE — holds joints together.
Tendon: Connective tissue joining MUSCLE to BONE — transmits muscle force for movement.
Cartilage: Soft supportive tissue found at joints — cushions bones, absorbs shock, reduces friction.

4. State THREE advantages of an exoskeleton to arthropods.

1. Protection — supports and protects delicate inner organs from damage
2. Waterproof — prevents desiccation (drying out) of body
3. Muscle attachment — provides surface for muscle attachment to enable movement

5. Explain how the Atlas and Axis vertebrae are adapted to their functions.

Atlas (1st cervical): Ring-shaped with no centrum; broad flat transverse processes; has facets for articulation with skull condyles → allows NODDING of the head (yes movement).

Axis (2nd cervical): Has an odontoid peg (dens) projecting from centrum → atlas pivots on this peg → allows ROTATION of the head (no movement). Also has large flattened neural spine for muscle attachment.
