🟡 Sulphur — Occurrence & Allotropes

Occurrence

Sulphur occurs naturally as the free element near volcanoes and hot springs (Sicily, USA). It also occurs in combined form as sulphate minerals (gypsum CaSO₄·2H₂O, barytes BaSO₄) and sulphide ores (pyrite FeS₂, galena PbS). It is present in proteins (amino acids cysteine and methionine).

Allotropes of Sulphur

Allotropes are different physical forms of the same element in the same state. Sulphur has two main solid allotropes.

Rhombic Sulphur (α-S)

  • Stable below 96°C (transition temperature)
  • Yellow, octahedral crystals
  • Density: 2.07 g/cm³
  • Melting point: 113°C
  • Most stable allotrope at room temperature

Monoclinic Sulphur (β-S)

  • Stable between 96°C and 119°C
  • Pale yellow, needle-shaped crystals
  • Density: 1.96 g/cm³
  • Melting point: 119°C
  • Converts to rhombic below 96°C

Transition Temperature

At 96°C, both allotropes are in equilibrium. Above 96°C, monoclinic is stable; below 96°C, rhombic is stable. Both consist of S₈ ring molecules — they differ only in the packing arrangement of the rings.

Extraction of Sulphur — Frasch Process

Sulphur is mined from underground deposits using the Frasch Process: three concentric pipes are sunk into the sulphur bed. Superheated water (160°C) melts the sulphur; hot compressed air forces liquid sulphur to the surface where it solidifies (99.5% pure).

💨 Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂)

Laboratory Preparation

Method 1: Copper + Hot Conc. H₂SO₄

Cu + 2H₂SO₄(conc.) → CuSO₄ + SO₂ + 2H₂O
(heat required; H₂SO₄ acts as oxidising agent)

Method 2: Sodium Sulphite + Dilute H₂SO₄

Na₂SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + SO₂ + H₂O

Method 3: Burning Sulphur

S + O₂ → SO₂

Physical Properties of SO₂

Appearance

Colourless gas with a pungent, choking smell

Density

Denser than air. Collected by upward delivery.

Solubility

Very soluble in water (→ acidic solution)

Chemical Properties of SO₂

1. Reaction with Water — Acidic Oxide

SO₂(g) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₂SO₃(aq)
(sulphurous acid — weak diprotic acid; turns blue litmus red)

2. Reaction with Bases

SO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₃ + H₂O
(sodium sulphite)
SO₂ + NaOH → NaHSO₃
(sodium bisulphite — excess SO₂)

3. Reducing Agent

SO₂ + Cl₂ + 2H₂O → H₂SO₄ + 2HCl
(SO₂ oxidised from +4 to +6; Cl₂ reduced)

Also decolourises acidified KMnO₄ solution — a test for SO₂ as a reducing agent.

4. Bleaching Action

SO₂ bleaches moist coloured materials by reduction (adding hydrogen). Unlike chlorine bleaching (oxidation), colours return on heating — so SO₂ is used for bleaching delicate materials like wool, silk, straw hats, and dried fruits.

5. Oxidising Agent (limited)

SO₂ + 2H₂S → 3S + 2H₂O
(SO₂ acts as oxidising agent; precipitate of sulphur)

Test for SO₂

⚗️ Identification Tests

  • Turns acidified KMnO₄ colourless (reducing agent)
  • Turns K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄ from orange to green
  • Gives white precipitate with Ba(NO₃)₂ / BaCl₂ solution after oxidation to SO₄²⁻

Uses of SO₂

Preservative

Preserves dried fruits, wines, and juices (E220)

Bleaching

Bleaching wool, silk, straw, and paper pulp

Industrial

Making H₂SO₄ (Contact Process); making sulphites

🔬 Sulphur Trioxide (SO₃)

What is SO₃?

Sulphur trioxide is the anhydride of sulphuric acid. It is produced industrially in the Contact Process and reacts vigorously with water to form H₂SO₄.

Physical Properties

State

Colourless fuming liquid/solid at room temperature (bp 45°C)

Density

Much denser than air; white fumes in moist air

Solubility

Reacts violently with water — not simply dissolved

Chemical Properties of SO₃

1. Reaction with Water

SO₃(g) + H₂O(l) → H₂SO₄(l)
(violent exothermic reaction; fumes of H₂SO₄ produced)

This is why SO₃ is NOT absorbed directly in water industrially — it creates acid mist. Instead it is absorbed in 98% H₂SO₄ to form oleum, which is then diluted.

2. Reaction with Bases

SO₃ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + H₂O
(sodium sulphate formed)

3. Formation of Oleum (Fuming H₂SO₄)

SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇
(pyrosulphuric acid / oleum; then diluted with water to give H₂SO₄)

4. Equilibrium in Contact Process

2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g) ΔH = −197 kJ/mol
(vanadium(V) oxide catalyst, 450°C, 1–2 atm)

This is the key equilibrium step — discussed in full on the next slide.

🏭 The Contact Process

What is the Contact Process?

The Contact Process is the industrial manufacture of sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), the most important industrial chemical. Over 200 million tonnes are produced annually worldwide.

Raw Materials

Sulphur / Pyrite

S or FeS₂ burned in air to produce SO₂

Air (Oxygen)

Purified, dried; provides O₂ for oxidation steps

98% H₂SO₄

Used to absorb SO₃ to form oleum

The Three Stages

Stage 1: Production of SO₂

S(s) + O₂(g) → SO₂(g)
4FeS₂(s) + 11O₂(g) → 2Fe₂O₃(s) + 8SO₂(g)
(if using iron pyrite)

Gas is purified and dried before the next stage.

Stage 2: Oxidation of SO₂ to SO₃ (Key Equilibrium)

2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g) ΔH = −197 kJ/mol
ConditionValue UsedReason (Le Chatelier)
Temperature450°CCompromise: low T gives high yield but slow rate; 450°C balances both (~98% conversion)
Pressure1–2 atmReaction is favoured by high pressure (3→2 mol gas), but 1–2 atm gives adequate yield; higher pressure is costly
CatalystV₂O₅ (vanadium pentoxide)Increases rate; does not shift equilibrium

Stage 3: Absorption of SO₃ & Formation of H₂SO₄

SO₃(g) + H₂SO₄(l) → H₂S₂O₇(l) (oleum)
(SO₃ absorbed in 98% H₂SO₄, NOT water — avoids acid mist)
H₂S₂O₇(l) + H₂O(l) → 2H₂SO₄(l)
(oleum carefully diluted with water to give 98% H₂SO₄)

⚠️ Why Not Absorb SO₃ Directly in Water?

SO₃ + H₂O is so exothermic that a fine acid mist forms which is difficult to condense and causes air pollution. Absorbing in 98% H₂SO₄ gives oleum which is then diluted safely.

🧪 Properties of Sulphuric Acid (H₂SO₄)

Physical Properties

Appearance

Colourless, oily, viscous liquid; very dense (1.84 g/cm³)

Boiling point

337°C (high — due to hydrogen bonding between molecules)

Mixing with water

Highly exothermic — ALWAYS add acid to water, never reverse

Properties of Dilute H₂SO₄

Dilute H₂SO₄ is a strong diprotic acid: H₂SO₄ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻

ReactionEquationProduct
With metals (Mg, Zn)Mg + H₂SO₄ → MgSO₄ + H₂↑Metal sulphate + hydrogen
With metal oxidesCuO + H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + H₂OMetal sulphate + water
With carbonatesCaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂Sulphate + water + CO₂
With hydroxides2NaOH + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂OSodium sulphate + water
With BaCl₂/Ba(NO₃)₂Ba²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄↓White ppt — test for SO₄²⁻

Properties of Concentrated H₂SO₄

⚠️ Concentrated H₂SO₄ behaves differently from dilute!

1. Dehydrating Agent

Removes water (H and OH) from compounds as H₂O molecules:

C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ → 12C + 11H₂O
(sugar → black carbon + steam; spectacular reaction)
HCOOH → CO + H₂O
(formic acid dehydrated to carbon monoxide)

2. Oxidising Agent (hot, concentrated)

Cu + 2H₂SO₄(conc.) → CuSO₄ + SO₂ + 2H₂O
(Cu dissolved — not possible with dilute)
C + 2H₂SO₄(conc.) → CO₂ + 2SO₂ + 2H₂O
(non-metals oxidised)
S + 2H₂SO₄(conc.) → 3SO₂ + 2H₂O

3. Reaction with NaCl / NaBr (displacement)

NaCl + H₂SO₄(conc.) → NaHSO₄ + HCl↑
(cold; used to make HCl gas)
2NaCl + H₂SO₄(conc.) → Na₂SO₄ + 2HCl↑
(hot)

Uses of Sulphuric Acid

Fertilisers (60%)

Making (NH₄)₂SO₄, superphosphate

Chemicals

Making HCl, HNO₃, dyes, drugs, detergents

Industry

Car batteries, steel pickling, oil refining, paint pigments

🥚 Hydrogen Sulphide (H₂S)

⚠️ Safety Note

H₂S is a highly toxic gas — more poisonous than HCN. It smells of rotten eggs. All experiments must be done in a fume cupboard.

Laboratory Preparation

Iron(II) Sulphide + Dilute HCl or H₂SO₄ (Kipp's Apparatus)

FeS(s) + H₂SO₄(dilute) → FeSO₄(aq) + H₂S(g)
FeS(s) + 2HCl(aq) → FeCl₂(aq) + H₂S(g)

Physical Properties

Smell

Rotten egg odour; detectable at very low concentrations

Density

Slightly denser than air (M=34); collected by upward delivery

Solubility

Slightly soluble in water → weak diprotic acid (H₂S ⇌ 2H⁺ + S²⁻)

Chemical Properties of H₂S

1. Combustion

2H₂S + 3O₂(excess) → 2SO₂ + 2H₂O
(blue flame)
2H₂S + O₂(limited) → 2S + 2H₂O
(yellow S deposited)

2. Reducing Agent

H₂S + Cl₂ → S + 2HCl
(H₂S oxidised; yellow sulphur precipitate)
SO₂ + 2H₂S → 3S + 2H₂O
(SO₂ acts as oxidising agent here)

H₂S decolourises acidified KMnO₄ and turns K₂Cr₂O₇ green — both tests for reducing action.

3. Reaction with Metals Salts (Qualitative Analysis)

H₂S is used in qualitative analysis to precipitate metal sulphides — many of which are insoluble and characteristically coloured:

IonSulphideColour
Pb²⁺PbSBlack
Cu²⁺CuSBlack
Fe²⁺FeSBlack
Zn²⁺ZnSWhite
Cd²⁺CdSYellow
As³⁺As₂S₃Yellow

Test for H₂S

Turns lead(II) acetate paper black: Pb(CH₃COO)₂ + H₂S → PbS↓ + 2CH₃COOH. This is the definitive test for H₂S.

🌍 Environmental Impact of Sulphur Compounds

Acid Rain

How Acid Rain Forms

Normal rain is slightly acidic (pH ~5.6) due to dissolved CO₂. Acid rain (pH 4–5) forms when SO₂ and NOₓ from burning fossil fuels dissolve in water vapour and are oxidised in the atmosphere:

2SO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 2H₂SO₄
(sulphuric acid — main component of acid rain)
4NO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4HNO₃
(nitric acid contribution)
Figure: Acid rain formation from SO₂ emissions and its environmental effects

Effects of Acid Rain

On Aquatic Life

  • Lakes become acidic (pH < 5)
  • Fish and invertebrates die
  • Disrupts food chains

On Forests & Soil

  • Damages leaf surfaces and kills trees
  • Leaches nutrients from soil
  • Releases toxic Al³⁺ ions from soil

On Buildings

  • Corrodes limestone and marble: CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂
  • Corrodes iron and steel structures

On Human Health

  • Acid aerosols cause respiratory problems
  • Acidified water leaches lead/copper from pipes

Control of SO₂ Emissions

MethodHow It Works
Flue gas desulphurisation (FGD)Reacts SO₂ in exhaust with CaO or Ca(OH)₂: SO₂ + CaO → CaSO₃ (calcium sulphite)
Low-sulphur fuelsUse fuels with <0.5% S content; hydrodesulphurisation of oil
Catalytic convertersReduce NOₓ in vehicle exhaust (also reduces acid rain contribution)
Renewable energySolar, wind, hydro — no SO₂ emitted

Question 6 — Short Answer [3 marks]

Explain, with equations, how acid rain forms from sulphur dioxide released by burning fossil fuels, and describe TWO environmental effects of acid rain.

Model Answer: SO₂ is released when fossil fuels (coal, oil) containing sulphur are burned: S + O₂ → SO₂. In the atmosphere, SO₂ reacts with oxygen and water vapour: 2SO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 2H₂SO₄. The sulphuric acid dissolves in rain, giving acid rain (pH 4–5). Effects include: (1) Acidification of lakes and rivers, killing fish and aquatic organisms at pH < 5. (2) Damage to forests — acid leaches nutrients and releases toxic Al³⁺ ions, killing trees. (Also: corrosion of limestone buildings; respiratory damage in humans.)
Question 7 — Short Answer [3 marks]

Describe THREE differences in the reactions of dilute and concentrated sulphuric acid with copper metal, including equations.

Model Answer: Dilute H₂SO₄ does NOT react with copper — Cu is below hydrogen in the reactivity series so H⁺ cannot oxidise it. Concentrated H₂SO₄ DOES react with copper when heated: Cu + 2H₂SO₄(conc.) → CuSO₄ + SO₂ + 2H₂O. Difference 1: Dilute = no reaction; Conc. = reaction occurs. Difference 2: Conc. H₂SO₄ acts as oxidising agent; dilute acts only as an acid. Difference 3: Conc. produces SO₂ gas (pungent); dilute produces no gas with Cu.
Question 8 — Short Answer [2 marks]

State why SO₃ cannot be absorbed directly in water during the Contact Process, and describe what is done instead.

Model Answer: SO₃ reacts very vigorously with water in a highly exothermic reaction, producing a fine mist of sulphuric acid droplets that are very difficult to condense and would pollute the air. Instead, SO₃ is absorbed in 98% concentrated H₂SO₄ to form oleum (H₂S₂O₇): SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇. The oleum is then carefully diluted with water to produce the required concentration of H₂SO₄: H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄.
