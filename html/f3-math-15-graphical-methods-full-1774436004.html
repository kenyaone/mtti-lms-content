Solving Cubic Equations Graphically

The roots of y = x³ + ax² + bx + c are where the curve crosses the x-axis (y = 0).

Solve x³ − 3x = 0 graphically:
Plot y = x³ − 3x; roots are where y = 0
From graph: x ≈ −1.73, 0, 1.73 (i.e. −√3, 0, √3)
Solve x³ − 3x = 1 graphically:
Plot y = x³ − 3x and y = 1 on the same axes
Read x-values at intersection points
Steps for graphical solution:
  1. Prepare table of values for suitable x range
  2. Plot y = f(x) on graph paper
  3. Draw the line y = k (constant)
  4. Read x-coordinates of intersection points

Average Rate of Change

The average rate of change between two points is the gradient of the chord.

Average rate = Δy / Δx = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁)
This is the gradient of the secant line through the two points.
Example: y = x² between x = 1 and x = 3.
y₁ = 1, y₂ = 9
Average rate = (9−1)/(3−1) = 8/2 = 4

Rate of Change at an Instant

The instantaneous rate at a point is the gradient of the tangent at that point.

Method — drawing a tangent:
  1. Draw the curve accurately on graph paper
  2. At the required point, draw a tangent (touches curve at one point)
  3. Choose two points on the tangent far apart
  4. Compute gradient = Δy/Δx of the tangent
Example: y = x³ at x = 2. Draw tangent. From graph, tangent through (1, 5) and (3, 19).
Gradient = (19−5)/(3−1) = 14/2 = 7
(Actual dy/dx = 3x² = 12 — closer approximation needs more accurate tangent)

Empirical Graphs

An empirical graph is drawn from experimental data. A line of best fit summarises the trend.

Drawing a line of best fit:
  • Plot all data points as a scatter graph
  • Draw a straight line passing through the mean point (x̄, ȳ)
  • Aim to have equal numbers of points above and below the line
  • Use the line to interpolate (within range) or extrapolate (beyond range)
Reading the graph: From the line y = mx + c, read gradient m and y-intercept c to find the empirical relationship.
