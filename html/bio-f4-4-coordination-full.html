⚡ The Nervous System
🧠 Coordination: The linking together of all physiological activities in the body so they take place at the right time and place.

Two Main Coordination Systems

Nervous System

Fast, electrical signals through neurons. Short-duration response. Localised targets.

Endocrine (Hormonal) System

Slower, chemical signals through blood. Long-duration response. Widespread targets.

Components of the Mammalian Nervous System

  • CNS — Central Nervous System: brain & spinal cord
  • PNS — Peripheral Nervous System: cranial and spinal nerves
  • Sense organs — receptors detecting stimuli
  • ANS — Autonomic Nervous System: controls involuntary actions

Receptors, Conductors & Effectors

ComponentDefinitionExamples
ReceptorsStructures that detect stimuliSense organs (eye, ear, skin)
ConductorsTransmit impulses from receptors to effectorsNeurons (nerve cells)
EffectorsResponding partsMuscles, glands

Somatic vs Autonomic Nervous System

Somatic (Voluntary)

Controls conscious/voluntary actions — skin, bones, joints, skeletal muscles.

Autonomic (Involuntary)

Controls involuntary actions — digestive system, blood vessels, cardiac muscle, glands.

🔬 Neurons & Nerve Impulse Transmission
🔬 Neuron (Nerve Cell): The basic structural and functional unit of the nervous system. Conducts nerve impulses.

Parts of a Neuron & Their Functions

Cell body (Centron)
Contains nucleus and cytoplasm; metabolic centre
Axon
Transmits impulses AWAY from cell body
Dendrites
Relay impulses TOWARD cell body / across adjacent neurons
Myelin Sheath
Insulates axon; speeds up impulse transmission
Schwann Cells
Form myelin sheath; aid nutrition and regeneration of axon
Nodes of Ranvier
Gaps between Schwann cells; speed up impulse (saltatory conduction)
Nissl Granules
Contain mitochondria; provide energy for metabolic processes

Three Types of Neurons

TypeDirection of impulseLocation
Sensory neuronReceptors → CNSOutside CNS; bipolar (cell body on axon)
Motor neuronCNS → EffectorsCell body inside CNS (terminal)
Relay/InterneuronWithin CNSInside CNS; usually no myelin sheath

Impulse Transmission Across a Synapse

Impulse arrives at synaptic knob→ Vesicles release acetylcholine→ Diffuses across synaptic cleft→ Attaches to post-synaptic membrane→ Depolarisation → new impulse

Functions of a Synapse

  • Allows transmission of impulses from one neuron to the next
  • Ensures impulses travel in ONE direction only
  • In the brain, synapses store information/memory

Reflex Action

⚡ Reflex Action: An automatic, involuntary response to an external stimulus (e.g. withdrawing hand from hot object, sneezing).

Reflex Arc — Withdrawal from Hot Object

Skin receptor stimulated→ Sensory neuron→ Synapse→ Relay neuron (spinal cord)→ Motor neuron→ Effector muscle contracts→ Hand withdrawn

Conditioned Reflex

Conditioned reflex: A response caused by an associated stimulus that substitutes the normal stimulus. It is learned (e.g. Pavlov's dogs salivating at a bell). Involves spinal cord + specialised brain centres.
Simple ReflexConditioned Reflex
Independent of experienceDependent on experience (learned)
One stimulus evokes responseSubstitute + original stimulus evoke response
Reflex is simpleReflex is modified

👁️ The Human Eye
👁️ Accommodation: The ability of the eye to adjust to bring an image from a near or far object into sharp focus on the retina.

Cornea
Transparent, curved → refracts light rays into eye
Iris
Coloured part; radial + circular muscles control pupil size
Pupil
Opening in iris; controls amount of light entering
Lens
Biconvex, elastic; refracts light; changes shape for focus
Ciliary body/muscles
Contract/relax to change lens shape for accommodation
Retina
Contains rods (dim light) and cones (colour/bright light)

Blind spot
No rods/cones; where optic nerve exits
Choroid

Sclera
White, tough outer layer → maintains eye shape

👂 The Human Ear
👂 The ear has TWO functions: Hearing and Balance.

How the Ear Performs Hearing

Pinna collects sound waves→ Auditory canal directs to eardrum→ Eardrum vibrates→ Ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) amplify→ Oval window → cochlea fluid vibrates→ Sensory hairs stimulated → impulses→ Auditory nerve → brain for interpretation
Eustachian tube: Connects middle ear to throat — equalises air pressure between middle ear and atmosphere.
Round window (Fenestra rotunda): Dissipates vibrations from inner ear to middle ear.

How the Ear Maintains Balance

  • Three semi-circular canals arranged at right angles to each other
  • Each canal has an ampulla with sensory receptors (hair cells)
  • Head movement causes endolymph to move in one or more canals
  • Fluid movement stimulates sensory hairs → impulses generated
  • Auditory nerve transmits impulses to brain for interpretation of body position/balance

What Happens if the Auditory Nerve is Damaged?

  • Deafness (no sound interpretation)
  • Loss of body balance
  • Impulses not transmitted to brain
✏️ Practice Questions

1. State the function of the myelin sheath in a neuron. What happens at the nodes of Ranvier?

Myelin sheath: Insulates the axon and speeds up the transmission of nerve impulses.
Nodes of Ranvier: Gaps between Schwann cells where the axon is not covered. Impulses "jump" from node to node (saltatory conduction), which greatly speeds up impulse transmission.

2. Describe the pathway of a reflex action when a person touches a hot object.

1. Heat receptors in skin are stimulated → 2. Sensory neuron carries impulse to spinal cord → 3. Impulse crosses synapse to relay neuron (in grey matter) → 4. Motor neuron carries impulse to effector muscle → 5. Muscle contracts → 6. Hand is withdrawn. All this happens without conscious thought.

3. Explain the accommodation of the eye when focusing from a far object to a near object.

For near objects: Ciliary muscles CONTRACT → tension in suspensory ligaments REDUCES → lens BULGES/thickens (increases curvature) → radial muscles contract, circular muscles relax → pupil enlarges to allow more light. The thicker, more curved lens bends light more sharply to focus it on the retina.

4. State THREE differences between nervous and hormonal communication.

1. Speed: Nervous is rapid; hormonal is slower.
2. Transmission: Nervous is electrical through neurons; hormonal is chemical through blood.
3. Duration: Nervous response is short-lasting; hormonal response persists longer.
4. Target: Nervous response is localised; hormonal is widespread.

5. A person is suffering from cretinism. Which gland is malfunctioning and which hormone is deficient? State TWO effects of this condition.

Gland: Thyroid gland. Hormone deficient: Thyroxine (under-secretion).
Effects: (1) Retarded growth (dwarfism); (2) Low mental development/intelligence; (3) Lowered metabolism; (4) Low body temperature; (5) Coarse hair and puffy eyes.
