Definition and Range of Probability

P(E) = Number of favourable outcomes / Total outcomes
0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1    P(impossible) = 0    P(certain) = 1
DescriptionProbability
Impossible0
Very unlikelyClose to 0
Even chance (50:50)0.5
Very likelyClose to 1
Certain1
Example: A fair die is rolled. Find P(even number).
S = {1,2,3,4,5,6}; even = {2,4,6} → P(even) = 3/6 = ½

Sample Space and Theoretical Probability

The sample space S is the set of all possible outcomes.

Two coins tossed:
S = {HH, HT, TH, TT} → |S| = 4
P(two heads) = 1/4    P(at least one head) = 3/4
Two dice rolled — P(sum = 7):
Favourable: (1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1) → 6 outcomes
P(sum=7) = 6/36 = 1/6

Combined Events — Addition Rule

Mutually exclusive events (cannot both occur):
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)
Not mutually exclusive:
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)
Example: From a pack of 52 cards. P(King or Heart).
P(King)=4/52; P(Heart)=13/52; P(King of Hearts)=1/52
P(King or Heart) = 4/52 + 13/52 − 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13

Combined Events — Multiplication Rule

Independent events:
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
Dependent events (conditional):
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
Example: Bag has 3 red and 5 blue balls. Two drawn without replacement. P(both red).
P(1st red) = 3/8
P(2nd red | 1st red) = 2/7
P(both red) = 3/8 × 2/7 = 6/56 = 3/28

Tree Diagrams

Tree diagrams show all possible outcomes of successive events and their probabilities.

Example: Coin tossed twice.

Start ──H (½)──── H (½) → HH = ¼
                 └── T (½) → HT = ¼
      └──T (½)──── H (½) → TH = ¼
                 └── T (½) → TT = ¼
P(exactly 1 head) = P(HT) + P(TH) = ¼ + ¼ = ½
Key Rule: Multiply probabilities along branches; add probabilities for separate paths leading to the same outcome.
