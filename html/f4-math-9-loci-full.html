📍 Definition of Locus

What is a Locus?

A locus (plural: loci) is the set of all points that satisfy a given condition or rule.

Think of it as: the path traced by a point as it moves according to a fixed rule.
Common Loci:
ConditionLocus
Fixed distance r from point OCircle, centre O, radius r
Equidistant from two points A and BPerpendicular bisector of AB
Equidistant from two linesAngle bisector
Fixed distance from a lineTwo parallel lines
⭕ Locus as a Circle

Points at Fixed Distance from a Point

The locus of all points at distance r from a fixed point O is a circle with centre O and radius r.

Example 1:

A dog is tied to a post with a 5m rope. The locus of the dog's maximum reach is a circle of radius 5m centred on the post.

Example 2:

Describe the locus of a point P that moves so that |OP| = 3 cm, where O is origin.

Locus: Circle, centre O(0,0), radius 3 cm. Equation: x² + y² = 9

Locus Between Two Radii

Points within distance r from O but at distance ≥ r₁ → annulus (ring) between two circles.

↔ Perpendicular Bisector Locus

Equidistant from Two Points

The locus of all points equidistant from two fixed points A and B is the perpendicular bisector of AB.

Construction: Open compass to more than half AB, draw arcs from A and B above and below. Join intersection points — this is the perpendicular bisector.
Example:

A(2,0) and B(8,0). Find equation of locus of points equidistant from A and B.

Midpoint = (5,0). AB is horizontal, so perp. bisector is vertical: x = 5

Real-life:

A mobile phone tower is to be built equidistant from two towns. The ideal location lies on the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the two towns.

📐 Angle Bisector Locus

Equidistant from Two Lines

The locus of points equidistant from two intersecting lines is the angle bisector of the angle between the lines.

Note: There are two angle bisectors (for the two angles formed) — they are perpendicular to each other.
Construction:

1. Mark equal distances along each line from intersection point.

2. Draw arcs from these points.

3. Join intersection of arcs to the vertex.

Real-life application:

Finding the location of a fire station equidistant from two roads uses the angle bisector locus.

🔗 Intersection of Loci

Finding Points Satisfying Multiple Conditions

When a point must satisfy two conditions simultaneously, find the intersection of both loci.

Example:

Find the locus of points that are:

(i) 4 cm from point A, AND

(ii) equidistant from two points B and C

Draw circle radius 4cm centred at A. Draw perpendicular bisector of BC. The intersection points satisfy both conditions.

Example 2:

Construct a point P such that PA = 3cm and PB = 5cm where AB = 6cm.

Draw circle radius 3cm at A and circle radius 5cm at B. Intersection gives P.

📏 Loci and Inequalities

Regions Defined by Loci

ConditionLocus/Region
PA = rCircle, radius r
PA ≤ rInterior of circle (disk)
PA ≥ rExterior of circle
PA < PBRegion closer to A (one side of perp. bisector)
Example:

Shade the region where a point is within 3cm of A AND closer to A than to B.

Draw circle radius 3 at A. Draw perpendicular bisector of AB. Shade region inside circle on A's side of perp. bisector.

💡 Real-Life Applications

Town Planning

Example:

A hospital must be built within 10km of Town A and within 8km of Town B. The hospital must also be on the same side as the main road (which bisects angle between directions to two mountains).

Solution: Draw circles of radius 10km (A) and 8km (B). Draw angle bisector of roads. The valid region is the intersection of the two circles on the correct side of the bisector.

Navigation

Example:

A ship must stay at least 2km from two rocky islands. The safe zone is outside circles of radius 2km centred on each island — the complement of the union of the two circles.

✏️ Practice Problems

Problem 1

Describe the locus of a point P such that angle APB = 90° where A and B are fixed points.

Solution:

By Thales' theorem, the locus is a circle with AB as diameter (excluding points A and B).

Problem 2

A(0,0), B(6,0). Find the equation of the locus of points equidistant from A and B.

Solution:

PA = PB → √(x²+y²) = √((x−6)²+y²)

x² = x²−12x+36 → 12x = 36 → x = 3 (vertical line)
