⛏️ General Methods of Metal Extraction
🪨 Ore: A rock from which a metal can be extracted economically. A mineral is the useful part of the rock.

Steps in Metal Extraction

  1. Mining — open-cast (near surface) or deep mining
  2. Concentration of ore — increases % of metal:
      • Froth flotation: ore + oil + water + air; ore floats as froth, waste sinks
      • Chemical leaching: ore dissolved in a selective chemical, filtered, then crystallised
  3. Reduction of ore to metal — method depends on reactivity:

Reactivity vs Extraction Method

K, Na, Li, Ca, Mg, Al→ Electrolysis (too reactive for carbon)
Zn, Fe, Sn, Pb→ Reduction by carbon (coke)
Cu, Ag, Au, Pt→ Found free OR roasting of ore
🔑 Rule: The more reactive the metal, the harder and more expensive it is to extract. Carbon cannot reduce metals above it in the reactivity series.

Benefits vs Environmental Costs of Mining

✅ Benefits

  • Employment creation
  • Produces useful materials
  • National wealth/revenue

⚠️ Side Effects

  • Large holes / land damage
  • Dangerous waste materials
  • Displacement of communities
🔋 Extraction of Sodium & Aluminium

Sodium — Downs Process (Electrolysis of molten NaCl)

Chief ore: Rock salt (NaCl). CaCl₂ is added to lower melting point from 800°C to 600°C.
ElectrodeReactionProduct
Cathode (–) steel2Na⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → 2Na(l)Liquid sodium (floats, tapped off)
Anode (+) graphite2Cl⁻(l) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻Chlorine gas (kept separate by steel gauze)

Aluminium — Hall's Cell (Electrolysis of molten Al₂O₃ in cryolite)

Chief ore: Bauxite (Al₂O₃·2H₂O). Al₂O₃ dissolved in molten cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) to lower temperature from 2050°C to 900–1000°C.

Ore concentration steps:

  1. Heat with conc. NaOH → Al₂O₃ dissolves as sodium aluminate; Fe₂O₃ filtered off
    Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH + 3H₂O → 2Na[Al(OH)₄](aq)
  2. Treat with CO₂ → Al(OH)₃ precipitates
    Na[Al(OH)₄] + CO₂ → Al(OH)₃↓ + NaOH
  3. Roast at 1000°C:
    2Al(OH)₃ → Al₂O₃ + 3H₂O
ElectrodeReactionProduct
Cathode (–) carbon liningAl³⁺(l) + 3e⁻ → Al(l)Liquid aluminium (tapped off from bottom)
Anode (+) carbon rods2O²⁻(l) → O₂(g) + 4e⁻Oxygen — burns carbon anodes (must replace regularly)
⚠️ Overall: 4Al³⁺ + 6O²⁻ → 4Al + 3O₂. Oxygen burns the carbon anodes — they must be replaced frequently.
🏭 Extraction of Iron, Zinc & Copper

Iron — Blast Furnace

Raw materials: Haematite (Fe₂O₃) + Coke (C) + Limestone (CaCO₃). Hot air blasted through tuyères.
1. C + O₂ → CO₂ (coke burns — exothermic, raises temp to 1600°C) 2. CO₂ + C → 2CO (CO₂ reduced to CO — endothermic) 3. Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂ (iron(III) oxide reduced to iron) 4. CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (limestone decomposes) 5. CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃(l) (slag — removes silica impurity)

Iron from blast furnace = pig/cast iron (~95% Fe, brittle). Wrought iron = 99% Fe, not brittle.

Zinc — Reduction by Carbon

Chief ore: Zinc blende (ZnS). Steps: froth flotation → roast in air → reduce with coke.
2ZnS + 3O₂ → 2ZnO + 2SO₂ (roasting) ZnO + C → Zn + CO (reduction at 1300°C) Zinc vapour collected & condensed (b.p. 907°C)

Copper — From Copper Pyrites (CuFeS₂)

Steps: froth flotation → roasting → reduction → electrolytic refining.
2CuFeS₂ + 4O₂ → Cu₂S + 2FeO + 3SO₂ (roasting) FeO + SiO₂ → FeSiO₃(l) (slag removal) 2Cu₂S + 3O₂ → 2Cu₂O + 2SO₂ 2Cu₂O + Cu₂S → 6Cu + SO₂ (blister copper ~97%)

Electrolytic refining: Impure copper = anode, pure copper = cathode, CuSO₄ solution = electrolyte.

Anode: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ (anode dissolves) Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (pure copper deposited)
💡 Sludge (gold, silver, platinum) collects below the anode — valuable by-products!

Explaining Physical Properties

High m.p. & b.p.

Strong metallic bonds — fixed positive ions in a "sea" of delocalised electrons. More delocalised e⁻ = stronger bond = higher m.p.

Exception: Na has low m.p. — only 1 e⁻ per atom → weak bonds.

Thermal & electrical conductivity

Delocalised electrons carry both heat and electrical charge. Heat → electrons vibrate and pass energy along. Electricity → electrons flow along the wire.

Ductility

Layers of atoms slip over each other when pulled → wire formation. Metal bond is non-directional, so structure doesn't break.

Malleability

Layers slip when hammered → thin sheets. Same principle as ductility — atoms slide without shattering.

📏 Density increases with atomic number because atomic mass increases. Group I metals (Na, K) are exceptions — low density due to large atomic radius and weak metallic bonds.

✏️ Practice Questions

1. Why is electrolysis used to extract sodium but not iron?

Sodium is very high in the reactivity series (electrochemical series) and has a very high negative electrode potential. Carbon cannot displace it from its compounds. Electrolysis must be used. Iron has a lower reactivity and carbon (coke) can easily reduce iron(III) oxide to iron in the blast furnace.

2. Why are carbon anodes in the Hall's cell replaced frequently?

Oxygen gas is produced at the carbon anodes. At the high temperature of 900–1000°C, this oxygen reacts with and burns the carbon anodes, forming CO₂ and CO. The anodes gradually burn away and must be replaced.

3. State the role of limestone (CaCO₃) in the blast furnace. Give equations.

Limestone removes acidic impurities (silica SiO₂ and alumina Al₂O₃) as slag.
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃(l) [slag]
The slag is less dense than molten iron, floats on top, and is tapped off separately.

4. Which metal reacts with cold water but not steam? Which reacts with steam but not cold water? Give equations.

Sodium reacts vigorously with cold water: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑
Iron and zinc react only with steam:
2Fe + 3H₂O(g) → Fe₂O₃ + 3H₂↑
Zn + H₂O(g) → ZnO + H₂↑
Copper does NOT react with either.

5. Explain why galvanising iron with zinc prevents corrosion even when the zinc coating is scratched.

Zinc is higher than iron in the electrochemical series, making it a more reactive metal. When the zinc coating is scratched and both metals are exposed, zinc acts as a sacrificial anode — it oxidises (corrodes) preferentially, protecting the iron underneath. Zinc continues to protect iron even when damaged.
