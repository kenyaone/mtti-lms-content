Introduction to Nitrogen

Nitrogen makes up about 78% of air as diatomic molecules (N₂). The N≡N triple bond is very strong, making nitrogen largely unreactive (inert).

Its inert nature means it dilutes oxygen in air, slowing down respiration, burning, and rusting.

Properties of Nitrogen & Preparation

Physical & Chemical Properties

  • Colourless, odourless, tasteless gas; almost insoluble in water
  • Slightly lighter than air
  • Inert (unreactive) due to strong N≡N triple bond
  • Neither burns nor supports combustion

Chemical test for nitrogen: Confirmed by elimination — it extinguishes a splint, has no smell/colour, does not turn lime water milky, neutral to litmus.

Laboratory Preparation

Heat concentrated sodium nitrite + ammonium chloride:

NaNO₂(aq) + NH₄Cl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + N₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) [heat]

Collected over water. This N₂ is purer than N₂ isolated from air (no noble gas impurities).

Alternatively, heat ammonium nitrite:

NH₄NO₂(s) → N₂(g) + 2H₂O(g) [heat]

Isolation from Liquid Air (Fractional Distillation)

StepProcess
1Remove dust by filtration/electrostatic precipitation
2Remove CO₂ with KOH solution
3Remove water vapour with conc. H₂SO₄ or condensation at –25°C
4Compress at ~200 atm, cool, expand → liquid air
5Fractional distillation: N₂ boils at –196°C, O₂ at –183°C → N₂ taken off first

Reaction of N₂ with burning Mg:

3Mg(s) + N₂(g) → Mg₃N₂(s) [heat]

Mg₃N₂ + H₂O → ammonia smell detected:

Mg₃N₂(s) + 6H₂O(l) → 2NH₃(g) + 3Mg(OH)₂(aq)

Uses of Nitrogen

  • Haber process: manufacture of ammonia
  • Filling electric bulbs (inert — no soot formation)
  • Liquid N₂ as inert refrigerant (semen storage for AI)
  • Food preservation in canned products
  • Enhanced oil recovery in oil fields

Ammonia – Preparation and Properties

Laboratory Preparation

Heat ammonium chloride + slaked lime (calcium hydroxide):

Ca(OH)₂(s) + 2NH₄Cl(s) → CaCl₂(aq) + 2NH₃(g) + 2H₂O(g)

Drying: Passed through quicklime (CaO) or caustic potash (KOH pellets).

NOT dried with conc. H₂SO₄ (reacts: 2NH₃ + H₂SO₄ → (NH₄)₂SO₄) or anhydrous CaCl₂ (forms complex CaCl₂·4NH₃).

Collection: Upward delivery (lighter than air; very soluble in water).

Tests for Ammonia

  • Pungent smell (urine-like)
  • Only common alkaline gas — turns moist red litmus paper blue
  • Dense white fumes with HCl gas:
    NH₃(g) + HCl(g) → NH₄Cl(s) [white fumes]

Physical Properties

  • Colourless gas, pungent smell
  • Lighter than air (collected by upward delivery)
  • Highly soluble in water (1 vol water dissolves ~750 vol NH₃)

Fountain experiment: A drop of water dissolving in the flask creates a partial vacuum → atmospheric pressure forces water in as a fountain (turns blue from alkali).

NH₃ dissolves in water:

NH₃(g) + H₂O(l) ⇌ NH₄⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) [weakly alkaline]

Chemical Properties of Ammonia

Burning in oxygen:

4NH₃(g) + 3O₂(g) → 2N₂(g) + 6H₂O(g)

Catalytic oxidation (Pt catalyst → basis of Ostwald process):

4NH₃(g) + 5O₂(g) → 4NO(g) + 6H₂O(g) [Pt catalyst]

As reducing agent (reduces CuO):

3CuO(s) + 2NH₃(g) → 3Cu(s) + 3H₂O(l) + N₂(g)

Reaction with chlorine:

8NH₃(g) + 3Cl₂(g) → 6NH₄Cl(s) + N₂(g)

With acids (neutralisation):

NH₃(g) + HCl(aq) → NH₄Cl(aq)
2NH₃(g) + H₂SO₄(aq) → (NH₄)₂SO₄(aq)

The Haber Process – Industrial Manufacture of Ammonia

Raw Materials

  • Nitrogen: Fractional distillation of liquid air
  • Hydrogen: From water gas (Bosch process) or cracking of crude oil

Overall Equation

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) + Heat (–92 kJ/mol)

Only 6–10% of the gases combine in one pass. Unreacted gases are recycled.

Conditions and Reasons

ConditionValueReason
Pressure200–500 atmReactants have more volume than products → high pressure shifts equilibrium right (more NH₃)
Temperature~450°C (compromise)Low temp favours NH₃ (exothermic), but too slow → 450°C balances yield and rate
CatalystFinely divided Fe + Al₂O₃Fe speeds up equilibrium; Al₂O₃ (promoter) increases catalyst activity
PurificationRemove CO₂, H₂O, dust, SO₂These poison the catalyst

Process Steps

  1. Purification — remove impurities that poison catalyst
  2. Compression — gases compressed at ~500 atm
  3. Heat exchanger — gases heated to reaction temperature
  4. Catalytic chamber — N₂ + H₂ react at 450°C over Fe catalyst
  5. Second heat exchanger — hot products cool incoming gases (energy efficiency)
  6. Condenser — NH₃ liquefied and drawn off; unreacted N₂/H₂ recycled
💡 Siting factors: availability of raw materials (natural gas/crude oil), cheap energy, transport, technology and labour force.

Nitric(V) Acid & The Ostwald Process

Laboratory Preparation of HNO₃

React conc. H₂SO₄ with solid potassium nitrate (KNO₃) gently — all-glass apparatus (HNO₃ attacks rubber):

KNO₃(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → KHSO₄(aq) + HNO₃(g)

HNO₃ vapour condenses in a cooled collection flask. Appears brown due to thermal decomposition into NO₂.

Ostwald Process (Industrial)

Raw materials: Ammonia (from Haber process) + atmospheric air

StepReaction
1. Catalytic oxidation of NH₃4NH₃ + 5O₂ → 4NO + 6H₂O [Pt-Rh, 900°C]
2. Oxidation of NO to NO₂2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂
3. Absorption in water2NO₂ + H₂O → HNO₃ + HNO₂
4. Oxidation of HNO₂2HNO₂ + O₂ → 2HNO₃

Result: ~55–65% HNO₃; concentrated further by distillation with conc. H₂SO₄.

Properties of Dilute HNO₃

Dilute HNO₃ is a typical acid but reacts with metals to give NO (not H₂):

3Mg(s) + 8HNO₃(dil) → 3Mg(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2NO(g) + 4H₂O(l)

Also neutralises bases, reacts with carbonates and metal oxides.

Properties of Concentrated HNO₃

Strong oxidising agent. Reduced to NO₂ and water:

Cu(s) + 4HNO₃(conc) → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2NO₂ + 2H₂O

Oxidises C, S, Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺. Decomposes on heating:

4HNO₃(l) → 4NO₂(g) + O₂(g) + 2H₂O(g)

Stored in dark bottles (light causes decomposition).

Test for Nitrates – Brown Ring Test

Dissolve test substance in H₂O, acidify with dil H₂SO₄, add FeSO₄ solution, then carefully add conc. H₂SO₄ down the side of the tube:

→ Brown ring at interface = NO₃⁻ present

Fe²⁺(aq) + NO(g) → [Fe(H₂O)₅NO]²⁺(aq) [brown ring complex]

Uses of nitric acid: fertilisers, explosives (TNT), dyes, nylon, metal refining, oxidising agent.

Environmental Pollution by Nitrogen Compounds

1. Acid Rain

NO from combustion engines → NO₂ → dissolves in rain → HNO₂/HNO₃ (acid rain)

Effects of acid rain:

  • Loss of chlorophyll (chlorosis) from plant leaves
  • Corrosion of stone buildings and metallic structures
  • Leaching of minerals from soil → poor crop yields

2. Smog Formation

NO₂ undergoes chemical reactions in air to form smog — reduces visibility, irritates eyes, causes breathing problems.

3. Eutrophication

Excess nitrate ions (from fertilisers) in water → rapid algal growth → reduced dissolved O₂ → kills aquatic animals. Nitrates in drinking water may form carcinogenic compounds.

Prevention Measures

  1. Recycle unreacted gases in HNO₃ manufacture
  2. Treat sewage before releasing to rivers
  3. Fit exhaust systems with catalytic converters (convert NOₓ → N₂)
  4. Add lime to acidified lakes and soils
  5. Apply fertilisers in correct proportions

Q5. Explain with equations why high pressure is used in the Haber process but a compromise temperature of ~450°C is used instead of a very low temperature.
High pressure is used because the forward reaction produces fewer moles of gas (4 moles) from more moles of reactants (4 moles N₂ + H₂ = combined), so high pressure shifts equilibrium right producing more NH₃. A very low temperature would give a higher yield (reaction is exothermic) but the rate would be too slow. A compromise of ~450°C balances an acceptable yield with a reasonable rate of reaction.
Q6. Describe the laboratory test to distinguish between SO₄²⁻ and CO₃²⁻ ions in solution.
Add barium chloride solution to both samples. Both form white precipitates (BaSO₄ and BaCO₃). Then acidify with dilute hydrochloric acid or nitric acid. BaCO₃ dissolves (effervescence of CO₂) while BaSO₄ remains as a white precipitate. The sample that dissolves contains CO₃²⁻; the one that does not dissolve contains SO₄²⁻.
Q7. Describe what happens when lead(II) nitrate is heated. Write the equation and state all observations.
White crystalline lead(II) nitrate crystals decrepitate (cracking sound) on heating. A colourless gas (oxygen) is produced first, immediately followed by red-brown fumes of nitrogen(IV) oxide (NO₂). A residue remains which is red when hot and yellow on cooling (lead(II) oxide, PbO). A pale yellow liquid (dinitrogen tetroxide, N₂O₄) may condense on cooling. Equation: 2Pb(NO₃)₂(s) → 2PbO(s) + 4NO₂(g) + O₂(g)
