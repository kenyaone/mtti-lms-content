💥 Rate of Reaction & Collision Theory
📐 Rate of Reaction = Change in amount of substance ÷ Time taken
It measures how fast a reactant is consumed or a product is formed per unit time.

Fast reactions

  • Burning of magnesium
  • Explosion of gunpowder
  • Neutralisation reactions

Slow reactions

  • Rusting of iron
  • Burning of charcoal
  • Ripening of fruit

Collision Theory

  • Chemical reactions occur when reacting particles (atoms, ions or molecules) collide
  • Not all collisions produce a reaction — only fruitful collisions do
  • For a collision to be fruitful, particles must have at least the activation energy
  • Collision frequency = rate at which particles collide. Higher frequency → faster reaction
  • Solids only react at their surface — interior particles are locked inside
  • Dissolved solids react faster because particles can move freely and collide
⚠️ Solid NaCl and solid Pb(NO₃)₂ do NOT react when mixed — solid particles cannot collide. But dissolved in water, they react immediately because ions move freely.
⚡ Activation Energy & Energy Diagrams
⚡ Activation Energy (Eₐ): The minimum energy that colliding particles must possess for a chemical reaction to occur — enough energy to break existing bonds.

Example: H₂ and O₂ don't react at room temperature without ignition — the flame provides the activation energy to break H–H and O=O bonds.

2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l) [requires ignition spark]

Energy Diagrams

Exothermic reaction

  • Products at lower energy than reactants
  • ΔH is negative
  • Self-sustaining once started
  • Energy released from bond formation > energy to break bonds

Endothermic reaction

  • Products at higher energy than reactants
  • ΔH is positive
  • Needs continuous energy supply
  • Energy to break bonds > energy released in bond formation
🔑 Key rule: Lower activation energy = faster reaction rate. A catalyst lowers Eₐ without being consumed.
🔬 Factors Affecting Rate of Reaction

Six main factors affect reaction rate. Each must be investigated while keeping others constant.

1. Concentration

↑ Concentration → ↑ particles per volume → ↑ collision frequency → ↑ rate

Experiment: Na₂S₂O₃ + HCl → sulphur precipitate (cross disappears faster)

Na₂S₂O₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + SO₂ + S↓

2. Temperature

↑ Temp → ↑ kinetic energy → ↑ velocity → ↑ collision frequency + more particles exceed Eₐ → ↑ rate

Experiment: Mg + HCl at 30°C, 40°C, 50°C — graph of H₂ volume vs time

3. Surface Area

↑ Surface area (smaller pieces) → more exposed particles → ↑ collisions → ↑ rate

Experiment: CaCO₃ powder vs chips + HCl — mass loss measured

CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑

4. Catalyst

Lowers activation energy → more particles react → ↑ rate. Not consumed.

Experiment: MnO₂ catalyses H₂O₂ decomposition

2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂↑ (MnO₂ catalyst)

5. Light

Light energy initiates reactions. ↑ Intensity → ↑ rate.

  • Photosynthesis (sunlight)
  • 2AgBr → 2Ag + Br₂ (photography)
  • Cl₂ + H₂ → 2HCl (bright sunlight = explosive)

6. Pressure (gases only)

↑ Pressure → same particles in smaller volume → ↑ collision frequency → ↑ rate

Only affects gaseous reactants

📊 Rate ∝ 1/time. Plotting 1/t against concentration gives a straight line through origin.
🧪 Catalysts & How They Work
🧪 Catalyst: A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being chemically changed at the end of the reaction.

How a Catalyst Works

A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy (E₂ < E₁). More particles can now exceed this lower energy barrier, leading to more fruitful collisions.

Common Industrial Catalysts

ProcessCatalystReaction
Haber process (NH₃)Finely divided iron (Fe)N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
Contact process (H₂SO₄)Vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅)2SO₂ + O₂ → 2SO₃

✅ A catalyst does NOT affect the position of equilibrium — it only helps the equilibrium to be reached faster. The final composition of the mixture is unchanged.
⇌ Reversible Reactions & Chemical Equilibrium
⇌ Reversible Reaction: A reaction in which products can be converted back to reactants under suitable conditions. Shown by ⇌ (double arrows).

Example 1 — Copper(II) Sulphate

CuSO₄·5H₂O(s) ⇌ CuSO₄(s) + 5H₂O(l) (blue crystals) (white powder) Heat: forward → Cool + add water: ← backward

The white anhydrous CuSO₄ turns blue again when water is added — this is also used as a test for water.

Example 2 — Chromate/Dichromate

2CrO₄²⁻(aq) + 2H⁺(aq) ⇌ Cr₂O₇²⁻(aq) + H₂O(l) (yellow) (orange)

Add acid (HCl) → more H⁺ → equilibrium shifts right → orange
Add alkali (NaOH) → H⁺ removed → equilibrium shifts left → yellow

Chemical Equilibrium

⚖️ Chemical Equilibrium: A state reached in a closed system where the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the backward reaction. Concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (but not necessarily equal).
A + B ⇌ C + D At equilibrium: Rate(forward) = Rate(backward)
🔑 Equilibrium is dynamic — both forward and backward reactions are still occurring, but at the same rate. It is NOT a static state.
⚖️ Le Chatelier's Principle
⚖️ Le Chatelier's Principle: "If a system in equilibrium is subjected to a stress (change), the equilibrium will shift in the direction which tends to relieve that stress."

Effect of Concentration

Br₂(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ OBr⁻(aq) + Br⁻(aq) + 2H⁺(aq) yellow-orange colourless
  • Add NaOH (removes H⁺) → equilibrium shifts right → colourless
  • Add HCl (adds H⁺) → equilibrium shifts left → yellow-orange

Effect of Temperature

2NO₂(g) ⇌ N₂O₄(g) ΔH = –ve (exothermic forward) (dark-brown) (pale-yellow)
  • ↑ Temperature → shifts left → more NO₂ → dark-brown
  • ↓ Temperature → shifts right → more N₂O₄ → pale-yellow

2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g) ΔH = –197 kJ/mol 3 moles 2 moles (exothermic)
FactorTheoretical bestActual conditionReason

✏️ Practice Questions

1. State the collision theory and explain what is meant by a "fruitful collision".

Collision theory states that chemical reactions occur when reacting particles (atoms, ions or molecules) collide. A fruitful collision is one that produces a chemical reaction — the particles must collide with energy equal to or greater than the activation energy and with the correct orientation.

2. A student adds manganese(IV) oxide to hydrogen peroxide solution. State TWO observations and write the equation for the reaction.

Observations: (i) Vigorous effervescence/rapid bubbling occurs. (ii) A glowing splint relights (confirming oxygen gas).
Equation: 2H₂O₂(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g) [MnO₂ catalyst]

3. Consider: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = –92 kJ/mol. State the effect of: (a) increasing pressure, (b) increasing temperature, (c) adding iron catalyst — on the yield of ammonia.

(a) ↑ Pressure → equilibrium shifts RIGHT (4 moles → 2 moles) → more NH₃ formed.
(b) ↑ Temperature → equilibrium shifts LEFT (exothermic forward reaction) → less NH₃ formed.
(c) Adding catalyst → no effect on yield — equilibrium reached faster only; final composition unchanged.

4. Why does powdered marble react faster with hydrochloric acid than marble chips of the same mass?

Powdered marble has a much greater total surface area than marble chips. More surface area means more particles are exposed and available for collision with HCl particles. This increases the frequency of fruitful collisions, hence the rate of reaction is faster.

5. State Le Chatelier's Principle and use it to explain what happens when sodium hydroxide is added to the equilibrium: 2CrO₄²⁻ + 2H⁺ ⇌ Cr₂O₇²⁻ + H₂O.

Le Chatelier's Principle: If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a stress, it shifts to relieve that stress.
Adding NaOH introduces OH⁻ ions which react with H⁺: OH⁻ + H⁺ → H₂O. This decreases [H⁺]. The equilibrium shifts LEFT to replace H⁺, forming more CrO₄²⁻. The solution changes from orange back to yellow.
