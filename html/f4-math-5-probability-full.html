🎲 Basic Probability

Definition

P(A) = Number of favourable outcomes / Total outcomes
Sample Space (S): The set of all possible outcomes. E.g. for a die: S = {1,2,3,4,5,6}
Event: A subset of the sample space.

For any event A: 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1

P(certain event) = 1  |  P(impossible event) = 0

Worked Examples

Example 1: Rolling a die

P(even) = 3/6 = 1/2  |  P(prime) = 3/6 = 1/2

Example 2: Drawing a card

From a pack of 52 cards, P(King) = 4/52 = 1/13

➕ Addition Law

Mutually Exclusive Events

Events that cannot both occur at the same time.

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

(for mutually exclusive events)

Example:

P(rolling a 2 or 5) = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3

Non-Mutually Exclusive Events

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)
Example:

P(King or Heart) = P(K) + P(H) − P(K of Hearts) = 4/52 + 13/52 − 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13

Complementary Events: P(A') = 1 − P(A)  |  P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
✖️ Multiplication Law

Independent Events

Events where the outcome of one does not affect the other.

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
Example:

Tossing a coin and rolling a die. P(Head and 6) = 1/2 × 1/6 = 1/12

Dependent Events (Conditional)

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A)

P(B|A) = probability of B given A has occurred

Example:

Bag has 5 red, 3 blue balls. Two drawn without replacement.

P(both red) = 5/8 × 4/7 = 20/56 = 5/14

🌳 Tree Diagrams

Using Tree Diagrams

Tree diagrams show all possible outcomes and their probabilities.

Example: Tossing a coin twice

S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}

P(HH) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4

P(at least one head) = 1 − P(TT) = 1 − 1/4 = 3/4

Example: Bag problem

Bag A: 3 red, 2 blue. Bag B: 4 red, 1 blue. Draw one from each.

P(both red) = 3/5 × 4/5 = 12/25

P(one of each) = P(RB) + P(BR) = 3/5×1/5 + 2/5×4/5 = 3/25+8/25 = 11/25

🔢 Conditional Probability

Definition

P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A)

Probability of B given that A has already occurred

Example 1:

In a class: 15 boys, 10 girls. 6 boys and 4 girls play football.

P(plays football | boy) = 6/15 = 2/5

Example 2:

P(A) = 0.4, P(B) = 0.3, P(A∩B) = 0.12

P(B|A) = 0.12/0.4 = 0.3

Since P(B|A) = P(B), A and B are independent.

💡 Real-Life Applications

Medical Testing

Example:

A disease affects 1% of population. A test is 95% accurate. If a person tests positive, what is the probability they have the disease?

This uses Bayes' theorem: P(Disease|Positive) = P(Pos|Disease)×P(Disease) / P(Positive)

= (0.95×0.01) / (0.95×0.01 + 0.05×0.99) = 0.0095/0.059 ≈ 0.161 (16.1%)

Sports & Weather

Weather Example:

P(rain Mon) = 0.6, P(rain Tue | rained Mon) = 0.7

P(rain both days) = 0.6 × 0.7 = 0.42

P(rain at least one day) = 1 − P(no rain either) = 1 − (0.4×0.3) = 0.88

✏️ Practice Problems

Problem 1

A bag contains 4 red, 3 blue, 2 green balls. One is drawn at random. Find: P(red), P(not blue), P(red or green).

Solution:

Total = 9. P(red) = 4/9. P(not blue) = 6/9 = 2/3. P(red or green) = 6/9 = 2/3

Problem 2

Two cards drawn without replacement from a pack of 52. Find P(both aces).

Solution:

P(both aces) = 4/52 × 3/51 = 12/2652 = 1/221

Problem 3

P(A) = 1/3, P(B) = 1/4, A and B independent. Find P(A∪B).

Solution:

P(A∩B) = 1/3 × 1/4 = 1/12

P(A∪B) = 1/3 + 1/4 − 1/12 = 4/12 + 3/12 − 1/12 = 6/12 = 1/2
