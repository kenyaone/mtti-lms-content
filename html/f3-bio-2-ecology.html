📖 Ecological Terms

🔬 Ecology
Study of interrelationships between organisms and their environment
🌐 Environment
Surroundings of the organism — biotic or abiotic factors
🏠 Habitat
Specific locality where an organism lives, with a set of environmental conditions
🎭 Ecological Niche
The role of an organism in its habitat, e.g. feeding relationship
👥 Population
A group of organisms of the same species occupying a given habitat
🌐 Community
Different species of organisms coexisting and interacting in a given habitat
🌿 Ecosystem
A community of organisms interacting with each other and the environment
🌏 Biosphere
The Earth and its atmosphere where living organisms are found
⚖️ Carrying Capacity
Maximum number of organisms an area can support without being depleted
📦 Biomass
Dry weight (mass) of living organisms in a given area (kg/m²/year)
🗺️ Biome
Geographical area with particular climatic conditions and flora/fauna — contains many ecosystems
🔭 Autecology / Synecology
Autecology = study of a single species. Synecology = study of communities/populations interacting in an ecosystem

💨 Wind

  • Influences rate of water evaporation from organisms
  • Increases rate of transpiration and soil evaporation
  • Agent of soil erosion; may break/uproot trees
  • Aids formation of sand dunes (habitats for desert plants)
  • Disperses fruits, seeds, and spores
  • Creates waves in water → aerates water → replenishes O₂
  • Agent of pollination

🌡️ Temperature

  • Influences rate of enzyme action in photosynthesis and metabolism
  • Organisms function within a narrow temperature range
  • Affects distribution of organisms
  • Changes in temperature affect rate of photosynthesis and biochemical reactions
  • Temperature increases rate of transpiration

☀️ Light

  • Needed by green plants and photosynthetic bacteria (primary producers)
  • Animals depend on plants directly or indirectly for food
  • Main source of light is the sun
  • Necessary for synthesis of Vitamin D in certain animals
  • Some plants need light for flowering
  • Seeds like lettuce need light for germination

💧 Humidity

  • Amount of water vapour held by air
  • Affects rate of water loss from organisms by evaporation and transpiration
  • Low humidity → increased rate of transpiration
  • Influences distribution of organisms

🌧️ Rainfall / Water

  • Amount and distribution affects vegetation type
  • Fewer organisms in deserts (less rainfall)
  • Water: raw material for photosynthesis; solvent for mineral salts
  • Provides turgidity for plant support; medium for transport
  • Disperses fruits, seeds, and spores

⛰️ Topography

  • Altitude affects light, atmospheric pressure and temperature
  • Slope influences surface runoff and wind erosion
  • Mountains affect organism distribution (leeward vs windward side)
  • Mountains act as physical barriers → isolation of species
  • Background may offer camouflage to some organisms

🧪 pH

  • Each plant requires a specific pH to grow (acidic, neutral, or alkaline)
  • pH affects enzyme reaction in metabolism

🧂 Salinity

  • Some ions needed for plant and animal nutrition
  • Osmoregulation in plants and animals is affected by salinity

🦁 Biotic Factors

What are Biotic Factors?

Living organisms in an area that interact with each other in various ways. Examples include feeding relationships, predation, competition, diseases, and human activities.

FactorDescriptionEffect on Organisms
CompetitionOrganisms compete for food, light, water, mates, shelterPoorly adapted organisms die or migrate; better-adapted ones increase in population
PredationPredator feeds on preyBoth control each other's population; without predators, prey exceeds carrying capacity → population crash
ParasitismOrganism lives on/in host, gets food, causes harmDeprives host of food; weakens host; reduces reproductive ability
SymbiosisAssociation between different species where both benefitMutual benefit (mutualism)
SaprophytismOrganisms obtain organic matter from dead tissuesRecycles nutrients (C, N) from dead to living organisms
Human ActivitiesDeforestation, agriculture, pollution, poachingAffects ecosystem balance; loss of biodiversity

📊 Population Estimation Methods

MethodProcedureBest used for
Quadrat MethodThrow quadrats randomly; count organisms; calculate average per quadrat; scale up to total areaStationary organisms: plants, slow invertebrates
Line TransectStretch a string across the study area; count all organisms touching the stringQuick survey along a gradient (e.g. from lake to land)
Belt TransectTwo parallel lines define a strip; count organisms inside; repeat at least 3 times; calculate mean density then total populationMore detailed distribution study
Capture-Recapture (Lincoln Index)Catch → mark with permanent ink → release → recapture; count marked vs unmarked; apply formulaMobile animals: grasshoppers, fish

🧮 Lincoln Index Formula

N = (M × C) ÷ R

N = total population  |  M = number marked in 1st catch  |  C = total caught in 2nd catch  |  R = marked individuals in 2nd catch

Example: M=50, C=80, R=20 → N = (50×80)÷20 = 200

Slide 8 • Scenario

🎯 Apply Your Knowledge

📋 Scenario: Lake Nakuru Ecosystem

Food web: Algae → Flamingos → Fish Eagles; Algae → Small fish → Pelicans → Fish Eagles; Small fish → Hippos.

Farmers in the catchment area are using chemical fertilizers that wash into the lake, causing excessive algal growth (eutrophication).

Q1: What would happen to the flamingo population if fish eagles were completely removed? Use the concept of carrying capacity in your answer.
✅ Model answer: Flamingo population would initially increase rapidly because predation pressure is removed. However, flamingos would eventually exceed the lake's carrying capacity, causing depletion of algae (their food). Food shortage would lead to starvation, disease, and a sharp decline (population crash) — a classic boom-and-crash cycle demonstrating the importance of predator-prey balance.
Q2: Explain how eutrophication from chemical fertilizers would disrupt the food web in the lake.
✅ Model answer: Chemical fertilizers (nitrates/phosphates) wash into the lake → eutrophication = excessive algal growth at the surface. Algae block sunlight from submerged plants → no photosynthesis → submerged plants die. Dead organic matter is decomposed by bacteria, which consume dissolved oxygen → fish and aquatic animals suffocate and die → collapse of higher trophic levels (pelicans, fish eagles).
Slide 9 • Practice

✏️ Practice Questions

1. Distinguish between a food chain and a food web. Give an example of each.
✅ Food chain: A linear nutritional sequence showing energy flow from producers to consumers. Example: Grass → Grasshopper → Lizard → Eagle. If one organism is removed, the chain breaks.

Food web: A complex network of interconnected food chains where consumers feed on more than one type of organism. Example: Grass → (Grasshoppers, Rabbits) → (Lizards, Hawks) → Eagles. More stable than food chains — removal of one organism doesn't collapse the web.
2. Describe in detail the capture-recapture method for estimating a grasshopper population.
✅ Steps: (1) Capture grasshoppers in the study area; (2) Count and mark them using waterproof/permanent ink on their bodies; (3) Record number marked (M); (4) Release and allow time for mixing with general population; (5) Recapture grasshoppers; (6) Count total caught (C) and number that are marked (R); (7) Apply Lincoln Index: N = (M × C) ÷ R. Assumptions: no births/deaths/immigration between catches; marks don't affect survival.
3. Explain how temperature and light interact to affect the productivity of an ecosystem.
✅ Light: The primary energy source for green plants; intensity and duration affect rate of photosynthesis → food production. Without sufficient light, primary production falls, reducing food available for all trophic levels.

Temperature: Controls enzyme activity in metabolic processes including photosynthesis. Optimum temperature maximises enzyme activity → maximum productivity. Extremes (too hot/cold) denature enzymes. Together: tropical regions (high light + warm temperature) have higher ecosystem productivity than polar regions.
