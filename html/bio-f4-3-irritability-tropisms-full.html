⚡ Irritability, Stimulus & Response
⚡ Irritability (Sensitivity): The ability (responsiveness) of a living organism to detect and respond to changes in the environment.

Stimulus

A change in the environment of an organism that causes a change in the organism's activity.

Response

A change in the activity of an organism caused by a stimulus.

Importance of Irritability

  • Allows organisms to adjust to environmental conditions
  • Helps organisms detect and respond to stimuli for survival

🔬 Tactic Responses (Taxes)
🔬 Tactic Response (Taxis): A response in which the whole organism (or its motile parts e.g. gametes) moves in response to a unidirectional stimulus. Named after the source of stimulus.

Characteristics of Tactic Responses

  • Caused by a unidirectional stimulus
  • Usually does NOT involve growth
  • Response is either positive (towards stimulus) or negative (away from stimulus)
  • Named according to the source of stimulus (e.g. phototaxis, chemotaxis, aerotaxis)

Examples of Tactic Responses

Importance of Tactic Responses

Protista (e.g. Euglena)

  • Move towards favourable environment
  • Move away from unfavourable conditions
  • Move towards food/prey

Microscopic plants

  • Escape injurious stimuli
  • Seek favourable habitats
🌿 Tropisms — Types & Mechanisms
🌿 Tropism: A growth movement of plants in response to an external unilateral (unidirectional) stimulus. Shoots/roots grow TOWARDS (+) or AWAY (–) from the stimulus.

☀️ Phototropism

Response to unilateral light. Light causes auxins to migrate to the darker side → more growth on dark side → shoot bends TOWARDS light.

Shoot: positively phototrophic
Root: negatively phototrophic

⬇️ Geotropism

Response to gravity. Auxins accumulate on lower side.

Root: positively geotropic (grows DOWN)
Shoot: negatively geotropic (grows UP)

💧 Hydrotropism

Response to water/moisture.

Root: positively hydrotropic (grows TOWARDS water)
Leaves: negatively hydrotropic

🌀 Thigmotropism

Response to touch/contact. Auxins migrate to outer side → faster growth away from contact → tendrils/stem twine around support.

⚗️ Chemotropism

Response to chemical gradient. e.g. Pollen tube grows towards the ovary through the style following a chemical gradient.

Importance of Tropisms to Plants

  • Phototropism → exposes leaves/shoots for maximum sunlight absorption (photosynthesis)
  • Hydrotropism → enables roots to seek water
  • Thigmotropism → enables stems/tendrils to obtain mechanical support
  • Geotropism → roots grow deep for anchorage; shoots grow upward
  • Chemotropism → enables pollen tube to grow to embryo sac for fertilization
🌱 Auxins & Seedling Experiments
🧪 Auxin (IAA — Indole Acetic Acid): A plant growth hormone produced at the shoot tip. It moves downward and causes cell elongation. Light and gravity affect its distribution, causing bending.

How Auxins Cause Phototropism

  1. Auxins produced at the shoot tip
  2. Light causes auxins to migrate to the darker (shaded) side
  3. Higher concentration of auxins on dark side → more cell elongation
  4. Shoot bends/curves towards the light source

Horizontal Seedling — Geotropism

Shoot (placed horizontal)

Auxins accumulate on lower side due to gravity. High concentration of auxins in shoot stimulates growth → more elongation on lower side → shoot curves upward.

Root (placed horizontal)

Auxins accumulate on lower side. But high concentration of auxins in root inhibits growth → upper side (less auxin) grows faster → root curves downward.

⚠️ Key difference: In shoots, high auxin = MORE growth. In roots, high auxin = LESS growth (roots are more sensitive to auxin).
🌿 Etiolation & Taxis vs Tropism
🌙 Etiolation: A phenomenon exhibited by plants when grown in darkness or very dim light.

Characteristics of Etiolated Plants

  • Pale yellow colour (absence of chlorophyll)
  • Small, underdeveloped leaves
  • Long, slender, weak stems
  • Extended internodes (hypocotyl)
🔑 Plants exhibit etiolation to reach light — it is a survival response. Once light is available, chlorophyll synthesis begins and normal growth resumes.

Practical Importance of Plant Responses in Agriculture

  • Understanding phototropism helps in crop spacing to maximise light absorption
  • Auxin-based herbicides (e.g. 2,4-D) disrupt plant hormone balance to kill weeds
  • Rooting powder contains synthetic auxins to stimulate root growth in cuttings
  • Fruit ripening can be controlled using ethylene (a plant hormone)
  • Understanding geotropism helps explain how seeds orient correctly when planted
✏️ Practice Questions

1. Distinguish between a tactic response and a tropic response. Give ONE example of each.

Tactic: Whole organism moves toward/away from stimulus (locomotory, fast, no growth). e.g. Euglena swimming toward light (positive phototaxis).
Tropic: Plant grows (bends) toward/away from stimulus (growth movement, slow, involves growth). e.g. Shoot bending toward light (positive phototropism).

2. Explain why a shoot bends toward a unilateral source of light.

Auxins (IAA) are produced at the shoot tip and move downward. Unilateral light causes auxins to migrate to the darker (shaded) side. The higher concentration of auxins on the dark side causes greater cell elongation on that side. This unequal growth causes the shoot to bend/curve toward the light source.

3. A seedling was placed horizontally. After 7 days the shoot grew upward and the root grew downward. Explain these results.

Shoot curves upward: Gravity causes auxins to accumulate on the lower side. High auxin concentration stimulates cell elongation on the lower side → lower side grows longer → shoot bends upward (negative geotropism).

Root curves downward: Same auxin accumulation on lower side, but roots are more sensitive to auxin. High auxin concentration inhibits root growth on lower side → upper side (less auxin) grows faster → root bends downward (positive geotropism).

4. What is etiolation? Explain why plants exhibit etiolation.

Etiolation is a phenomenon where plants grown in darkness become pale yellow (no chlorophyll), develop long weak stems, and have small underdeveloped leaves. Plants exhibit etiolation as a survival response — the elongated stems help them reach light as quickly as possible. Once light is reached, normal photosynthesis and development can resume.

5. State FOUR ways in which tropisms are important to plants.

1. Phototropism — exposes leaves/shoots to maximum sunlight for photosynthesis
2. Hydrotropism — enables roots to seek and absorb water
3. Thigmotropism — enables stems/tendrils to obtain mechanical support
4. Geotropism — roots grow deep for anchorage; shoots grow upward toward light
5. Chemotropism — enables pollen tube to grow to embryo sac for fertilization
