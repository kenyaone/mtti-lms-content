Electrolysis - Principles

Key Definitions

  • Electrolysis: Decomposition of an electrolyte using electrical energy
  • Electrolyte: A substance that conducts electricity when molten or in aqueous solution (e.g. ionic compounds)
  • Anode: Positive electrode - where oxidation occurs (anions discharge)
  • Cathode: Negative electrode - where reduction occurs (cations discharge)
Memory Aid: An Ox (Anode = Oxidation), Red Cat (Reduction = Cathode). OILRIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)

Discharge of Ions

In a mixture of ions, the order of discharge depends on position in the electrochemical series and concentration.

  • At cathode: metal ions or H+ discharged first (metal lower in reactivity series discharged preferentially)
  • At anode: Cl-, Br-, I- discharged preferentially over OH-; if only OH- present, O2 is produced

Electrode Equations

Electrolysis of dilute H2SO4:
Cathode: 2H+ + 2e- rarr H2 (reduction)
Anode: 4OH- rarr 2H2O + O2 + 4e- (oxidation)
Volume of H2 : O2 = 2 : 1
Electrolysis of CuSO4 (inert electrodes):
Cathode: Cu2+ + 2e- rarr Cu (copper deposited)
Anode: 2H2O rarr O2 + 4H+ + 4e- (oxygen produced)
Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

First Law

The mass of substance deposited at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge) passed.

m = proportional to Q, where Q = It (charge = current x time)

Second Law

The mass of different substances deposited by the same quantity of electricity is proportional to their relative atomic mass divided by valency (equivalent mass).

m = (A x I x t) / (n x F), where A = relative atomic mass, n = valency, F = 96500 C/mol (Faraday constant)

Worked Examples

Example 1: What mass of copper is deposited when 2A flows for 965 seconds? (A of Cu = 64, n = 2)
Q = 2 x 965 = 1930 C
m = (64 x 1930)/(2 x 96500) = 0.64g
Example 2: How long to deposit 1.08g of silver (A = 108, n = 1) using 0.5A?
t = (m x n x F)/(A x I) = (1.08 x 1 x 96500)/(108 x 0.5) = 1930 seconds
Industrial Applications of Electrolysis

1. Electroplating

Depositing a thin layer of metal onto an object using electrolysis. The object to be plated is the cathode; the plating metal is the anode.

Example: Silver-plating a spoon: spoon = cathode, silver = anode, silver nitrate solution = electrolyte. Silver dissolves from anode and deposits on spoon (cathode).
  • Uses: Jewellery (gold/silver plating), cutlery, car parts (chromium plating for corrosion resistance), tin cans

2. Extraction of Metals (Electrolytic Reduction)

  • Aluminium: Extracted from molten bauxite (Al2O3) dissolved in molten cryolite. Al deposited at cathode; O2 at anode (burns carbon electrodes).
  • Sodium: Extracted from molten NaCl (Downs Cell). Na at cathode; Cl2 at anode.

3. Purification of Metals

Impure copper (anode) dissolves and pure copper deposits on cathode in CuSO4 solution. Impurities fall as anode sludge.

4. Manufacture of Chemicals

  • Chlorine and NaOH from brine (Chlor-alkali industry)
  • Hydrogen gas production
Electrochemical Cells

Simple Cell (Voltaic/Galvanic Cell)

A device that converts chemical energy into electrical energy through a spontaneous redox reaction.

  • Anode (negative): More reactive metal - oxidation (loses electrons)
  • Cathode (positive): Less reactive metal - reduction (gains electrons)
  • Electrolyte: Solution allowing ion flow
  • Salt bridge: Allows ion flow between half-cells to complete the circuit

The Daniell Cell

Zinc-Copper Cell:
Anode (Zn): Zn rarr Zn2+ + 2e- (oxidation)
Cathode (Cu): Cu2+ + 2e- rarr Cu (reduction)
Overall: Zn + Cu2+ rarr Zn2+ + Cu
EMF ≈ 1.1V

Electrochemical Series

Metals arranged in order of their tendency to lose electrons (reactivity). The greater the difference in position, the higher the cell voltage.

K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Ni > Sn > Pb > H > Cu > Ag > Au (most to least reactive)
Corrosion and Its Prevention

Rusting of Iron

Rusting is the corrosion of iron in the presence of both oxygen and water to form hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust).

4Fe + 3O2 + 6H2O rarr 2Fe2O3.3H2O (rust)

Rust is an electrochemical process: iron acts as anode, impurities as cathode, water as electrolyte.

Factors That Speed Up Rusting

  • Presence of salt (electrolyte) in water
  • Acid conditions (lower pH)
  • Contact with a less reactive metal (increases galvanic cell action)
