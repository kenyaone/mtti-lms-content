🌱 Introduction to Reproduction

What is Reproduction?

The process by which living organisms give rise to new members of their own species which resemble the parents.

Importance of Reproduction

  • Ensures continuity of the species
  • Maintains life of species
  • Replaces dead individuals
FeatureSexual ReproductionAsexual Reproduction
GametesFusion of male & female gametesNo gametes involved
ParentsTwo parents (usually)One parent only
VariationGenetic variation (crossing over)No genetic variation
Offspring4 haploid cells produced (meiosis)2 identical cells (mitosis)
AdvantagesVariation; stronger offspring; greater adaptabilityFaster; retains useful traits; shorter lifecycle
DisadvantagesLess certain; sex-linked diseases transmittedNo variation; lowered disease resistance

Methods of Asexual Reproduction

  • Sporulation: Formation of spores (small haploid cells). Occurs in moulds, ferns, bryophytes, pteridophytes.
  • Budding: Outgrowth from parent drops off and develops into new organism. Occurs in Hydra, jellyfish, yeast, some fungi.
  • Binary fission: Cell splits into 2 equal cells. Occurs in Amoeba, Euglena, Paramecium, bacteria.

🔬 Mitosis — Cell Division for Growth

What is Mitosis?

A type of cell division that occurs during growth, leading to an increase in cell number. All cells maintain the same chromosome number — the diploid (2n) state.

Significance: Ensures each daughter cell has the same number and types of chromosomes as the parent cell; responsible for growth and tissue repair.

Interphase (Resting phase)

  • Replication of organelles
  • Duplication of DNA
  • Production of energy (ATP) for cell division
  • Cell is preparing for division but appears normal

Prophase

  • Stage of dehydration (chromatin condenses)
  • Chromosomes shorten and thicken (become visible)
  • Each chromosome replicates into two chromatids
  • Chromatids joined at the centromere
  • Formation of spindle fibres
  • Nuclear membrane disintegrates

Metaphase

  • Chromosomes move to the equator (early metaphase)
  • Chromosomes line up at the equatorial plate
  • Homologous chromosomes do NOT associate
  • Spindle fibres attach to centromeres

Anaphase

  • Centromeres split
  • Chromatids separate
  • Sister chromatids move to opposite poles of the cell
  • Pulled by spindle fibres

Telophase

  • Chromatids reach the poles
  • Nuclear membrane reforms around each set of chromosomes
  • Cytokinesis — cytoplasm divides
  • Two identical diploid daughter cells are formed

🧬 Meiosis — Reduction Division

What is Meiosis?

Division of diploid cells (2n) to form gametes which are haploid (n). It involves TWO successive divisions producing 4 haploid daughter cells.

Significance: Gives rise to gametes; source of genetic variation through crossing over.

First Meiotic Division

  • Interphase I: Cell in non-dividing state; chromosomes thread-like
  • Prophase I: Chromosomes shorten & thicken; homologous chromosomes pair (synapsis); point of contact = chiasma (crossing over occurs here → variation)
  • Metaphase I: Paired homologous chromosomes line up at equator
  • Anaphase I: Homologous pairs separate and move to poles
  • Telophase I: Two new haploid nuclei form

Second Meiotic Division

  • Prophase II: Chromosomes shorten and thicken again (stage of dehydration)
  • Metaphase II: Chromosomes move to equator
  • Anaphase II: Chromatids of each chromosome separate to poles
  • Telophase II: Four haploid daughter cells formed

End result: 4 haploid cells (gametes)

🎯 Apply Your Knowledge

📋 Scenario: Cancer and Cell Division

Cancer is characterised by uncontrolled cell division. Cells divide rapidly without following the normal cell cycle, invading surrounding tissues.

Q1: Cancer cells divide by mitosis. Explain why cancer cells can form tumours (large masses of cells) if left untreated.
✅ Model: In normal mitosis, cell division is controlled — cells divide only when needed (for growth or repair) and stop dividing when they reach a normal population size (contact inhibition). Cancer cells have lost this control mechanism and undergo uncontrolled mitosis continuously. Each cancer cell divides into 2, then 4, 8, 16... exponentially, forming a growing mass called a tumour. Because mitosis produces genetically identical cells, all tumour cells carry the same abnormal genetic information, and the tumour grows rapidly.
Q2: During meiosis, crossing over can produce new combinations of genes. Explain the biological significance of this for a species' survival.
✅ Model: Crossing over (at the chiasma during Prophase I of meiosis) causes exchange of segments between homologous chromosomes, producing new combinations of alleles in gametes. This creates genetic variation in offspring. Variation is important for survival because: (1) In a changing environment, some variants may be better adapted to survive; (2) Natural selection acts on this variation — well-adapted individuals survive and reproduce; (3) A genetically diverse population is less likely to be wiped out by a single disease or environmental change (greater resilience); (4) It drives evolution over time.

🔬 Sexual Reproduction in Plants & Flowers

What is a Flower?

The reproductive structure of a plant that bears male and female parts. It produces seeds and fruits.

PartFunction
ReceptacleExpanded end of stalk bearing all floral parts
Calyx (Sepals)Usually green; protect flower in bud
Corolla (Petals)Often coloured/scented to attract insects
Androecium (stamens)Male part; anther produces pollen grains (male gametes)
Gynaecium (pistil)Female part; ovary → ovules (female gametes); style; stigma

Types of Flowers

  • Hermaphrodite/Bisexual: Has both stamen and carpel
  • Unisexual: Either male (staminate) or female (carpelate/pistilate)
  • Monoecious: Male and female flowers on the same plant (e.g. maize)
  • Dioecious: Male and female flowers on different plants (e.g. pawpaw)
  • Complete flower: Has all four parts (calyx, corolla, androecium, gynoecium)

✏️ Practice Questions

1. Describe in detail what happens during Prophase I of meiosis that does NOT happen during Prophase of mitosis.
✅ During Prophase I of meiosis (but NOT in mitosis): (1) Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) forming bivalents; (2) Each bivalent consists of 4 chromatids; (3) Non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes may exchange segments at points called chiasmata — this is crossing over, the source of genetic variation; (4) Bivalents (paired chromosomes) are visible, not just single chromosomes as in mitosis.
2. A cell has 46 chromosomes (2n=46). How many chromosomes will be in each daughter cell after: (a) mitosis, and (b) meiosis II?
✅ (a) After mitosis: 46 chromosomes in each daughter cell (diploid; mitosis maintains chromosome number). (b) After meiosis II: 23 chromosomes in each cell (haploid; meiosis halves the chromosome number). Meiosis produces 4 cells each with 23 chromosomes from 1 cell with 46 chromosomes.
3. State THREE differences between sexual and asexual reproduction.
✅ Sexual vs Asexual: (1) Sexual involves fusion of gametes (fertilisation); asexual does not use gametes — parts of organism develop into new individuals. (2) Sexual produces genetic variation (crossing over during meiosis); asexual produces genetically identical offspring (clones). (3) Sexual requires two parents (usually); asexual requires only one parent. (4) Sexual produces fewer offspring; asexual can produce many offspring rapidly.
