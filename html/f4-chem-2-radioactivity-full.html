Introduction to Radioactivity

Definition

Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of radiation from an unstable atomic nucleus. Discovered by Henri Becquerel (1896); studied by Marie and Pierre Curie.

Nuclear instability occurs when the neutron-to-proton ratio is too high or too low. The nucleus emits radiation to reach a more stable configuration.

Key Terms

  • Radioisotope: An unstable isotope that undergoes radioactive decay
  • Activity: The rate of radioactive decay (unit: Becquerel, Bq)
  • Background radiation: Low-level radiation always present in the environment (cosmic rays, radon gas, rocks, medical sources)

Nuclear Equations

Balancing Rules

  • Mass number (top) and atomic number (bottom) must balance on both sides
  • Alpha decay: mass number -4, atomic number -2
  • Beta decay: mass number unchanged, atomic number +1
  • Gamma: no change in mass or atomic number

Alpha Decay Examples

238/92 U rarr 234/90 Th + 4/2 He (Uranium-238 to Thorium-234 + alpha)
226/88 Ra rarr 222/86 Rn + 4/2 He (Radium-226 to Radon-222 + alpha)

Beta Decay Examples

14/6 C rarr 14/7 N + 0/-1 e (Carbon-14 to Nitrogen-14 + beta)
32/15 P rarr 32/16 S + 0/-1 e (Phosphorus-32 to Sulphur-32 + beta)
In beta decay: a neutron converts to a proton + electron: n rarr p + beta
Half-Life

Definition

The half-life (t1/2) is the time for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. It is constant for a given isotope and independent of temperature, pressure or chemical state.

Calculations

After n half-lives: Amount remaining = Initial amount x (1/2)^n

Example 1: 64g sample, t1/2 = 8 days. Mass after 32 days?
n = 32/8 = 4 half-lives. Remaining = 64 x (1/2)^4 = 64/16 = 4g
Example 2: 80g reduced to 10g. How many half-lives?
80 rarr 40 rarr 20 rarr 10 = 3 half-lives

Nuclear Fission and Fusion

Nuclear Fission

A heavy nucleus splits into two smaller nuclei + neutrons + large amounts of energy.

235/92 U + neutron rarr 141/56 Ba + 92/36 Kr + 3 neutrons + energy
  • Released neutrons trigger more fissions: chain reaction
  • Nuclear reactor: controlled chain reaction for electricity generation
  • Atomic bomb: uncontrolled chain reaction

Nuclear Fusion

Two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing enormous energy.

2/1 H + 3/1 H rarr 4/2 He + neutron + energy
  • Requires ~10 million degrees Celsius to occur
  • The Sun's energy source
  • Very little radioactive waste produced compared to fission

Uses and Hazards of Radioactivity

Beneficial Uses

Hazards

  • Ionising radiation damages DNA - can cause cancer and mutations
  • High doses cause radiation sickness, organ failure, death
  • Long-lived radioactive waste remains dangerous for thousands of years

Safety Precautions

  • Store sources in lead-lined containers
  • Handle with tongs - never bare hands
  • Minimise exposure time; maximise distance from source
  • Wear dosimeters (film badges) to monitor dose received
  • Lead aprons, thick concrete shielding in reactors

Worked Example - Half-life:
Initial = 100g, t1/2 = 5 years, time = 20 years.
n = 20/5 = 4 half-lives.
Remaining = 100 x (1/2)^4 = 100/16 = 6.25g
