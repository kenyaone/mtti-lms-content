🌍 What is Evolution?
🌍 Evolution: A gradual change in living organisms from simple life forms to more complex forms over a long period of time.

Theories of Origin of Life

Organic Evolution

Present forms of organisms emerged gradually from pre-existing forms (some of which no longer exist). Organisms changed over millions of years.

Chemical Evolution

Life originated when simple chemical compounds reacted to form the simplest life forms. Explains how life first began from non-living chemicals.

Special Creation

Maintains that the whole universe and all living organisms came into being due to the act of a supernatural being. Not a scientific theory.

🔍 Evidence for Evolution

🦴 1. Fossils

Remains of organisms preserved in natural materials for millions of years. Show types of organisms that existed long ago and structural changes over time (e.g. human skull, horse leg).

🦴 2. Comparative Anatomy

Similar structures in different organisms suggest common ancestry. Key concepts: homologous structures, analogous structures, vestigial structures, adaptive radiation, convergence, divergence.

🐣 3. Comparative Embryology

Embryos of different animals appear very similar — shows relationship and common ancestry. e.g. vertebrate embryos all have gill slits early in development.

🩸 4. Comparative Serology

Biochemical and immunological comparisons of blood — antigen-antibody reactions, blood groups, Rh factor — show relatedness and common ancestry.

🗺️ 5. Geographical Distribution

Organisms differ by region due to continental drift (Pangea → present continents). Isolation led to different evolution patterns — e.g. marsupials in Australia, llamas in S. America.

🔬 6. Cell Biology (Cytology)

Similar cell structures and organelles (e.g. mitochondria) in all organisms point to a common ancestry.

🦴 Comparative Anatomy — Structural Evidence

Homologous Structures

🦴 Homologous structures: Have a common embryonic origin but are modified to perform different functions in different organisms.
Example: The pentadactyl limb — the forelimb of vertebrates (human arm, whale flipper, bat wing, horse leg) all share the same basic bone structure (humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, phalanges) but serve different functions.
🔑 Divergence / Adaptive Radiation: A basic structural form modified to serve different functions in different environments — e.g. beaks of Darwin's finches, birds' feet, vertebrate forelimbs.

Analogous Structures

🦋 Analogous structures: Different structures modified to perform a similar function but with NO common embryonic origin.
Example: Wings of birds and wings of insects — both used for flying but developed from completely different structures. Also: eyes of humans vs eyes of octopuses.
🔑 Convergence: Different organisms evolving similar structures to adapt to similar environments.

Vestigial Structures

🔻 Vestigial structures: Greatly reduced in size and have ceased to function — remnants of structures that were useful in ancestors.

📜 Theories of Evolution: Lamarck vs Darwin

🧪 Lamarck's Theory — "Use and Disuse"

  • Organisms develop characteristics in response to their environment
  • Use of a body part leads to its development; disuse leads to degeneration
  • These acquired characteristics are inherited by offspring
  • Example: Giraffes stretched their necks reaching for leaves → longer necks passed to offspring
❌ NOT accepted today
Why Lamarck's theory is rejected: Evidence does NOT support it. Acquired characteristics are NOT inherited — inherited characteristics are found only in reproductive cells (gametes), not in body cells that are changed by use/environment.

🌿 Darwin's Theory — "Natural Selection"

  • Variations arise spontaneously (by chance) in a population
  • Some variations give the organism an advantage in its environment
  • Advantaged organisms survive and reproduce ("survival of the fittest")
  • The favourable characteristic is inherited by offspring
  • Unfavourable variations lead to elimination from the population
✅ Accepted by biologists

🌿 Natural Selection
🌿 Natural Selection: A process where organisms with certain characteristics are favoured by the environment, tend to survive and produce viable offspring, while others not favoured are eliminated from subsequent generations.

How Natural Selection Works

  1. Organisms produce more offspring than can survive → struggle for survival
  2. Organisms in a population show natural variation
  3. Some variations give an advantage in the environment
  4. Advantaged organisms survive and reproduce ("survival of the fittest")
  5. Disadvantaged organisms are eliminated
  6. If the favourable character is inherited, offspring are better adapted
  7. As environment changes, survival value of characters may change

Advantages of Natural Selection

  • Eliminates disadvantageous characteristics from the population
  • Perpetuates advantageous characteristics
  • Allows better-adapted organisms to survive adverse environmental changes

Role of Sexual Reproduction in Evolution

Sexual reproduction is important in evolution because it:
  • Brings about useful variations through recombination of genes
  • Makes offspring better adapted for survival and more resistant to diseases
  • May lead to the origin of new species

Role of Mutation in Evolution

  • Mutations bring about heritable variation in a population
  • Advantageous mutations → organism competes better → new adapted species/varieties
  • Disadvantageous mutations → organism eliminated from population
  • Only mutations in gametes influence evolution (gametes form the new offspring)
🦋 Industrial Melanism — Peppered Moth
🦋 Industrial Melanism: A classic example of natural selection where the frequency of dark (melanic) forms of the peppered moth increased in polluted industrial areas.
🦋

Speckled White (Normal)

  • Camouflaged on lichen-covered bark
  • Protected from predatory birds
  • Predominated in unpolluted areas
  • In polluted areas — easily seen and eaten
🦋

Melanic Black (Mutant)

  • Arose by mutation from the white form
  • Camouflaged on soot-blackened bark
  • Selective advantage in industrial areas
  • Survived better where soot covered trees

The Full Story (Step by Step)

  1. Originally, speckled white moths predominated in unpolluted England — camouflaged on lichen-covered tree bark
  2. Industrial pollution caused tree barks to be blackened with soot
  3. White moths on blackened bark became visible to predatory birds and were eaten
  4. A black mutant appeared suddenly by mutation
  5. Black moths had a selective advantage on soot-covered bark — they survived and reproduced
  6. Over time, black moths became more common in industrial areas
  7. White moths remained abundant in unpolluted areas (rural/forest)
🔑 This demonstrates: Natural selection acts on existing variation. The environment did NOT cause the mutation — it simply selected for the already-existing black mutant which happened to be better camouflaged.

Proof that Evolution is Still Occurring

  • Bacteria developing resistance to antibiotics (e.g. penicillin-resistant bacteria)
  • Houseflies and mosquitoes becoming resistant to DDT
  • New bacterial strains through mutation and natural selection

✏️ Practice Questions

1. Distinguish between homologous and analogous structures. Give ONE example of each.

Homologous: Structures with the same embryonic origin but modified to perform different functions. e.g. The pentadactyl limb (human arm, whale flipper, bat wing — same bone arrangement, different uses).

Analogous: Structures with different origins but modified to perform the same function. e.g. Wings of birds and wings of insects (both for flying, but developed from completely different structures).

2. Explain why Lamarck's theory of evolution is not accepted by biologists today.

Lamarck's theory is rejected because:
1. Evidence does NOT support the idea that acquired characteristics are inherited.
2. Changes in body cells due to use/environment are NOT passed to offspring — only characteristics coded in gametes (reproductive cells) can be inherited.
3. Experiments have consistently failed to demonstrate inheritance of acquired characteristics.

3. Using the peppered moth example, explain how natural selection operates.

Before pollution: White/speckled moths were camouflaged on lichen-covered bark → predatory birds could not see them → they survived and reproduced. Black mutants were visible on light bark → eaten by birds → eliminated.

After industrial pollution: Tree bark turned black with soot. White moths became visible → eaten. Black moths were now camouflaged → survived and reproduced in greater numbers. The environment SELECTED the better-adapted variety — this is natural selection.

4. State THREE pieces of evidence that evolution is still occurring today.

1. Antibiotic resistance in bacteria — new strains of bacteria resistant to penicillin and other antibiotics have evolved through mutation and natural selection.
2. Pesticide resistance — houseflies and mosquitoes have become resistant to DDT.
3. Industrial melanism — the increase in black peppered moths in polluted areas represents ongoing natural selection.

5. Give FOUR vestigial structures found in humans and explain what they suggest about human evolution.

Vestigial structures in humans: Appendix (remnant of larger caecum in herbivore ancestors), coccyx (remnant of a tail), nictitating membrane (third eyelid), ear muscles (for moving ears, useful in ancestors).

These suggest humans share ancestry with other organisms in which these structures were functional — providing evidence that humans evolved from earlier, different life forms.
