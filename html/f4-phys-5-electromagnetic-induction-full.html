🔌 Electromagnetic Induction — Basics

Electromagnetism — interaction between electric current and magnetic field, producing induced e.m.f and induced current.

Key Experiment

  • Move wire upward in magnetic field → galvanometer deflects one way
  • Move wire downward → deflects opposite direction
  • Hold still or move horizontally → no deflection

E.m.f is induced due to relative motion between conductor and magnetic field.

SLIDE 3 OF 10

📜 Laws of Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's Law

"The induced e.m.f in a conductor is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linking the conductor."

Lenz's Law

"An induced current flows in such a direction that its magnetic effect opposes the change producing it."

Fleming's Right Hand Rule (Generator Rule)

First finger = direction of field
Thumb = direction of motion
Second finger = direction of induced current

Factors Affecting Magnitude of Induced E.M.F

  • Rate of relative motion (faster → larger e.m.f)
  • Strength of magnetic field (stronger → larger)
  • Length of conductor in the field (longer → larger)

⚙️ AC & DC Generators

AC Generator / Alternator

Armature (coil) rotates in magnetic field. Two slip rings + carbon brushes. Current max at 90°, min at 270°. Used in car alternators and hydroelectric plants.

DC Generator

Same as AC but uses commutator (split ring) instead of slip rings. After each 180° rotation, connections reverse so current flows in one direction.

Eddy Currents

Loops of current in metal conductors opposing their producing force. Reduced by laminating metal cores. Useful in moving-coil meter damping.

🔄 Mutual Induction & Transformers

Mutual induction — changing magnetic flux in one coil induces e.m.f in a nearby coil.

Transformer

Converts alternating voltage. Core of laminated soft iron. Primary coil (Np turns, Vp) and secondary coil (Ns turns, Vs).

Np / Ns = Vp / Vs = Is / Ip
Efficiency = (Ps / Pp) × 100% = (Is × Vs)/(Ip × Vp) × 100%

✏️ Worked Examples

Example 1 — Step-up Transformer

Primary: 200 turns, 240 V, 0.6 A. Secondary current: 0.1 A. Find Ns and Vs.

Ns = (Ip/Is) × Np = (0.6/0.1) × 200 = 1200 turns

Vs = (Ns/Np) × Vp = (1200/200) × 240 = 1440 V

Example 2 — Efficiency

Step-up transformer: Np=100, Ns=10000, Vp=12V, Ip=0.5A, efficiency=90%. Find Is.

Pp = Ip×Vp = 0.5×12 = 6 W

Ps = efficiency × Pp = 0.9×6 = 5.4 W

Vs = (Ns/Np)×Vp = 1200 V

Is = Ps/Vs = 5.4/1200 = 0.0045 A

🧪 Practice Problems

P1. A transformer has Np=500, Ns=50. If Vp=240V, find Vs and state type.

Vs = (50/500)×240 = 24 V — Step-down transformer

P2. Same transformer (Ip=0.1A). Find Is assuming 100% efficiency.

Np/Ns = Is/Ip → Is = Ip×Np/Ns = 0.1×500/50 = 1 A

P3. Why are transformer cores made of laminated soft iron?

Laminations reduce eddy currents. Soft iron magnetizes/demagnetizes easily, reducing hysteresis losses.
