Definitions of Acids & Bases

Arrhenius Theory

Acid: Produces H⁺ (or H₃O⁺) ions in water

HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻

Base: Produces OH⁻ ions in water

NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻

Brønsted-Lowry Theory

Acid: Proton (H⁺) donor


Base: Proton (H⁺) acceptor


More general — includes reactions without water

Properties of Acids

Physical Properties

  • Sour taste (e.g. lemon juice, vinegar)
  • Corrosive — damage skin and tissues
  • Turn blue litmus red
  • pH less than 7
  • Conduct electricity (contain ions)

Chemical Properties

Acid + Metal Oxide →

Salt + Water

Properties of Bases & Alkalis

Definitions

Base: A metal oxide or hydroxide that reacts with acids to form salt and water only.

Alkali: A base that is soluble in water (produces OH⁻ ions).

All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.

Physical Properties

  • Bitter taste
  • Soapy/slippery feel
  • Turn red litmus blue
  • pH greater than 7
  • Corrosive (strong bases)

Chemical Properties

  • React with acids (neutralisation)
  • React with ammonium salts → ammonia gas
  • React with some metals (amphoteric)
  • Precipitate metal hydroxides from solutions

Preparation of Salts

Methods of Preparing Salts

1. Neutralisation

Acid + Alkali → Salt + Water

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Used for soluble salts from strong acids and alkalis

2. Acid + Metal

Acid + Metal → Salt + H₂

H₂SO₄ + Zn → ZnSO₄ + H₂

Only reactive metals (Mg, Zn, Fe, Al)

3. Acid + Metal Oxide

Acid + Metal Oxide → Salt + Water

H₂SO₄ + CuO → CuSO₄ + H₂O

For insoluble bases

4. Precipitation

Two soluble salts → insoluble salt

BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄↓ + 2NaCl

For insoluble salts (BaSO₄, PbSO₄, AgCl)

Solubility Rules: All nitrates soluble. Most chlorides soluble (except AgCl, PbCl₂). Most sulphates soluble (except BaSO₄, PbSO₄, CaSO₄).

Water of Crystallisation & Special Properties

Water of Crystallisation

Some salts crystallise with a fixed number of water molecules in their crystal structure. These are called hydrated salts.

SaltFormulaWater molecules
Copper(II) sulphateCuSO₄·5H₂O5
Sodium carbonateNa₂CO₃·10H₂O10
Iron(II) sulphateFeSO₄·7H₂O7
Calcium sulphate (gypsum)CaSO₄·2H₂O2

Efflorescence

Hydrated salts that lose water of crystallisation to the atmosphere and form a white powder on their surface.

Example: Na₂CO₃·10H₂O → Na₂CO₃·H₂O + 9H₂O

Deliquescence

Salts that absorb moisture from the atmosphere and dissolve in it to form a solution.

Examples: CaCl₂, NaOH, MgCl₂, ZnCl₂

Hygroscopic Substances

Absorb moisture from air but do not dissolve. Used as drying agents (e.g. anhydrous CaCl₂, silica gel, P₂O₅).

Neutralisation & Titration

Neutralisation

The reaction between an acid and a base to form a salt and water only. The pH of the resulting solution is 7 (if strong acid + strong base).

H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)

Titration

A technique used to find the concentration of an acid or base by reacting it with a known concentration of the other.

  1. Fill burette with acid/base of known concentration
  2. Pipette known volume of the other into conical flask
  3. Add 2-3 drops of indicator
  4. Add from burette until endpoint (colour change)
  5. Record titre and calculate concentration

🔢 Titration Formula

C₁V₁/n₁ = C₂V₂/n₂

C = concentration (mol/L), V = volume (mL), n = mole ratio from equation
