Electrolytes & Non-Electrolytes

Key Definitions

Electrolysis — the decomposition of an ionic compound (electrolyte) by passing an electric current through it in molten or aqueous state.


Electrolyte — a substance that conducts electricity when molten or dissolved in water, and is decomposed in the process.


Non-electrolyte — a substance that does not conduct electricity in any state (e.g. sugar, ethanol).

✅ Strong Electrolytes

  • HCl (hydrochloric acid)
  • H₂SO₄ (sulphuric acid)
  • NaOH (sodium hydroxide)
  • NaCl (sodium chloride)
  • CuSO₄ (copper sulphate)

❌ Non-Electrolytes

  • C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose/sugar)
  • C₂H₅OH (ethanol)
  • Distilled water
  • Urea
  • Paraffin oil
Note: Pure water is a very weak electrolyte. Tap water conducts slightly due to dissolved salts.

Electrolysis — Setup & Terminology

⚡ Anode (+)

Connected to positive terminal. Anions (negative ions) move here and are oxidised (lose electrons).

⚡ Cathode (−)

Connected to negative terminal. Cations (positive ions) move here and are reduced (gain electrons).

Memory tip: Anode = Oxidation, Cathode = Reduction → AOCROC (An Ox, A Cat Reduces!)

Electrolysis of Dilute Sulphuric Acid

Using platinum electrodes and dilute H₂SO₄ solution:

Ions present: H⁺, SO₄²⁻, OH⁻ (from water)

Cathode (−) — Reduction

H⁺ ions are discharged:

4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂↑

Hydrogen gas produced

Anode (+) — Oxidation

OH⁻ ions discharged (SO₄²⁻ not discharged):

4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂↑ + 4e⁻

Oxygen gas produced

📊 Volume Ratio

Volume of H₂ : Volume of O₂ = 2 : 1

This is because 4 moles of H₂ form 2 moles H₂, while 4 moles OH⁻ form only 1 mole O₂

Overall equation: 2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)

Electrolysis of Copper(II) Sulphate Solution

With Platinum Electrodes

Cathode: Cu²⁺ discharged (deposited)

Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Anode: OH⁻ discharged

4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂↑ + 4e⁻

Cathode: pink copper deposit. Anode: oxygen gas

With Copper Electrodes

Cathode: Cu²⁺ deposited

Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Anode: Copper dissolves

Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻

Anode loses mass, cathode gains mass. Solution concentration unchanged.

Application: Copper electrodes in CuSO₄ is the basis of electroplating and copper refining.

Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

First Law

The mass of a substance deposited or liberated at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge) passed through the electrolyte.

m ∝ Q    where Q = It

Second Law

When the same quantity of electricity passes through different electrolytes, the masses of substances deposited are proportional to their chemical equivalents (molar mass ÷ valency).

Electroplating & Industrial Applications

Electroplating

Coating a metal object with a thin layer of another metal using electrolysis.

Setup:

  • Object to plate = cathode
  • Plating metal = anode
  • Salt of plating metal = electrolyte

Uses:

  • Prevent corrosion (tin cans)
  • Decoration (jewellery)
  • Hardening surfaces (chrome)
  • Electrical conductivity

Extraction of Aluminium

Aluminium is extracted from bauxite (Al₂O₃) by electrolysis of molten aluminium oxide mixed with cryolite (lowers melting point).


Cathode: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al

Anode: 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻

Purification of Copper

Impure copper = anode, pure copper = cathode, CuSO₄ solution = electrolyte.


Impurities fall as anode sludge. Pure copper deposits on cathode.

Preferential Discharge of Ions

When a mixture of ions is present, not all are discharged equally. The order of preference depends on:

  • Position in the electrochemical series
  • Concentration of ions
  • Nature of electrode (active vs inert)

Cations — Cathode Order

(Most readily discharged first)

  1. Ag⁺ (silver)
  2. Cu²⁺ (copper)
  3. H⁺ (hydrogen)
  4. Zn²⁺ (zinc)
  5. Al³⁺ (aluminium)
  6. Na⁺ (sodium)

Anions — Anode Order

(Most readily discharged first)

  1. I⁻ (iodide)
  2. Br⁻ (bromide)
  3. Cl⁻ (chloride)
  4. OH⁻ (hydroxide)
  5. SO₄²⁻ (sulphate)
  6. NO₃⁻ (nitrate)
Key rule: In dilute solutions, OH⁻ is discharged at the anode. In concentrated halide solutions, the halide ion (Cl⁻, Br⁻) is preferentially discharged.
