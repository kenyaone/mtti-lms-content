🔬 What is Classification?

Binomial Nomenclature

A scientific system of naming organisms using two names — the generic (genus) name and the specific (species) name.

  • Scientific names must be in Latin or latinised
  • Family names are formed by adding the suffix "-idae" to the stem of the genus (e.g. Rana → Ranidae)
  • Generic names must be a single unique name
  • Written in italics or underlined, with genus capitalised and species in lowercase

Taxonomic Hierarchy (King Philip Came Over For Good Soup)

Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

Example: Homo sapiens — Genus: Homo, Species: sapiens

👑 The Five Kingdoms

🦠 Monera
e.g. Bacteria
  • Unicellular
  • Prokaryotic
  • Cell wall (no cellulose)
  • Lack most organelles
  • Microscopic
🔵 Protista
e.g. Protozoa, Algae
  • Unicellular
  • Eukaryotic
  • Cell walls (no cellulose)
  • Usually microscopic
🍄 Fungi
e.g. Mucor, Rhizopus
  • Have hyphae (form mycelia)
  • No chlorophyll
  • Have rhizoids
  • Have sporangia
🌿 Plantae
e.g. Ferns, Flowering plants
  • Contain chlorophyll
  • Autotrophic
  • Cellulose cell walls
  • Indefinite growth
🐾 Animalia
e.g. Insects, Mammals
  • No cell walls
  • Locomotion
  • Heterotrophic
  • Fast response to stimuli

💰 Economic Importance of Fungi & Bacteria

🍄 Fungi — Beneficial

  • Food: mushrooms, yeast (baking/brewing)
  • Antibiotics: penicillin, streptomycin
  • Vitamin B source
  • Nutrient recycling (decomposers)
  • Mycorrhizal association (water absorption)
  • Nitrogen fixation

🍄 Fungi — Harmful

  • Food decay
  • Diseases: ringworms
  • Crop diseases: late blight

🦠 Bacteria — Beneficial

  • Antibiotics manufacture
  • Silage, cheese, butter, yoghurt
  • Curing tea, tobacco
  • Vitamins B12 and K
  • Enzymes (amylase, invertase)
  • Insulin production
  • Biogas production
  • Sewage treatment
  • Nitrogen fixation (Rhizobium)

🦠 Bacteria — Harmful

  • Diseases in humans & animals
  • Food spoilage
  • Denitrifying bacteria reduce soil fertility

Rules for constructing a Dichotomous Key

  • Use observable characteristics only
  • Start with major characteristics
  • Use a single characteristic at a time
  • Use contrasting characteristics at each stage
  • Avoid repeating the same characteristics

🎯 Apply Your Knowledge

📋 Scenario: Field Trip to Karura Forest

During a biology field trip, students collected specimens labeled A–D:

  • A: An organism with no nucleus membrane, unicellular, found in soil
  • B: A green plant with fronds bearing sori on the underside
  • C: An organism with hyphae, no chlorophyll, found on rotting wood
  • D: A small animal with 6 legs, 3 body parts, and one pair of wings
Q1: Classify specimens A–D into their correct kingdoms. Give one reason for each classification.
✅ Model answer: A = Monera (prokaryotic, no nuclear membrane); B = Plantae (fronds with sori = fern/Pteridophyta); C = Fungi (hyphae, no chlorophyll, decomposes organic matter); D = Animalia/Insecta (6 legs, 3 body parts, wings = insect class)
Q2: What class does specimen D belong to in Phylum Arthropoda? Give TWO distinguishing characteristics.
✅ Model answer: Class Insecta. Distinguishing characteristics: (i) Three body parts — head, thorax, abdomen; (ii) Three pairs of legs (six legs); (iii) One pair of antennae; (iv) Compound eyes.
Slide 9 • Practice Questions

✏️ Practice

1. Differentiate between a monocot and a dicot plant. Give THREE differences.
✅ Model answer: Monocot: one seed leaf, parallel venation, fibrous root system, no secondary growth, narrow leaves. Dicot: two seed leaves, network venation, tap root system, secondary growth occurs, broad leaves. (Accept any 3 points)
2. State FOUR economic benefits of bacteria to humans.
✅ Model answer: (i) Manufacture of antibiotics; (ii) Fermentation of cheese, butter, milk, yoghurt; (iii) Production of vitamins B12 and K; (iv) Biogas production; (v) Sewage treatment; (vi) Nitrogen fixation increases soil fertility; (vii) Production of enzymes (amylase) and hormones (insulin)
3. Explain why Gymnosperms and Angiosperms are both classified under Spermatophyta.
✅ Model answer: Both Gymnosperms and Angiosperms are seed-bearing plants (spermatophytes). They both: are photosynthetic, have well-differentiated roots/stems/leaves, and have a well-developed vascular system. The key distinction is that gymnosperm seeds are naked (exposed) while angiosperm seeds are enclosed in fruits.
