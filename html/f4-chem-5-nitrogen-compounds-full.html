🌬️ Nitrogen Gas (N₂)

Occurrence in Nature

Nitrogen makes up approximately 78% of the atmosphere by volume. It also occurs in proteins, nucleic acids (DNA/RNA), and in many minerals such as saltpetre (KNO₃) and Chile saltpetre (NaNO₃).

Physical Properties of N₂

State & Appearance

Colourless, odourless gas at room temperature. Slightly less dense than air.

Solubility & Boiling Point

Slightly soluble in water. Boiling point: −196°C (liquid nitrogen used in cryogenics).

Chemical Properties

PropertyDetail
ReactivityGenerally unreactive due to very strong N≡N triple bond (bond energy = 945 kJ/mol)
With oxygenReacts only at very high temperatures / lightning: N₂ + O₂ → 2NO
With hydrogenReacts under industrial conditions (Haber Process): N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃
Supporter of combustionDoes NOT support combustion — extinguishes flames

Laboratory Preparation

Heating ammonium nitrite (or ammonium dichromate) NH₄NO₂ → N₂ + 2H₂O

⚠️ Note

The gas is collected over water. Impurities (O₂, CO₂) are removed using alkaline pyrogallol and KOH solution respectively.

Industrial Extraction

Nitrogen is obtained industrially by fractional distillation of liquefied air. Air is compressed, cooled, and allowed to evaporate. Nitrogen (bp −196°C) evaporates before oxygen (bp −183°C) and argon (bp −186°C).

🏭 The Haber Process

What is the Haber Process?

An industrial process developed by Fritz Haber (1909) for the synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen. It is the foundation of modern agriculture through fertiliser production.

Raw Materials

Nitrogen (N₂)

Obtained from fractional distillation of liquid air

Hydrogen (H₂)

Obtained from steam reforming of natural gas (methane + steam over nickel catalyst)

The Reaction

Reversible Reaction (Equilibrium) N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = −92 kJ/mol

Optimum Conditions & Le Chatelier's Principle

FactorChosen ConditionReason (Le Chatelier)
Temperature450°C (compromise)Low T favours forward reaction but rate too slow; 450°C balances rate and yield (~15%)
Pressure200 atmHigh P favours side with fewer moles of gas (products: 2 mol vs reactants: 4 mol); higher P gives more NH₃
CatalystIron (Fe) with promoters Al₂O₃, K₂OIncreases rate without affecting equilibrium position
N₂:H₂ ratio1:3Stoichiometric ratio for maximum efficiency

Why Not Higher Temperature?

The reaction is exothermic (ΔH = −92 kJ/mol). By Le Chatelier's Principle, increasing temperature shifts equilibrium LEFT (toward reactants), reducing NH₃ yield. 450°C is a compromise between rate and yield.

🧪 Properties of Ammonia (NH₃)

Physical Properties

Appearance

Colourless gas with a pungent, choking smell

Density

Less dense than air (lighter than air). Collected by downward delivery.

Solubility

Very soluble in water — demonstrated by the fountain experiment

Chemical Properties

1. Basic Nature — Reaction with Water

NH₃(g) + H₂O(l) ⇌ NH₄⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

NH₃ is a weak base. It turns red litmus blue and has pH ~11.

2. Reaction with Acids (Salt Formation)

NH₃ + HCl → NH₄Cl
(dense white fumes)
NH₃ + HNO₃ → NH₄NO₃
(ammonium nitrate — fertiliser)
2NH₃ + H₂SO₄ → (NH₄)₂SO₄
(ammonium sulphate — fertiliser)

3. Combustion

4NH₃ + 3O₂ → 2N₂ + 6H₂O
(burns with a pale yellow flame)

4. Catalytic Oxidation (industrial — Ostwald)

4NH₃ + 5O₂ → 4NO + 6H₂O (Pt catalyst, 900°C)

5. Reaction with Copper(II) Oxide

2NH₃ + 3CuO → 3Cu + N₂ + 3H₂O
(NH₃ acts as a reducing agent; black CuO → red Cu)

Uses of Ammonia

Industrial Uses

  • Making nitrogenous fertilisers (urea, ammonium nitrate)
  • Manufacturing nitric acid (Ostwald Process)
  • Making nylon and other polymers
  • Refrigerant in industrial cooling

Domestic Uses

  • Cleaning agents and household cleaners
  • Smelling salts (ammonium carbonate)
  • Manufacture of explosives (TNT)

⚗️ Nitric Acid & The Ostwald Process

The Ostwald Process

Industrial manufacture of nitric acid (HNO₃) from ammonia, developed by Wilhelm Ostwald (1902). It has three main stages.

Stage 1: Catalytic Oxidation of Ammonia

4NH₃(g) + 5O₂(g) → 4NO(g) + 6H₂O(g)
Platinum-Rhodium gauze catalyst, 900°C, 4–10 atm

Stage 2: Oxidation of NO to NO₂

2NO(g) + O₂(g) → 2NO₂(g)
(Cooled gases; no catalyst needed — exothermic)

Stage 3: Absorption of NO₂ in Water

4NO₂(g) + O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) → 4HNO₃(aq)
(Gives ~60% nitric acid; concentration by distillation)

Key Reactions of NO₂

Reaction with water

3NO₂ + H₂O → 2HNO₃ + NO

Dimerisation equilibrium

2NO₂(g) ⇌ N₂O₄(g) ΔH = −57 kJ/mol
(cooling favours N₂O₄; heating favours NO₂)

Environmental Impact of Nitrogen Oxides

Acid Rain

NO₂ + water vapour in the atmosphere forms nitric acid, contributing to acid rain that damages ecosystems and buildings.

4NO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4HNO₃

Photochemical Smog

NOₓ from vehicle exhaust reacts with hydrocarbons in sunlight to produce ground-level ozone and toxic brown haze.

Test for NO₂

NO₂ turns moist starch-iodide paper blue (oxidises I⁻ to I₂ which reacts with starch). It also turns moist blue litmus paper red.

🌿 Nitrogenous Fertilisers

Why Nitrogen Fertilisers?

Nitrogen is essential for plant growth (proteins, chlorophyll, nucleic acids). Natural soil nitrogen is often insufficient for high-yield agriculture. Synthetic fertilisers supplement it.

Common Nitrogen Fertilisers

FertiliserFormula% N by massNotes
UreaCO(NH₂)₂46%Highest N content; breaks down to NH₃ + CO₂ in soil
Ammonium nitrateNH₄NO₃35%Fast-acting; can be explosive if impure
Ammonium sulphate(NH₄)₂SO₄21%Acidifies soil; used in acidic-loving crops
Calcium ammonium nitrateNH₄NO₃·CaCO₃26%Common in Kenya; neutral pH effect
Diammonium phosphate(NH₄)₂HPO₄21%Also provides phosphorus

Manufacture of Key Fertilisers

Ammonium Nitrate

NH₃(g) + HNO₃(aq) → NH₄NO₃(aq)

Ammonia (Haber) + Nitric acid (Ostwald) are neutralised. Solution concentrated by evaporation.

Ammonium Sulphate

2NH₃ + H₂SO₄ → (NH₄)₂SO₄

Used on tea and coffee plantations which prefer slightly acidic soil.

Disadvantages of Excessive Fertiliser Use

Eutrophication

Nitrates leach into lakes/rivers → algal bloom → oxygen depletion → death of aquatic life (dead zones).

Soil Acidification

Ammonium-based fertilisers release H⁺ in soil, lowering pH and reducing crop yields over time.

Water Pollution

Nitrates in drinking water cause methaemoglobinaemia (blue baby syndrome) in infants. WHO limit: 50 mg/L NO₃⁻.

Greenhouse Gases

Denitrification produces N₂O — a potent greenhouse gas (300× more warming than CO₂ over 100 years).

♻️ The Nitrogen Cycle

What is the Nitrogen Cycle?

The nitrogen cycle is the biogeochemical cycle that describes how nitrogen is converted between its various chemical forms as it circulates through the atmosphere, terrestrial, and aquatic ecosystems.

Figure: The Nitrogen Cycle showing fixation, nitrification, denitrification, and assimilation pathways

Key Processes in the Nitrogen Cycle

ProcessTransformationOrganisms / Agent
N₂ FixationN₂ → NH₃ / NH₄⁺Rhizobium (legumes), Azotobacter (free-living), lightning
AmmonificationOrganic N → NH₄⁺Decomposer bacteria & fungi on dead matter
NitrificationNH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻Nitrosomonas (NH₄⁺→NO₂⁻), Nitrobacter (NO₂⁻→NO₃⁻)
AssimilationNO₃⁻/NH₄⁺ → proteinsPlants absorb from soil via roots
DenitrificationNO₃⁻ → N₂ (+ N₂O)Pseudomonas, Thiobacillus (anaerobic conditions)

Question 6 — Short Answer [3 marks]

Explain why a temperature of 450°C is used in the Haber Process rather than a very low or very high temperature. Reference both rate and equilibrium in your answer.

Model Answer: The reaction N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ is exothermic. A very low temperature would shift equilibrium to the right (favouring products) giving high yield, but the rate of reaction would be very slow. A very high temperature gives a fast rate but shifts equilibrium left, reducing NH₃ yield. 450°C is a compromise — it provides adequate reaction rate while maintaining a reasonable yield (~15%). The iron catalyst further improves the rate without affecting equilibrium.
Question 7 — Short Answer [3 marks]

Write balanced equations for all three stages of the Ostwald Process and state the conditions for Stage 1.

Model Answer:
Stage 1: 4NH₃ + 5O₂ → 4NO + 6H₂O (Pt/Rh catalyst, 900°C, 4–10 atm)
Stage 2: 2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂ (cooling, no catalyst)
Stage 3: 4NO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4HNO₃ (absorption tower)
Question 8 — Short Answer [2 marks]

What is eutrophication? Describe how nitrogenous fertilisers cause it and state one consequence for aquatic life.

Model Answer: Eutrophication is the process whereby excessive nutrients (especially nitrates and phosphates) enrich a water body. Nitrogenous fertilisers leach NO₃⁻ from agricultural land into rivers and lakes. This causes rapid algal bloom on the water surface. The algae block sunlight for submerged plants and, when the algae die, decomposers multiply using up dissolved oxygen. This leads to deoxygenation of the water and the death of fish and other aquatic organisms.
