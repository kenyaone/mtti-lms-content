What is Radioactivity?

Definition

Radioactivity is the spontaneous disintegration of unstable atomic nuclei with the emission of radiation (alpha, beta or gamma) and release of energy.

Key Points

  • Process is spontaneous — cannot be controlled by temperature, pressure or chemical state
  • Radioactive elements have unstable nuclei (too many/few neutrons)
  • Discovered by Henri Becquerel (1896) using uranium
  • Further studied by Marie & Pierre Curie (discovered radium and polonium)

Natural Radioactivity

Occurs naturally in heavy elements: Uranium (U), Radium (Ra), Thorium (Th), Polonium (Po), Radon (Rn)

Artificial Radioactivity

Induced by bombarding stable nuclei with particles (neutrons, protons) in nuclear reactors or accelerators.

Types of Radiation

⚛ Alpha (α) Radiation

  • Nature: Helium nucleus (²₄He) — 2 protons + 2 neutrons
  • Charge: +2
  • Mass: 4 atomic mass units
  • Penetration: Least — stopped by paper or few cm of air
  • Ionisation: Strongest ioniser
  • Deflection: Deflected by electric & magnetic fields

⚡ Beta (β) Radiation

  • Nature: Fast-moving electron (⁰₋₁e)
  • Charge: −1
  • Mass: Negligible (1/1836 of proton)
  • Penetration: Medium — stopped by 3mm aluminium
  • Ionisation: Moderate
  • Deflection: Deflected (opposite to alpha)

☢ Gamma (γ) Radiation

  • Nature: Electromagnetic wave (high energy photons)
  • Charge: 0 (neutral)
  • Mass: 0
  • Penetration: Most — requires several cm of lead or concrete
  • Ionisation: Weakest ioniser
  • Deflection: NOT deflected by fields

Nuclear Equations

Rules for Balancing Nuclear Equations

  • Sum of mass numbers must be equal on both sides
  • Sum of atomic numbers must be equal on both sides

Alpha Decay — loses ²₄He

Mass number decreases by 4, atomic number decreases by 2:

²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂He

Uranium-238 decays to Thorium-234 + alpha particle

Beta Decay — emits ⁰₋₁e

Mass number unchanged, atomic number increases by 1:

²³⁴₉₀Th → ²³⁴₉₁Pa + ⁰₋₁e

Thorium-234 decays to Protactinium-234 + beta particle

Gamma Emission

No change in mass or atomic number. Gamma rays accompany alpha or beta decay as excess energy is released.

Half-Life

Definition

Half-life (t½) is the time taken for half the atoms in a radioactive sample to decay.

Half-Life Formula

N = N₀ × (½)ⁿ    where n = t / t½
SymbolMeaning
NRemaining amount
N₀Initial amount
nNumber of half-lives
tTotal time elapsed
t½Half-life period

📊 Worked Example

A 80g sample has a half-life of 20 years. How much remains after 60 years?

n = 60 ÷ 20 = 3 half-lives

N = 80 × (½)³ = 80 × ⅛ = 10g

Note: Half-life is constant for a given isotope — it cannot be changed by any physical or chemical means.

Uses of Radioactivity

🏥 Medicine

  • Cancer treatment (radiotherapy)
  • Medical imaging (X-rays, PET scans)
  • Sterilising medical equipment
  • Iodine-131 for thyroid treatment
  • Cobalt-60 for tumour treatment

⚡ Energy

  • Nuclear power stations
  • Nuclear submarines
  • Radioisotope thermoelectric generators
  • Space probes (e.g. Voyager)

🔬 Research & Industry

  • Radiocarbon dating (Carbon-14)
  • Thickness measurement in industry
  • Food preservation (irradiation)
  • Leak detection in pipes
  • Smoke detectors (Americium-241)

⚠ Dangers

  • Causes cancer and mutations
  • Burns and radiation sickness
  • Damage to DNA
  • Long-term environmental contamination
  • Nuclear weapons

Nuclear Fission & Fusion

Nuclear Fission

The splitting of a heavy nucleus into two lighter nuclei with release of large amounts of energy and neutrons.

²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + ⁹²₃₆Kr + 3¹₀n + energy

Used in nuclear reactors and atomic bombs. Neutrons released cause a chain reaction.

Nuclear Fusion

The joining of light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus, releasing enormous amounts of energy.

²₁H + ³₁H → ⁴₂He + ¹₀n + energy

Source of energy in the sun and stars. Used in hydrogen bombs. Research ongoing for clean energy.

Safety Precautions & Detecting Radiation

⚠ Safety Precautions

  • Use lead-lined containers for storage
  • Wear lead aprons and gloves
  • Use remote-handling tongs
  • Minimise exposure time
  • Maximise distance from source
  • Regular monitoring with dosimeters
  • Dispose of waste in designated sites

🔍 Detecting Radiation

  • Geiger-Müller tube — most common detector
  • Photographic film — worn as badge dosimeter
  • Cloud chamber — shows tracks of radiation
  • Scintillation counter — flashes of light
  • Electroscope — measures ionisation
Background radiation is low-level radiation that exists naturally in the environment from rocks, soil, cosmic rays and radon gas. It must be subtracted when measuring radioactive sources.
