Introduction to Chlorine

Chlorine is a diatomic non-metallic element (Cl₂). Electron arrangement: 2.8.7 — belongs to the halogen family (Group VII).

⚠️ Chlorine is very poisonous. All experiments must be done in a fume cupboard.

Preparation and Collection of Chlorine

Lab Preparation: MnO₂ + HCl

Heat concentrated HCl with manganese(IV) oxide (oxidising agent):

MnO₂(s) + 4HCl(aq) → MnCl₂(aq) + 2H₂O(l) + Cl₂(g) [heat]

Purification:

  • Pass through water → removes HCl gas (very soluble in water)
  • Pass through conc. H₂SO₄ or anhydrous CaCl₂ → dries the gas
⚠️ First bottle MUST be water (to remove HCl), second must be conc. H₂SO₄ (to dry). NOT the other way around — gas would be made wet again by passing through H₂SO₄ first then water.

Collection:

  • Wet Cl₂: Over brine (saturated NaCl) or hot water (less soluble in hot/salt water)
  • Dry Cl₂: By downward delivery (2.5× denser than air)

Properties of Chlorine Gas

Physical Properties

  • Greenish-yellow gas; irritating pungent smell; attacks nose and lungs
  • 2.5× denser than air → collected by downward delivery
  • Fairly soluble in water → greenish-yellow chlorine water
Cl₂(g) + H₂O(l) → HCl(aq) + HOCl(aq) [chlorine water = HCl + hypochlorous acid]

Action on Litmus Paper & Bleaching

Moist chlorine: turns litmus paper red (acidic) then bleaches it

Dry chlorine: has NO effect on dry litmus paper (needs moisture to react)

Mechanism of bleaching:

  1. Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HOCl (hypochlorous acid)
  2. HOCl is a strong oxidising agent → oxidises the dye (adds O to it) → colourless product
Dye(coloured) + HOCl → HCl + {Dye+O} [colourless]
💡 Chlorine bleaches by oxidation — this is permanent. SO₂ bleaches by reduction — not permanent (original colour returns in air). Chlorine does NOT bleach printers ink (made of carbon).

Effects of Sunlight on Chlorine Water

Exposed to sunlight: HOCl decomposes → releases O₂:

2HOCl(aq) → 2HCl(aq) + O₂(g) [slow, in sunlight]

Overall: 2H₂O + 2Cl₂ → 4HCl + O₂ (in sunlight)

O₂ collected relights a glowing splint.

Reactions of Chlorine with Metals and Non-Metals

Reaction with Hot Metals

Chlorine reacts with hot metals to form metal chlorides. Where two chlorides exist, the higher chloride is formed (more stable):

Iron (iron(III) chloride — FeCl₃ not FeCl₂):

2Fe(s) + 3Cl₂(g) → 2FeCl₃(g) [red-brown fumes; black solid on cooling]

FeCl₃ sublimes — collected in cooler part of apparatus. Note: iron must be hot; chlorine must be dry.

Magnesium:

Mg(s) + Cl₂(g) → MgCl₂(s) [blinding white flame; white powder]

Aluminium chloride:

2Al(s) + 3Cl₂(g) → Al₂Cl₆(s) [also sublimes on heating]

Reactions with Non-Metals

With phosphorus:

P₄(s) + 6Cl₂(g) → 4PCl₃(s) [limited Cl₂; white fumes]
P₄(s) + 10Cl₂(g) → 4PCl₅(s) [excess Cl₂]

With hydrogen (explosive!):

Cl₂(g) + H₂(g) → 2HCl(g) [heat/UV light; explosive mixture]

HCl produced gives white fumes of NH₄Cl with NH₃ at the tube mouth.

With burning candle/turpentine (hydrocarbons):

Cl₂ combines with H from hydrocarbon → HCl; carbon (soot) left behind.

C₁₀H₁₆(l) + 8Cl₂(g) → 16HCl(g) + 10C(s) [black cloud of carbon]

Chlorine as Oxidising Agent & Reactions with Alkalis

Chlorine as Oxidising Agent

Chlorine readily accepts electrons — oxidises many substances while being reduced to Cl⁻:

With H₂S:

Cl₂(g) + H₂S(g) → 2HCl(g) + S(s) [yellow deposits; at least one gas must be moist]

With Na₂SO₃ → oxidises SO₃²⁻ to SO₄²⁻:

Cl₂(g) + Na₂SO₃(aq) + H₂O → Na₂SO₄(aq) + 2HCl(aq)

With NH₃ → oxidises N in NH₃ to N₂:

8NH₃(g) + 3Cl₂(g) → 6NH₄Cl(s) + N₂(g)

Displacement of Halogens

Cl₂ is a stronger oxidising agent than Br₂ and I₂ → displaces them from their salts:

SolutionObservationExplanation
KF (fluoride)No changeCl₂ weaker oxidising agent than F₂
KBr (bromide)Colourless → red-brownCl₂ displaces Br₂: 2KBr + Cl₂ → 2KCl + Br₂
KI (iodide)Colourless → blackCl₂ displaces I₂: 2KI + Cl₂ → 2KCl + I₂

Reactions with Alkalis

With cold dilute NaOH → sodium hypochlorite (bleach):

Cl₂(g) + 2NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + NaClO(aq) + H₂O(l) [pale yellow solution]

NaClO (sodium chlorate(I)) bleaches by oxidation.

With hot concentrated NaOH:

3Cl₂(g) + 6NaOH(conc,hot) → 5NaCl(aq) + NaClO₃(aq) + 3H₂O(l)

With slaked lime Ca(OH)₂ → bleaching powder:

Cl₂(g) + Ca(OH)₂(s) → CaOCl₂(s) + H₂O(l) [bleaching powder, CaOCl₂]

Bleaching powder smells of Cl₂ because CO₂ in air reacts with it:

CaOCl₂(s) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + Cl₂(g)

Industrial Manufacture of Chlorine – Mercury Cathode Cell

Electrolysis of Brine

Electrolyte: Brine (concentrated NaCl solution)

Electrodes: Carbon (graphite) anode; flowing mercury cathode

Ions present: Na⁺, Cl⁻, H⁺, OH⁻

ElectrodeReactionReason
Anode (+)2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻High concentration of Cl⁻ → preferentially discharged over OH⁻
Cathode (–)2Na⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → 2Na(s)Mercury cathode discharges Na⁺ preferentially over H⁺

Sodium amalgam formed: Na dissolves in flowing mercury → Na/Hg (sodium amalgam)

Amalgam + water:

2Na/Hg(l) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g) + 2Hg(l)

Mercury is recycled.

Products of Brine Electrolysis

  • Chlorine gas at the anode
  • Hydrogen gas and sodium hydroxide at the cathode
💡 All three products are commercially valuable: Cl₂ for water treatment and PVC; NaOH for soap; H₂ for ammonia (Haber process).

Uses of Chlorine and Its Compounds

  • Manufacture of HCl
  • Bleaching powder for textiles and paper
  • Sterilisation of drinking water and swimming pools
  • Sewage treatment
  • Manufacture of PVC (polyvinyl chloride)
  • Manufacture of germicides, pesticides, fungicides (DDT, CFCs)
  • CFCs as aerosol propellants and refrigerants (freons)
  • Manufacture of chloroform (aesthetic)

Hydrogen Chloride Gas (HCl)

Laboratory Preparation

Concentrated H₂SO₄ + NaCl, heat gently:

H₂SO₄(l) + NaCl(s) → NaHSO₄(s) + HCl(g)

Gas dried with conc. H₂SO₄. Collected by downward delivery (slightly denser than air).

💡 HCl gas is very soluble in water — when making aqueous HCl solution, use an inverted funnel arrangement to prevent "sucking back" (water rushing up tube into hot flask).

Properties of HCl Gas

  • Colourless; strong, irritating pungent smell
  • 1¼× denser than air → collected by downward delivery
  • Very soluble in water → fumes strongly in moist air (hydrochloric acid)
  • Solution (hydrochloric acid) is a strong acid — fully ionises: HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

Test for HCl gas: White fumes of NH₄Cl when brought near ammonia gas:

NH₃(g) + HCl(g) → NH₄Cl(s) [white fumes]

HCl decomposed by oxidising agents (gives Cl₂):

MnO₂(s) + 4HCl(g) → MnCl₂(s) + 2H₂O(l) + Cl₂(g)

Properties of Hydrochloric Acid (HCl in water)

With metals above H:

Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)

Neutralisation:

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
2HCl(aq) + CuO(s) → CuCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)

With carbonates:

CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

Note: Metals BELOW hydrogen (e.g. Cu) do NOT react with HCl.

Tests for Chlorides, HCl Manufacture & Pollution

Test for Chloride Ions (Cl⁻)

Test 1 – Silver ions:

Add AgNO₃ solution, then acidify with dil HNO₃:

NaCl(aq) + AgNO₃(aq) → NaNO₃(aq) + AgCl(s) [white precipitate]

White precipitate (AgCl) is insoluble in dil HNO₃ but dissolves in excess ammonia. AgCl turns violet in light.

Note: Ag₂CO₃ also forms white ppt but dissolves in dil HNO₃ → AgCl confirmed if precipitate persists.

Test 2 – Lead ions:

Add Pb(NO₃)₂, warm solution:

Pb²⁺(aq) + 2Cl⁻(aq) → PbCl₂(s) [white ppt; dissolves on warming, recrystallises on cooling]

Large-Scale Manufacture of HCl

Burn H₂ in excess Cl₂ (from brine electrolysis):

H₂(g) + Cl₂(g) → 2HCl(g)

HCl gas dissolved in water over glass beads (large surface area). Commercial HCl is ~35% pure.

⚠️ Equal volumes of H₂ and Cl₂ explode in sunlight! H₂ is burned in excess Cl₂ for safety.

Uses of HCl: Sewage treatment, water chlorination, removing rust from metals before galvanising/electroplating, making dyes, drugs, photographic materials.

Environmental Pollution by Chlorine Compounds

  • Chlorine: Dissolves in rain → acid rain; adverse effects on plants, animals, buildings, soil
  • CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons): Non-biodegradable; diffuse into atmosphere; break down to free Cl and F atoms → deplete ozone layer (greenhouse gas). DDT banned in Kenya (NEMA recommends pyrethroids).
  • PVCs: Non-biodegradable; accumulate as plastic waste
  • DDT: Long-lasting pesticide; affects plant and animal life

Q5. Explain with equations what happens when chlorine water is exposed to sunlight. State all observations.
Chlorine water contains HCl and HOCl (hypochlorous acid). On exposure to sunlight, HOCl is unstable and decomposes, releasing oxygen gas: 2HOCl(aq) → 2HCl(aq) + O₂(g). Observation: gas collects (colourless) that relights a glowing splint, confirming it is oxygen. The overall reaction is: 2H₂O(l) + 2Cl₂(g) → 4HCl(aq) + O₂(g) [in sunlight].
Q6. Describe with equations the reactions that occur at both electrodes during the electrolysis of brine in the mercury cathode cell. State all products.
At the anode (graphite): Cl⁻ ions are preferentially discharged (high concentration) producing chlorine gas: 2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻. At the cathode (mercury): Na⁺ ions are preferentially discharged (mercury cathode discharges Na⁺ over H⁺): 2Na⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → 2Na(s). Sodium dissolves in flowing mercury forming sodium amalgam (Na/Hg). This reacts with water: 2Na/Hg + 2H₂O → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g) + 2Hg(l). Mercury is recycled. Products: Cl₂ (at anode), H₂ and NaOH (from cathode reaction).
Q7. Compare the bleaching action of chlorine and sulphur(IV) oxide. State the mechanism and relative permanence of each.
Chlorine bleaches by oxidation: HOCl adds oxygen to the dye, destroying its colour permanently. The colour cannot be restored. SO₂ bleaches by reduction: sulphurous acid removes oxygen from the dye, making it colourless. This is NOT permanent — the original colour can be restored by oxidation or prolonged exposure to air. Chlorine is a stronger bleaching agent but SO₂ is preferred for delicate materials (wool, straw) because it is milder.
