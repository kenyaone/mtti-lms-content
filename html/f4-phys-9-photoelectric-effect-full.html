💡 Photoelectric Effect

Discovered by Heinrich Hertz in 1887.

Photoelectric effect — electrons emitted from the surface of a substance when certain EM radiation falls on it.

  • Metal surfaces require ultraviolet radiation
  • Caesium oxide requires visible light

Key Terms

TermDefinition
Work function (Φ)Minimum energy to remove an electron from the surface. Units: electron volts (eV). 1 eV = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J
Threshold frequency (fco)Minimum frequency that causes photoelectric effect. Φ = hfco
Cut-off potential (Vco)P.D. to stop fastest photoelectron. ½mv² = eVco
SLIDE 3 OF 10

⚡ Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

KEmax = ½mv²max = hf − Φ
hfco = Φ (at threshold)
eVco = hf − hfco

Planck's Quantum Theory

Energy is quantized: E = nhf

Einstein: light consists of photons with energy E = hf

Higher frequency → higher photon energy → more energetic photoelectrons.

Factors Affecting Photoelectric Effect

  • Intensity — rate of emission proportional to intensity
  • Work function — higher work function = harder to emit electrons
  • Frequency — cut-off potential proportional to frequency

✏️ Worked Examples

Example 1 — Cut-off Frequency

λ = 3.310×10⁻⁷ m. Find fco.

f = c/λ = 3×10⁸/3.310×10⁻⁷ = 9.06×10¹⁴ Hz

Example 2 — Tungsten

Tungsten: Φ=4.52 eV, λ=2.50×10⁻⁷ m. Find cut-off potential.

Ephoton = hf = hc/λ = 6.62×10⁻³⁴×3×10⁸/2.50×10⁻⁷ = 4.97 eV

eVco = 4.97−4.52 = 0.45 eV → Vco = 0.45 V

Example 3 — Sodium

Φ=2.0 eV, λ=150 nm. Find max KE and velocity.

Ephoton = hc/λ = 8.29 eV | KEmax = 8.29−2.0 = 6.29 eV = 1.01×10⁻¹⁸ J

vmax = √(2KE/m) = 1.49×10⁶ m/s

🔬 Applications

1. Photo-emissive Cells

Two electrodes in evacuated glass bulb. Curved cathode + anode rod. Light ejects electrons → current flows. Used in: lift door controls, film sound reproduction.

2. Photoconductive Cells (LDR)

Semiconductors (e.g. CdS) reduce resistance when light shines on them. Photo-diodes and photo-transistors block current when light increases. Used in: fire alarms, camera exposure meters.

3. Photo-voltaic Cells

Generate e.m.f using light. Copper disc oxidized on one surface, thin gold film on exposed surface. Current increases with intensity. Used in: solar panels, calculators.

🤔 Wave vs Quantum Model

The classical wave model of light cannot explain:

  • Why below threshold frequency, no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity
  • Why emission is instantaneous (no time delay)
  • Why KEmax depends on frequency, not intensity

Einstein's photon model explains all these:

  • One photon interacts with one electron
  • Photon energy = hf must exceed Φ
  • More photons (higher intensity) → more electrons emitted (but same KE)

✏️ More Practice

Example — Lithium

Threshold frequency for Li = 5.5×10¹⁴ Hz. Find work function.

Φ = hfco = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ × 5.5×10¹⁴ = 3.64×10⁻¹⁹ J = 2.28 eV

Minimum frequency for emission

Given Φ = 2.0 eV = 3.2×10⁻¹⁹ J. Find fmin.

fmin = Φ/h = 3.2×10⁻¹⁹/6.626×10⁻³⁴ = 4.8×10¹⁴ Hz

🧪 Practice Problems

P1. Find the maximum KE of photoelectrons emitted when light of f=8×10¹⁴ Hz hits metal with Φ=2.5 eV.

Ephoton=hf=6.63×10⁻³⁴×8×10¹⁴=5.3×10⁻¹⁹ J=3.31 eV
KEmax=3.31−2.5=0.81 eV=1.3×10⁻¹⁹ J

P2. Explain why increasing light intensity does not increase maximum KE of photoelectrons.

Each photon interacts with one electron. Intensity determines number of photons but not their individual energy. Only frequency (hence hf) determines KEmax.
