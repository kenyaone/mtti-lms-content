☢️ Production of X-rays

Discovered by Wilhelm Röntgen in 1895. Named "X-rays" meaning "unknown rays".

X-ray Tube

  • Cathode — tungsten filament (thermionic emission)
  • Anode (target) — solid copper with tungsten target
  • High PD maintained: 10 kV to 1,000 kV
  • Electrons accelerated → hit tungsten target → produce X-rays
  • Only 0.2% of energy becomes X-rays (rest = heat)
  • Cooling oil circulates through anode
  • Lead shield absorbs stray X-rays
SLIDE 3 OF 10

⚡ Energy Changes in X-ray Tube

Energy Equations

Electrical energy gained: E = eV
Kinetic energy: eV = ½mev²
Maximum frequency: hfmax = eV
Minimum wavelength: (hc)/λmin = eV

h = 6.63×10⁻³⁴ Js (Planck's constant)

Lower frequencies when electrons make multiple collisions.

SLIDE 4 OF 10

✏️ Worked Examples

Example 1 — Energy

X-ray frequency f = 4×10¹⁷ Hz. Find energy.

E = hf = 6.63×10⁻³⁴ × 4×10¹⁷ = 2.65×10⁻¹⁶ J

Example 2 — Speed

X-ray tube at 60 kV, current 4 mA. Find (a) electrons/sec, (b) speed at target.

(a) n = I/e = 4×10⁻³/1.6×10⁻¹⁹ = 2.5×10¹⁶ e/s

(b) ½mv² = eV → v = √(2eV/m) = 2.13×10⁸ m/s

Example 3 — Min Wavelength

18 kV tube. Find λmin.

λmin = hc/eV = (6.6×10⁻³⁴×3×10⁸)/(1.6×10⁻¹⁹×18000) = 6.9×10⁻¹¹ m

🔍 Properties of X-rays

  1. Travel in straight lines
  2. Undergo reflection and diffraction
  3. Not deflected by electric or magnetic fields (uncharged)
  4. Ionize gases → cause conduction
  5. Affect photographic films
  6. Highly penetrating — pass through paper, metal foils, soft tissue
  7. Cause fluorescence (e.g. barium platinocyanide)

SLIDE 7 OF 10

🏥 Uses & Dangers

Uses of X-rays

  • Medicine — radiographs (diagnosis), radiotherapy (cancer treatment)
  • Industry — detect cracks in metal castings and welds
  • Science — X-ray crystallography (atomic structure)
  • Security — detect weapons and explosives at airports

Dangers

  • Destroy or damage living cells when overexposed
  • Can cause genetic mutations
  • Precaution: X-ray tubes shielded by lead

🧪 Practice Problems

P1. An X-ray tube operates at 30 kV. Find (a) maximum frequency of X-rays, (b) minimum wavelength.

(a) fmax=eV/h=(1.6×10⁻¹⁹×30000)/(6.63×10⁻³⁴)=7.24×10¹⁸ Hz
(b) λmin=hc/eV=6.63×10⁻³⁴×3×10⁸/(1.6×10⁻¹⁹×30000)=4.14×10⁻¹¹ m

P2. Why is only 0.2% of energy converted to X-rays?

Most kinetic energy (99.8%) is converted to heat when electrons hit the target; only a small fraction results in X-ray emission.
