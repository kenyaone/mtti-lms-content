🔄 The Nitrogen Cycle

What is the Nitrogen Cycle?

The process by which nitrogen in the air is made available to plants and animals, and eventually returns to the air.

Nitrogen gas (N₂) makes up 78% of the atmosphere but cannot be directly used by most organisms.

Nitrogen fixation (atmospheric): Lightning/thunderstorms → N₂ + O₂ → nitrogen oxides → dissolve in rain → nitric acid → deposited in soil as nitrates
Nitrogen fixation (biological): Symbiotic bacteria (Rhizobium) in root nodules of legumes; Free-living bacteria (Clostridium, Azotobacter); Nostoc algae (Anabaena, Chlorella) — all fix N₂ to nitrates
Plant uptake: Plants absorb nitrates from soil → synthesise plant proteins → animals eat plants → animal proteins
Decomposition: Dead plants/animals + nitrogenous wastes → decomposed by putrefying bacteria and fungi (saprophytes) → release ammonia
Nitrification: Ammonia → Nitrites (by Nitrosomonas & Nitrococcus) → Nitrates (by Nitrobacter)
Denitrification: Nitrates in soil → free nitrogen gas (by denitrifying bacteria: Pseudomonas, Thiobacillus, some fungi) — reduces soil fertility

🏭 Pollution: Causes, Effects & Control

Definition

Pollution: Any process which leads to adverse or harmful changes in the environment.

💨 Air Pollution
💧 Water Pollution
🌱 Soil Pollution

Air Pollution — Causes & Effects

  • Sulphur IV oxide, H₂S, nitrogen oxides → acid rain, damages plant leaves
  • CFCs, aerosols, insecticides → deplete ozone layer; affect respiratory organs
  • Smoke/fumes from industries/vehicles → CO poisoning; settles on leaves, stops photosynthesis
  • CO₂ → greenhouse effect → temperature inversion
  • Noise from machines/aircraft → affects hearing
  • Dust from cement factories → limits photosynthesis
  • Radioactive emissions → cancer, mutations, death

Control of Air Pollution

  • Lead-free petrol in vehicles
  • Smokeless fuels and electricity
  • Filtration and chemical treatment of harmful gases
  • Factories far from residential areas; tall chimneys
  • Reduce noise intensity (ear muffs)

Water Pollution — Causes & Effects

  • Agrochemical fertilizers → eutrophication → algal bloom
  • Silting → shallow water; clogs gills and stomata
  • Industrial/domestic waste → toxic materials kill producers
  • Untreated sewage → reduces O₂; promotes bacterial growth; epidemics
  • Oil spillage → blocks O₂; kills organisms
  • Hot industrial water → reduces dissolved O₂

Control of Water Pollution

  • Treat domestic/industrial waste before discharge
  • Ban phosphate-based detergents; use biodegradable ones
  • Environmental impact assessment before industries
  • Biological control; organic farming
  • Contour farming; reafforestation to control silting

Soil Pollution — Causes & Effects

  • Acid rain → alters soil pH
  • Aerosols/pesticides → accumulate in soil; toxic to plants
  • Petroleum spillage → prevents root O₂ uptake
  • Heavy metals from inorganic fertilizers → soil microorganisms cannot survive
  • Household/industrial waste → renders soil unsuitable for agriculture

Control of Soil Pollution

  • Organic farming techniques
  • Biological control of pests
  • Recycle non-degradable materials or bury safely
  • Controlled burning of garbage
  • Treatment of human and industrial waste

🌊 Eutrophication & Biological Control

🌿 Eutrophication

Definition: Enrichment of water bodies with nutrients (nitrates/phosphates) due to discharge of sewage/agrochemicals, leading to rapid growth of surface plants (algae).

Effects of Eutrophication

  • Algae block sunlight from submerged plants → no photosynthesis → plants die
  • Dead organic matter decomposed by bacteria → consume dissolved O₂
  • Aquatic animals suffocate and die → collapse of food web

🐛 Biological Control

Definition: Using a living organism to regulate, control or reduce the population of another organism.

  • Beetles introduced to feed on water hyacinth
  • Fish introduced to feed on mosquito larvae
  • Ladybirds to control aphids on crops
  • Advantage: No chemical pollution; self-sustaining
  • Risk: Introduced organism may become invasive

⚠️ Conservation Efforts

  • Reforestation to restore habitats
  • Wildlife reserves and national parks
  • Laws against poaching and illegal logging
  • Sustainable agriculture practices
  • Environmental impact assessment (EIA)

🔄 Interactive: Nitrogen Cycle Steps

Click each step below to reveal the organisms and process involved. Complete the cycle by going through all 6 steps.

Step 1: Atmospheric Fixation
Lightning → N₂ + O₂ → Nitrogen oxides → Nitric acid (HNO₃) → dissolves in rainwater → deposited in soil as nitrates (NO₃⁻).
Step 2: Biological Fixation
Rhizobium (root nodules of legumes), Azotobacter & Clostridium (free-living), Anabaena (algae) → fix N₂ to NH₃/nitrates.
Step 3: Plant Uptake
Plants absorb nitrates → form plant proteins. Animals eat plants → convert to animal proteins.
Step 4: Decomposition
Putrefying bacteria & saprophytic fungi break down dead organic matter → release ammonia (NH₃).
Step 5: Nitrification
NH₃ → Nitrites (NO₂⁻) via Nitrosomonas & Nitrococcus. Nitrites → Nitrates (NO₃⁻) via Nitrobacter.
Step 6: Denitrification
Denitrifying bacteria (Pseudomonas, Thiobacillus) → convert nitrates back to N₂ → returned to atmosphere. Reduces soil fertility.

🎯 Apply Your Knowledge

📋 Scenario: A Farmer's Dilemma

A farmer in Eldoret uses excessive nitrogen fertilizers on his maize farm. Heavy rains wash the fertilizer into a nearby river. Within months, the river is covered with green algae, fish are dying, and the water smells terrible.

Q1: Using your knowledge of the nitrogen cycle and eutrophication, explain step-by-step what is happening in the river.
✅ Model: (1) Excess nitrates (fertilizer) wash into river by surface runoff → eutrophication; (2) Algae grow rapidly at the surface, blocking sunlight; (3) Submerged plants can't photosynthesise → die; (4) Dead plants decomposed by aerobic bacteria → O₂ consumed rapidly; (5) Dissolved O₂ levels drop → fish suffocate and die (BOD increases); (6) Anaerobic decomposition continues → produces H₂S and other foul-smelling gases.
Q2: Suggest TWO biological and TWO non-biological methods the farmer could use to prevent future pollution.
✅ Biological: (1) Use biological control — introduce organisms that limit algae growth; (2) Plant buffer strips of vegetation along the river bank to absorb runoff; (3) Practice organic farming using compost instead of chemical fertilizers.
Non-biological: (1) Reduce the quantity of fertilizer applied (precision farming); (2) Contour/terrace farming to reduce surface runoff; (3) Apply fertilizer only when soil is dry, not before rain; (4) Construct settling ponds to trap fertilizer-laden runoff.

🦟 Disease Control: Malaria Deep Dive

Malaria Life Cycle Overview

Malaria is caused by Plasmodium (a protozoan) transmitted by the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquito.

🦟
Mosquito bites human
Injects Plasmodium sporozoites
🫀
Liver stage
Plasmodium multiplies in liver cells
🩸
Blood stage
Infects red blood cells → fever, anaemia
🦟
Cycle continues
Mosquito takes blood meal → picks up gametocytes
Q: Explain why the female Anopheles mosquito is called a vector for malaria, not the causative agent.
✅ Model: The causative agent (pathogen) of malaria is Plasmodium — a protozoan that directly causes disease symptoms. The female Anopheles mosquito is a vector (biological vector) because it transmits the pathogen from one host to another without itself being the cause of the disease. The mosquito acts as an intermediate host where Plasmodium undergoes sexual reproduction (sporogony), but the disease malaria is caused by Plasmodium's destruction of red blood cells in the human host.

✏️ Practice Questions

1. State TWO ways in which nitrogen in the atmosphere is made available for plant use.
✅ (1) Biological fixation by microorganisms — Rhizobium (in root nodules of legumes), Azotobacter/Clostridium (free-living in soil), Anabaena (algae) fix atmospheric N₂ into nitrates.
(2) Atmospheric/electrical fixation — lightning and thunderstorms cause N₂ to combine with O₂ to form nitrogen oxides → dissolve in rainwater as nitric acid → deposited in soil as nitrates.
2. Distinguish between nitrifying bacteria and denitrifying bacteria. Give examples of each.
✅ Nitrifying bacteria: Convert ammonia to nitrites and then to nitrates, increasing soil fertility. Examples: Nitrosomonas (NH₃ → NO₂⁻), Nitrococcus, Nitrobacter (NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻).

Denitrifying bacteria: Convert nitrates back to free atmospheric nitrogen gas (N₂), thereby reducing soil fertility. Examples: Pseudomonas, Thiobacillus.
3. Define biological control and give ONE advantage and ONE disadvantage.
✅ Definition: Using a living organism to regulate, control, or reduce the population of another organism (pest).
Advantage: No chemical pollution; long-lasting; self-sustaining; does not harm non-target species (if well-managed).
Disadvantage: The introduced organism may become invasive and disrupt the ecosystem; may not be effective if the pest has no natural enemies in the new area; takes time to show results.
