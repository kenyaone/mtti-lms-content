📖 Key Definitions in Genetics
🧬 Genetics: The study of heredity (inheritance) and variation — how characteristics are passed from parents to offspring.
Heredity
Transmission of traits from parents to offspring; resemblance among related individuals
Trait / Character
A characteristic of an organism e.g. eye colour, height, blood group
Gene
Unit of inheritance; hereditary factor at a fixed point (locus) on a chromosome that transmits traits
Allele
Alternative forms of genes controlling a particular characteristic at a specific locus
Chromosome
Threadlike structures in cell nuclei; carry genes (DNA + histone proteins); two types: autosomes and sex chromosomes
DNA
Deoxyribonucleic acid — double helix molecule making up chromosomes; contains genes; 4 bases: A-T, G-C pairs
Genotype
The genetic constitution/makeup of an individual (e.g. TT, Tt, tt)
Phenotype
The observable characteristic of an individual (what you can see)
Dominant gene
Expressed in the phenotype when homozygous OR heterozygous
Recessive gene
Only expressed in the homozygous state (e.g. tt)
Homozygous
Two identical alleles at a locus (e.g. LL or ll)
Heterozygous
Two different alleles at a locus (e.g. Ll)
🔑 F1 = First filial generation (first offspring from a cross). F2 = Second generation (offspring from F1 × F1).
⚖️ Mendel's First Law & Monohybrid Inheritance
⚖️ Mendel's First Law (Law of Segregation): Only one character from a contrasting pair can be carried in a gamete. Genes are transmitted individually without alteration — hence only one character can be inherited per gamete.
🔬 Monohybrid Inheritance: The study of inheritance of ONE character at a time (e.g. wing size in Drosophila).

Has Rh antigen. Due to dominant gene (RR or Rr). Majority of people.

🔗 Sex Linkage & Sex-Linked Traits
🔗 Sex Linkage: Genes located on sex chromosomes, transmitted along with genes determining sex. Linkage: Genes occurring together on a chromosome and passed to offspring without separation.

Sex-Linked Traits in Humans

X-Linked traits

  • Colour blindness
  • Haemophilia

Y-Linked traits

  • Hairy ear pinna
  • Baldness
⚠️ Males (XY) have only ONE X chromosome — so a recessive X-linked gene will always be expressed in males (no second X to mask it).

Haemophilia — X-Linked Recessive

XH = normal clotting; Xh = haemophilia

Normal parents (XHY × XHXh) can produce haemophilic sons — the mother is a carrier. Daughters cannot be haemophilic unless father is haemophilic AND mother is carrier.
🔄 Variation & Mutation
🔄 Variation: Sequence of differences occurring among individuals of the same species.

Types of Variation

Continuous Variation

  • Differences NOT clear-cut
  • Show intermediates
  • Quantitative
  • e.g. height, weight, skin colour, intelligence

Discontinuous Variation

  • Differences ARE clear-cut
  • No intermediates
  • Qualitative
  • e.g. blood group, tongue rolling, fingerprints

Causes of Variation

  • Random assortment of genes during meiosis
  • Crossing over between homologous chromosomes
  • Fertilization (random combination of gametes)
  • Mutation (change in DNA)
  • Environmental conditions
💥 Mutation: A sudden change in the structure of DNA at a particular locus/chromosome/gene.

Characteristics of Mutations

  • Arise suddenly and unpredictably
  • Random in nature
  • Generally rare
  • May breed true
  • Some desirable; some lethal
🧬 Types of Mutations

A. Chromosomal Mutations

Change in the nature, structure or number of chromosomes.
TypeHow it occurs
DuplicationA section of chromosome is repeated — genes are duplicated
DeletionA portion of chromosome breaks off — alters number and sequence of genes
InversionChromatid breaks at 2 places; middle piece rejoins in inverted position
TranslocationA section of chromatid breaks off and attaches to another chromosome
Non-disjunctionFailure of homologous chromosomes to separate during meiosis
PolyploidyChromosome number doubles or triples — beneficial in plants (↑ yield, disease resistance)

B. Gene Mutations

Alteration in the structure of a gene (change in DNA base sequence).

SubstitutionA portion of gene is replaced by a new portion

  • Haemophilia
  • Colour blindness
  • Phenylketonuria (PKU)
✏️ Practice Questions

1. Distinguish between genotype and phenotype. Give an example of each.

Genotype: The genetic constitution of an organism (e.g. Ll — heterozygous for wing size).
Phenotype: The observable characteristic (e.g. Long wings — because L is dominant).

2. In cattle, the gene for red coat (R) is co-dominant with the gene for white coat (W). What phenotype would a heterozygous (RW) cow have? What would be the F2 ratio if two RW cattle were crossed?

RW cattle would be roan (mixture of red and white hairs) — both genes expressed.
F2: RR (red) : RW (roan) : WW (white) = 1 Red : 2 Roan : 1 White (phenotypic ratio).

3. A carrier woman (XCXc) marries a colour blind man (XcY). What proportion of their sons will be colour blind?

Punnett square:
Mother gametes: XC and Xc | Father gametes: Xc and Y
Offspring: XCXc (carrier ♀), XcXc (colour blind ♀), XCY (normal ♂), XcY (colour blind ♂)
50% of sons will be colour blind.

4. Explain why only mutations in gametes (sex cells) influence evolution.

Only gametes are passed on to form the next generation (offspring). Mutations in somatic/body cells affect only that individual and cannot be inherited. Gamete mutations are transmitted to offspring, changing the gene pool of the population.

5. State THREE practical applications of genetics.

1. Breeding programmes — producing high-yielding, disease-resistant crops and livestock
2. Genetic engineering — manipulation of genes to produce desired characteristics
3. Genetic counselling — reducing harmful traits (albinism, colour blindness) in populations
4. Law — paternity determination using blood groups
