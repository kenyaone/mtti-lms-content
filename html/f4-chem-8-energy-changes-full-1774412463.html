🌡️ Exothermic & Endothermic Reactions
🔥 Exothermic Reaction
Heat released to surroundings.
Temperature rises.
ΔH = negative (–ve)
H(products) < H(reactants)
❄️ Endothermic Reaction
Heat absorbed from surroundings.
Temperature falls.
ΔH = positive (+ve)
H(products) > H(reactants)
ΔH = Hproducts − Hreactants

💧 Energy Changes in Physical Processes
🌡️ Enthalpy (H): The heat content of a substance. ΔH = Hfinal − Hinitial. Standard conditions: 25°C (298K), 1 atm. Symbol: ΔHθ

Latent Heat — Change of State

Molar Enthalpy of Fusion (ΔHfus)

Energy needed to melt 1 mole of solid at its melting point.

Process: Solid → Liquid

Bonds broken: intermolecular forces (NOT covalent bonds)

This is an endothermic process (ΔH = +ve)

Molar Enthalpy of Vaporisation (ΔHv)

Energy needed to convert 1 mole of liquid to vapour at boiling point.

Process: Liquid → Gas

ΔHv > ΔHfus because stronger intermolecular forces must be overcome

This is an endothermic process (ΔH = +ve)

Heating Curve — Water

🔑 During melting and boiling, temperature stays constant — energy goes into breaking intermolecular forces, NOT raising temperature.
🧮 Calculating Enthalpy Changes: ΔH = mcΔT
ΔH = m × c × ΔT
m = mass of solution (g) | c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹ | ΔT = temperature change (K or °C)

Types of Molar Enthalpy Changes

TypeDefinitionSign
ΔHsol (solution)Heat change when 1 mole dissolves in excess water±
ΔHc (combustion)Heat when 1 mole burns completely in excess O₂–ve
ΔHn (neutralisation)Heat when 1 mole of H₂O forms in acid-base reaction–ve
ΔHdis (displacement)Heat when 1 mole of substance is displaced from solutionusually –ve

💡 Worked Example — Enthalpy of Combustion of Ethanol

Given: 400 cm³ water, T₁ = 28°C, T₂ = 40°C, mass ethanol burnt = 0.92 g, M(C₂H₅OH) = 46 g/mol
Step 1: ΔT = 40 − 28 = 12°C (= 12 K)
Step 2: ΔH = mcΔT = (400 × 4.18 × 12) / 1000 = 20.064 kJ (for 0.92 g)
Step 3: Moles burnt = 0.92/46 = 0.02 mol
Step 4: Molar ΔHc = 20.064 / 0.02 = –1003.2 kJ mol⁻¹

💡 Worked Example — Neutralisation (HCl + NaOH)

Given: 50 cm³ of 2M HCl + 50 cm³ 2M NaOH, ΔT = 13.25°C
Step 1: Total mass = 100 g. ΔH = 100 × 4.18 × 13.25 / 1000 = 5.54 kJ
Step 2: Moles of HCl = (2 × 50)/1000 = 0.1 mol
Step 3: ΔHn = 5.54/0.1 = –55.4 kJ mol⁻¹
📌 Strong acid + strong base always gives ≈ –57 kJ/mol. Weak acid or base gives less (some energy used to ionise).
⚗️ Bond Energies & Enthalpy Calculations
🔗 Bond Dissociation Energy: Energy needed to break 1 mole of a particular covalent bond in the gaseous state. Breaking bonds = endothermic (+ve). Forming bonds = exothermic (–ve).

Common Bond Energies (kJ mol⁻¹)

BondEnergyBondEnergy
H–H436C–H413
Cl–Cl242C–C346
Br–Br193C–Cl339
H–Cl431O=O489

ΔH = Σ (bonds broken) − Σ (bonds formed)

💡 Worked Example — Formation of HCl

H₂(g) + Cl₂(g) → 2HCl(g)
Bonds broken: 1× H–H = +436 kJ; 1× Cl–Cl = +242 kJ → Total = +678 kJ
Bonds formed: 2× H–Cl = –(2 × 431) = –862 kJ
ΔH = (+678) + (–862) = –184 kJ for 2 mol HCl
Per mole: ΔHf = –184/2 = –92 kJ mol⁻¹ (exothermic)
⚠️ Always multiply bond energies by the number of each bond present in the equation. Check the balanced equation carefully before calculating.
♻️ Hess's Law of Constant Heat Summation
♻️ Hess's Law: "The total enthalpy change of a reaction is the same, regardless of whether the reaction occurs in one step or several steps — provided the initial reactants and final products are the same."

Using Hess's Law — Energy Cycle

Key rule: Route 1 = Route 2
ΔH₁ = ΔH₂ + ΔH₃ (or rearrange to find the unknown)

💡 Example 1 — ΔH°f of CO from combustion data

Given: ΔH°c (C graphite) = –393.5 kJ/mol; ΔH°c (CO) = –283 kJ/mol
Route 1: C + O₂ → CO₂, ΔH₁ = –393.5 kJ
Route 2: C + ½O₂ → CO (ΔHf), then CO + ½O₂ → CO₂ (–283 kJ)
Hess's Law: ΔH₁ = ΔHf + ΔH₂
–393.5 = ΔHf + (–283)
ΔHf = –393.5 + 283 = –110.5 kJ mol⁻¹

💡 Example 2 — ΔH°f of methane (CH₄)

Given: ΔH°c: C(gr) = –393.5; H₂ = –285.7; CH₄ = –890.4 kJ/mol
Equation: C(s) + 2H₂(g) → CH₄(g)
ΔH₁ (Route 1) = –393.5 + 2(–285.7) = –964.9 kJ
Hess's Law: ΔHf + ΔH°c(CH₄) = ΔH₁
ΔHf = –964.9 – (–890.4) = –74.5 kJ mol⁻¹
🔑 Standard conditions: 25°C (298 K) and 1 atm. The superscript θ indicates standard conditions: ΔH°f, ΔH°c.
💎 Lattice Energy, Hydration & Enthalpy of Solution
When an ionic solid dissolves in water, TWO energy changes occur:
1. Lattice energy (ΔHlatt) — energy to break apart the crystal lattice (+ve, endothermic)
2. Hydration energy (ΔHhyd) — energy released when ions are surrounded by water molecules (–ve, exothermic)
ΔHsol = ΔHhyd + ΔHlatt

Exothermic dissolving

Hydration energy > Lattice energy

ΔHsol = –ve

Temperature rises

e.g. NaOH, anhydrous CuSO₄, conc. H₂SO₄

Endothermic dissolving

Hydration energy < Lattice energy

ΔHsol = +ve

Temperature falls

e.g. NH₄NO₃, KNO₃, hydrated CuSO₄·5H₂O

Example — NaCl dissolving

NaCl(s) + H₂O → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) ΔH_latt = +776 kJ/mol (energy to break lattice) ΔH_hyd = –771 kJ/mol (energy released on hydration) ΔH_sol = –771 + 776 = +5 kJ/mol (slightly endothermic)
💧 Water molecules orientate their δ– (O) end towards Na⁺ ions and their δ+ (H) end towards Cl⁻ ions — electrostatic attraction releases hydration energy.
⛽ Common Fuels, Energy Content & Pollution
⛽ Fuel: A substance that undergoes combustion readily and releases heat energy. Combustion requires a fuel, heat (activation energy), and oxygen — the fire triangle.

Environmental Impact of Fuels

Air Pollution

  • CO — poisonous, incomplete combustion
  • CO₂ — greenhouse gas → global warming
  • SO₂ — from sulphur in fossil fuels → acid rain
  • NO₂ — from car engines → acid rain
  • Unburned hydrocarbons — carcinogenic

Control Measures

  • Catalytic converters: CO → CO₂; NOₓ → N₂; CₓHᵧ → CO₂ + H₂O
  • Scrubbers — remove SO₂ from emissions
  • Electrostatic precipitators — remove soot/particulates
  • Unleaded petrol — removes lead pollution
⚖️ Factors when choosing a fuel: availability, cost, storage safety, pollution, ease of use, and heating value.
✏️ Practice Questions

1. In an experiment, 50 cm³ of 2M HCl was neutralised by 50 cm³ of 2M NaOH. The temperature rose by 6.8°C. Calculate the molar enthalpy of neutralisation. (density = 1 g/cm³, c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹)

m = 100 g; ΔH = 100 × 4.18 × 6.8 / 1000 = 2.842 kJ
Moles of HCl = (2 × 50)/1000 = 0.1 mol
ΔHn = 2.842 / 0.1 = –28.42 kJ mol⁻¹

2. Use bond energies to calculate ΔH for: H₂(g) + Br₂(g) → 2HBr(g). (H–H = 436; Br–Br = 193; H–Br = 365 kJ/mol)

Bonds broken: H–H (+436) + Br–Br (+193) = +629 kJ
Bonds formed: 2 × H–Br = 2 × (–365) = –730 kJ
ΔH = 629 – 730 = –101 kJ (exothermic); ΔHf(HBr) = –101/2 = –50.5 kJ mol⁻¹

3. Calculate ΔH°f of CO₂ given: ΔH°c (C) = –394 kJ/mol; ΔH°c (CO) = –283 kJ/mol; ΔH°f (CO) = –111 kJ/mol.

C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)
Route 1: Directly. ΔH₁ = ΔH°c(C) = –394 kJ
Route 2: C → CO (–111 kJ) → CO₂ (–283 kJ): Total = –394 kJ ✓ (confirms Hess's Law)
Therefore ΔH°f (CO₂) = –394 kJ mol⁻¹

4. Explain why dissolution of NaOH is exothermic while dissolution of KNO₃ is endothermic.

For NaOH: Hydration energy (energy released when Na⁺ and OH⁻ ions are surrounded by water molecules) is greater than the lattice energy — net energy released — exothermic.
For KNO₃: Lattice energy (energy needed to break crystal lattice) is greater than the hydration energy — net energy absorbed — endothermic.

5. State THREE reasons why the experimental value of enthalpy of combustion is usually lower than the theoretical value.

1. Heat lost to surroundings (by radiation, convection, conduction).
2. Incomplete combustion of the fuel.
3. Heat absorbed by the beaker/calorimeter itself.
4. Draughts/wind carrying heat away.
