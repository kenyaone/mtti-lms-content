Mechanisms Promoting Cross-Pollination

  • Dioecism: Male & female flowers on different plants
  • Protandry: Anthers mature before stigma (e.g. sweet corn)
  • Protogyny: Stigma matures before anthers (e.g. plantain)
  • Self-incompatibility: Pollen of a plant cannot germinate on stigma of same plant
  • Heterostyly: Style and filament differ in length between flowers

🧬 Fertilisation in Flowering Plants

Definition

Fertilisation: Fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote.

Double Fertilisation (unique to Angiosperms)

  1. Pollen grain lands on stigma → pollen tube grows down the style
  2. Pollen tube penetrates ovule through micropyle
  3. Two male nuclei released from pollen tube into embryo sac
  4. First fertilisation: One male nucleus + egg cell → zygote (2n) → develops into embryo
  5. Second fertilisation: Other male nucleus + two polar nuclei → primary endosperm nucleus (3n) → develops into endosperm (food store)

🍃 Seed & Fruit Dispersal

Why Dispersal?

  • Reduces competition between parent and offspring
  • Reduces competition between offspring for space, water, light
  • Colonises new areas
AgentAdaptations of Seed/FruitExamples
WindLight; wings/parachutes/feathery pappus; inflated structuresDandelion (pappus), Jacaranda (wings), Tridax, Cotton
WaterLight; waterproof coat; airspaces for buoyancy; spongy pericarpCoconut, Water lily, Mangrove
Animals (outside)Hooks, spines, burs — catch on fur/clothingAcacia, Bidens (black jack), Xanthium
Animals (eating)Fleshy/sweet fruits; brightly coloured; sweet smell — eaten by animals, seeds pass undigestedTomato, mango, passion fruit, fig
Explosive/SelfBuilds up tension; fruit walls dry unevenly → sudden splittingSquirting cucumber, Touch-me-not (Impatiens), Witch hazel
GravityHeavy fruits fall and rollCoconut (gravity + water), acorns

💡 Significance of Dispersal

  • Reduces intraspecific competition between seedlings
  • Ensures seeds land in suitable germination conditions
  • Allows colonisation of new areas → species spread
  • Reduces chance of destruction by localised pests/diseases

✏️ Practice Questions

1. Distinguish between pollination and fertilisation in flowering plants.
✅ Pollination: Transfer of pollen grains from the anther to a receptive stigma of a flower (same or different plant). It is a physical process requiring external agents (insects, wind, etc.).

Fertilisation: The actual fusion of the male gamete nucleus (from pollen) with the female gamete (egg cell in ovule) to form a diploid zygote. It is a biological/chemical process that occurs inside the ovule after pollination.

Key difference: Pollination precedes fertilisation; pollination is transfer of pollen, fertilisation is fusion of gametes.
2. Explain the significance of double fertilisation and state what each product develops into.
✅ Double fertilisation is unique to angiosperms (flowering plants). Two male nuclei from the pollen tube fuse with different structures in the embryo sac:

(1) First male nucleus + egg cell → zygote (2n) → develops into the embryo (the new plant)
(2) Second male nucleus + two polar nuclei → primary endosperm nucleus (3n) → develops into the endosperm (food store for the developing seed)

Significance: Ensures both embryo and food supply are formed simultaneously — the embryo does not begin growing until its food store is ready.
3. State FOUR features that distinguish a wind-pollinated flower from an insect-pollinated flower.
✅ Wind-pollinated vs Insect-pollinated:
(1) Petals: Wind = small, dull, absent; Insect = large, brightly coloured
(2) Nectar: Wind = absent; Insect = present
(3) Pollen: Wind = light, smooth, dry, produced in huge quantities; Insect = heavy, sticky/spiky surface, less pollen
(4) Stigma: Wind = feathery, branched, hangs outside flower; Insect = sticky, enclosed inside flower
(5) Anthers: Wind = long filaments dangling loosely outside; Insect = firmly attached inside
