⚛️ The Nucleus & Radioactivity

Radioactivity discovered by Henri Becquerel in 1869. Marie and Pierre Curie isolated Polonium (Z=84) and Radium (Z=88) in 1898.

Radioactive decay — spontaneous disintegration of unstable nuclides to form stable ones with emission of radiation.

⚗️ Nuclear Equations

Alpha Decay (A−4, Z−2)

ᴬ_ZX → ᴬ⁻⁴₍Z₋₂₎Y + ⁴₂He

Example: ²³⁵₉₂U → ²³¹₉₀Th + ⁴₂He

Beta Decay (A same, Z+1)

¹₀n → ¹₁p + ⁰₋₁e (neutron converts to proton)

Example: ²³¹₉₀Th → ²³¹₉₁Pa + ⁰₋₁e

Conservation Rules

  • Total mass number (A) is conserved
  • Total charge (Z) is conserved
  • LHS must equal RHS for both A and Z

⏱️ Half-Life

Half-life (T½) — time for half the radioactive sample to decay. Activity approaches but never reaches zero.

Activity ∝ number of undecayed nuclei (Law of radioactive decay)

Examples

Sample (T½=98 min) reduces to 1/16th of original after how long?

1/16 = (1/2)⁴ → 4 half-lives → 4×98 = 392 minutes

💥 Nuclear Fission & Fusion

Nuclear Fission

Heavy nucleus splits into lighter nuclei + energy + neutrons. U-235 releases 2-3 neutrons per fission → chain reaction. Applications: atomic bomb, nuclear reactors.

Nuclear Fusion

Light nuclei combine to form heavier nucleus + energy. Requires very high temperatures (thermonuclear reaction). Example: ²₁H + ²₁H → ³₂He + ¹₀n + 3.3 MeV. Applications: hydrogen bomb, sun's energy.

🧪 Practice Problems

P1. T½ = 576 hr, initial mass 1280g. Find mass after 2304 hr.

2304/576 = 4 half-lives → mass = 1280×(1/2)⁴ = 1280/16 = 80 g

P2. Initial atoms = 5.12×10²⁰, T½=3s. Atoms decayed after 6s?

6s = 2 half-lives | Remaining = 5.12×10²⁰/4 = 1.28×10²⁰
Decayed = 5.12×10²⁰ − 1.28×10²⁰ = 3.84×10²⁰

P3. Balance: ²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ?

A: 238 = 234 + 4, Z: 92 = 90 + 2 → ⁴₂He (alpha particle)
