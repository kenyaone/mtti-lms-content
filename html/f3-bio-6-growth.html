📈 Growth — Definition & Characteristics

What is Growth?

An irreversible increase in the dry mass of an organism due to an increase in the number of cells and the size of existing cells. Dry mass is used (not wet mass) because water content can fluctuate.

Characteristics of Growth

  • Irreversible increase in size
  • Involves cell division (mitosis) and cell enlargement
  • Measured by increase in dry mass
  • Requires energy (ATP) from respiration
  • Controlled by hormones

📊 The Sigmoid (S-shaped) Growth Curve

All organisms show a characteristic S-shaped growth curve with 3 phases:

PhaseWhat happens
Lag PhaseSlow growth; organism adapts to environment; prepares for rapid division
Log (Exponential) PhaseRapid growth; resources plentiful; cells divide at maximum rate
Stationary (Plateau) PhaseGrowth rate = death rate; resources limited; carrying capacity reached

🌿 Regions of Growth in Plants

What is a Meristem?

A region of actively dividing cells in plants (plants grow at specific points, unlike animals that grow throughout the body).

Meristem TypeLocationFunctionType of Growth

🧪 Plant Growth Hormones

🌱 Auxins
🌿 Gibberellins

Auxins (Indole Acetic Acid — IAA)

  • Produced at apical meristems (shoot tips)
  • Diffuse downward to elongation zone; stimulate cell elongation
  • Phototropism: Light causes auxin to concentrate on shaded side → shaded cells elongate → shoot bends toward light
  • Geotropism: Gravity causes auxin to accumulate on lower side of horizontal roots/shoots → roots: lower side inhibited, curves down (positive geotropism); shoots: lower side stimulated, curves up (negative geotropism)
  • Apical dominance: High auxin concentration at apex inhibits lateral bud growth — pruning removes apex → lateral buds grow
  • Rooting powder: Auxins used to stimulate root formation in cuttings
  • Herbicides: High concentrations toxic to broadleaf plants (selective weedkillers)
  • Parthenocarpy: Auxins stimulate fruit development without fertilisation

Gibberellins

  • Produced in young leaves, embryos, seeds
  • Stimulate elongation of internodes → tall plants
  • Break seed dormancy → promotes germination
  • Promote bolting (rapid shoot elongation before flowering)
  • Promote fruit development (larger seedless fruits in grapes)
  • Stimulate enzyme activity during germination (e.g. amylase in barley)
  • Genetic dwarfism in plants is often due to lack of gibberellins

🌾 Seed Germination

Conditions for Germination

  • Water: Activates enzymes; softens testa; hydrolysis of stored food; provides turgidity
  • Oxygen: For aerobic respiration to provide energy (ATP) for growth
  • Suitable temperature: For enzyme activity; optimum ~25°C for most tropical seeds
  • Viability: Seed must be alive (embryo viable)
  • (Some seeds also need: light e.g. lettuce; after-ripening period)

Process of Germination

  1. Seed absorbs water through micropyle → testa softens
  2. Embryo produces gibberellins → stimulates enzyme production
  3. Enzymes (amylase, protease, lipase) hydrolyse stored food → soluble sugars, amino acids
  4. Soluble food transported to embryo → energy for growth (aerobic respiration)
  5. Radicle (root) emerges first, anchors seedling
  6. Plumule (shoot) emerges, grows upward
FeatureEpigeal GerminationHypogeal Germination
CotyledonsRise above soil (become photosynthetic)Remain below soil
HypocotylElongates, pulls cotyledons upDoes not elongate
EpicotylShort (does not elongate much)Elongates, pushes plumule up
Food reserveCotyledons become green; make food themselvesCotyledons remain as food store underground
ExamplesBean, sunflower, castor oilMaize, pea, onion, wheat

🦋 Metamorphosis in Insects

What is Metamorphosis?

Series of changes that some organisms go through from hatching to adult form.

🦋 Complete Metamorphosis
🦗 Incomplete Metamorphosis

Complete Metamorphosis (Holometabolous)

4 stages: Egg → Larva → Pupa → Adult (Imago)

  • Egg: Fertilised egg laid in suitable habitat
  • Larva (caterpillar/maggot): Actively feeds; moults periodically (ecdysis); looks completely different from adult
  • Pupa (chrysalis/cocoon): Non-feeding stage; inside, tissues reorganise to form adult (histolysis and histogenesis)
  • Adult (Imago): Reproductive stage; does not grow; may feed or not

Examples: Butterflies, moths, houseflies, mosquitoes, beetles

Incomplete Metamorphosis (Hemimetabolous)

3 stages: Egg → Nymph → Adult (Imago)

  • Egg: Fertilised egg
  • Nymph: Resembles adult but no wings; moults several times (instars) each time becoming more like the adult
  • Adult: Wings fully developed; reproductive

Examples: Grasshoppers, cockroaches, locusts, dragonflies, termites

Economic Importance of Metamorphosis

StageEconomic Importance
Larva (caterpillar)Damages crops and stored food; silk worm larva produces silk
PupaSilkworm pupa used for silk production
AdultPollinators (bees, butterflies); pests (locusts); food (e.g. winged termites)
OverallLarva and adult occupy different niches → reduce competition within species

🎯 Apply Your Knowledge

📋 Scenario: The Farmer's Experiment

A farmer sprays a plant with high concentrations of a synthetic auxin (2,4-D) on one side only. She notices the treated side stops growing, while the untreated side keeps growing — bending the plant toward the treated side. She also notices the lateral buds on the treated branch stop growing.

Q1: Explain why high concentrations of auxin inhibit growth on the treated side, while low concentrations normally promote growth.
✅ Model: Auxins (IAA) have a concentration-dependent effect on cell elongation. At low concentrations, auxin stimulates cells in the elongation zone to take in more water by osmosis, expanding the cell wall → cell elongation → growth. At high concentrations, auxin may inhibit cell elongation (the same receptor pathway is overstimulated → disrupts normal cell functioning). This is why 2,4-D at high concentrations is used as a herbicide — it causes abnormal, unregulated growth that eventually kills broadleaf plants.
Q2: Using the concept of apical dominance, explain why the lateral buds on the auxin-treated side stopped growing, and suggest what would happen if the shoot tip were removed.
✅ Apical dominance: Auxins produced at the shoot apex diffuse downward and inhibit the growth of lateral (axillary) buds. High auxin concentration near the apex keeps lateral buds dormant — this ensures the plant grows upward to reach light rather than sideways. If the shoot tip is removed (pruning), the source of auxin is removed, auxin concentration near lateral buds drops, and lateral buds resume growth → the plant becomes bushier. This is why gardeners prune plants to encourage bushy, lateral growth.

✏️ Practice Questions

1. Distinguish between epigeal and hypogeal germination using maize and bean as examples.
✅ Bean (epigeal): The hypocotyl elongates, pulling the cotyledons above the soil. Cotyledons emerge into light, turn green, photosynthesize, and eventually wither once the seedling becomes self-sufficient. The epicotyl is short.

Maize (hypogeal): The epicotyl elongates, pushing the plumule (shoot tip) up through the soil. Cotyledons (scutellum) remain below ground as a food store and gradually shrink as nutrients are used up. The hypocotyl does not elongate significantly.
2. Explain how a grasshopper undergoes incomplete metamorphosis. State THREE differences between complete and incomplete metamorphosis.
✅ Grasshopper (incomplete metamorphosis): Egg → Nymph (6 instars, moults each time) → Adult. Nymph resembles adult but has no wings; wings develop progressively with each moult.

Differences:
(1) Complete = 4 stages (egg, larva, pupa, adult); Incomplete = 3 stages (egg, nymph, adult)
(2) Complete has a pupa stage; incomplete does not
(3) Larva of complete metamorphosis looks completely different from adult; nymph of incomplete metamorphosis resembles the adult
(4) Complete: larva and adult exploit different food sources; Incomplete: nymph and adult may share the same food source
3. Describe the THREE phases of a sigmoid growth curve and explain what determines the transition from the log phase to the stationary phase.
✅ Lag phase: Slow initial growth; organism adapts to environment; few cells.
Log (exponential) phase: Rapid doubling of cell numbers; abundant nutrients; favourable conditions.
Stationary phase: Growth rate equals death rate; population size levels off.

Transition (log → stationary): As population grows, resources (food, water, space, light) become limiting. Intraspecific competition increases; metabolic waste accumulates; disease/predation increases. Eventually, the rate of new growth equals the death rate — the carrying capacity is reached.
