📖 Acids, Bases & Salts — Definitions
💧 Acid: A substance that dissociates in water to produce H⁺ ions as the only positively charged ions.

Strong Acids — Complete Dissociation

HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) H₂SO₄(aq) → 2H⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) HNO₃(aq) → H⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)

Weak Acids — Partial Dissociation

CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + CH₃COO⁻(aq) H₂CO₃(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + HCO₃⁻(aq)
⇌ shows reversible (partial) dissociation — a weak acid contains more molecules than ions.
⚗️ Base: A substance that dissociates in water to produce OH⁻ ions as the only negatively charged ions.

Strong Bases

Completely dissociate:

NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻ KOH → K⁺ + OH⁻

Weak Bases

Partially dissociate:

NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻
🧂 Salt: An ionic compound formed when a cation (from a base) combines with an anion (from an acid) during neutralisation.
HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) HNO₃(aq) + KOH(aq) → KNO₃(aq) + H₂O(l)
⚡ Strength, pH & Electrical Conductivity
Important: "Strong" refers to degree of dissociation — NOT concentration. A strong acid can be dilute; a weak acid can be concentrated.

Electrical Conductivity

Strong Electrolytes

  • Fully dissociate → many ions
  • High conductivity
  • Bulb glows brightly
  • HCl, NaOH, KOH, H₂SO₄

Weak Electrolytes

  • Partially dissociate → few ions
  • Low conductivity
  • Bulb glows faintly
  • CH₃COOH, NH₃(aq)
🔬 Experiment 1.2: When electrodes are dipped in solutions, strong electrolytes conduct well (bright bulb, visible electrode changes) while weak electrolytes conduct poorly.
🔄 Amphoteric Oxides & Hydroxides
🔄 Amphoteric: Oxides/hydroxides that react with BOTH acids AND bases — they show both acidic and basic properties.

Amphoteric Oxides

  • Zinc oxide (ZnO)
  • Lead(II) oxide (PbO)
  • Aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃)

Amphoteric Hydroxides

  • Zinc hydroxide Zn(OH)₂
  • Lead(II) hydroxide Pb(OH)₂
  • Aluminium hydroxide Al(OH)₃

Zinc — Reactions with Acid & Alkali

Basic behaviour (reacts with acid → simple salt):
ZnO(s) + 2HNO₃(aq) → Zn(NO₃)₂(aq) + H₂O(l) Zn(OH)₂(s) + 2HNO₃(aq) → Zn(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2H₂O(l)
Acidic behaviour (reacts with NaOH → complex salt):
ZnO(s) + 2NaOH(aq) + H₂O(l) → Na₂Zn(OH)₄(aq) [sodium zincate] Zn(OH)₂(s) + 2NaOH(aq) → Na₂Zn(OH)₄(aq)

Summary Table

🧪 Salt Preparation & Cation Tests

Methods of Salt Preparation

1. Neutralisation

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

2. Precipitation

Two soluble salts react → insoluble precipitate

Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂↓ + 2KNO₃

3. Titration

Used for soluble salts from weak acid + strong base or vice versa

4. Direct combination

Metal + non-metal directly

Fe(s) + S(s) → FeS(s)

Cation Tests — NaOH & NH₃ Solution

Cu²⁺Blue ppt, insolubleBlue ppt → deep blue (dissolves)

🔑 Key distinction: Al³⁺ dissolves in excess NaOH but NOT in excess NH₃. Cu²⁺ turns deep blue (tetraamminecopper complex) in excess NH₃.
📈 Solubility & Solubility Curves
📏 Solubility: The maximum mass (in grams) of a solute that dissolves in 100 g of water at a given temperature to form a saturated solution.

Effect of Temperature

Most solid salts

Solubility increases with temperature

e.g. KNO₃, NaNO₃, KCl

Gases

Solubility decreases with temperature

e.g. CO₂, NH₃ in water

Using Solubility Curves

  • Read the solubility of a substance at any temperature from the graph
  • Find the temperature at which two substances have the same solubility (crossover point)
  • Calculate the mass of crystals formed when a solution is cooled
Fractional Crystallisation: A mixture of salts can be separated by dissolving in hot water then cooling — the less soluble salt crystallises first.

Water Hardness

Temporary Hardness

  • Caused by Ca(HCO₃)₂ / Mg(HCO₃)₂
  • Removed by boiling
  • Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O + CO₂

Permanent Hardness

  • Caused by CaSO₄, MgSO₄, CaCl₂
  • NOT removed by boiling
  • Removed by washing soda, ion exchange
⚠️ Hard water wastes soap (forms scum), damages boilers (scale), and blocks pipes. Soft water lathers easily but may dissolve lead pipes.
🧫 Solvent Effects & Anion Tests

Effect of Solvent on Acid-Base Character

Water as solvent: Acids and bases only show their characteristic properties when dissolved in water (aqueous solution). Dry HCl gas does NOT affect dry litmus paper.
HCl(g) — dry → no acidic effect HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) — acidic

Non-aqueous Solvents

Some acids like HCl dissolved in organic solvents (e.g. benzene) do NOT conduct electricity or turn litmus red — they remain as molecules without dissociating.

Testing for Anions

Complex Ions

When excess NaOH or NH₃ is added to certain metal hydroxide precipitates, the precipitate dissolves by forming complex ions. E.g.:

Zn(OH)₂(s) + 2OH⁻(aq) → [Zn(OH)₄]²⁻(aq) [tetrahydroxozincate] Cu(OH)₂(s) + 4NH₃(aq) → [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻ [deep blue]
💧 Removing Water Hardness & Summary

Methods of Removing Water Hardness

MethodRemoves TemporaryRemoves Permanent
Boiling✅ Yes❌ No
Adding washing soda (Na₂CO₃)✅ Yes✅ Yes
Ion exchange resin✅ Yes✅ Yes
Distillation✅ Yes✅ Yes

Washing Soda Mechanism

Na₂CO₃(aq) + CaCl₂(aq) → CaCO₃↓ + 2NaCl(aq) Na₂CO₃(aq) + CaSO₄(aq) → CaCO₃↓ + Na₂SO₄(aq)

Scum Formation with Soap

2C₁₇H₃₅COONa + CaCl₂ → (C₁₇H₃₅COO)₂Ca↓ + 2NaCl (soap) (scum — insoluble)
Key Revision Points:
  • Strong acids/bases fully dissociate — low/high pH, good conductors
  • Amphoteric: ZnO, PbO, Al₂O₃ react with BOTH acids and alkalis
  • Cation tests: use NaOH and NH₃ — observe colour, solubility of precipitate
  • Solubility = g/100g water. Use curves for crystallisation calculations
  • Temporary hardness → boiling; Permanent → washing soda / ion exchange
✏️ Practice Questions

1. Write the dissociation equation for sulphuric(VI) acid and state whether it is strong or weak.

H₂SO₄(aq) → 2H⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq). Strong acid — fully dissociates (single arrow, not reversible).

2. Why does dry HCl gas not affect dry blue litmus paper?

Acids only show acidic properties in the presence of water. Dry HCl does not dissociate to produce H⁺ ions without water — so it cannot change litmus.

3. What is observed when excess NaOH is added to a solution containing Cu²⁺ ions? Write an equation.

A blue precipitate forms: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Cu(OH)₂(s). The precipitate does NOT dissolve in excess NaOH (unlike Zn²⁺, Al³⁺, Pb²⁺).

4. State TWO differences between temporary and permanent water hardness.

1. Cause: Temporary — Ca(HCO₃)₂/Mg(HCO₃)₂; Permanent — CaSO₄/MgSO₄/CaCl₂.
2. Removal: Temporary — removed by boiling; Permanent — NOT removed by boiling (needs washing soda or ion exchange).

5. Calculate the mass of KNO₃ crystals deposited when a saturated solution containing 120 g KNO₃ per 100 g water at 80°C is cooled to 20°C (solubility at 20°C = 32 g/100g water).

Mass deposited = 120 − 32 = 88 g of KNO₃ crystals.
